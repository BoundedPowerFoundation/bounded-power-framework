## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** WORKER-COOPERATIVE-EXAMPLE — a single-facility worker-owned cooperative business.

**1.2 Functional description:** A worker-owned and worker-governed business operating one production facility with 38 worker-owners and approximately 12 candidate workers in the membership pathway. Functional authority:
- Hires and admits new worker-owners through a defined candidacy process (typically 6–12 months of employment before membership vote)
- Governs through one-member-one-vote at the General Assembly; elected Board (5 members, 2-year staggered terms) handles operational decisions between assemblies
- Sets wages, hours, working conditions, and surplus distribution by member vote
- Disciplines members through a peer-review committee with appeal to the General Assembly; ultimate sanction is expulsion (loss of membership share, return of capital contribution, end of employment)
- Operates an internal grievance process for both members and non-member workers
- Holds and disposes of cooperative assets (equipment, lease, working capital, accumulated patronage)
- Sells goods and services to approximately 1,800 customers annually

The cooperative's authority over its members is consequential because membership combines employment, governance participation, and capital ownership in a single relationship. Loss of membership means simultaneous loss of job, voice, and accumulated capital share. This is not unusual for cooperatives; it is the structural feature that makes them cooperatives.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting employment, governance rights, and capital ownership of members.

**1.4 Domain:** Economic (primary).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates as a [STATE] cooperative corporation under the [STATE] Cooperative Corporation Act (or analogous statute; some states use general nonprofit or for-profit incorporation with cooperative bylaws). Authority derives from the cooperative's bylaws, adopted by member vote and amendable by member vote, and from the membership agreement signed by each worker-owner at admission. Subject to general employment law (wage-and-hour, anti-discrimination, OSHA), tax law, and contract law. The cooperative is a member of [REGIONAL COOPERATIVE FEDERATION], a voluntary association.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Zero. All worker-owners affirmatively chose membership through a candidacy process they could decline at any point. Candidate workers chose employment and may decline membership at the candidacy decision. Customers chose to transact.

The cooperative notes the framework's voluntary-but-stuck warning (form §2.3 note). The local labor market for the cooperative's trade is functional; departing members have not historically reported coercive exit conditions. The cooperative does not bind members through non-competes, non-solicits, or capital lock-up beyond the standard 90-day redemption window.

The cooperative acknowledges that this is the kind of voluntary-in-fact claim the framework treats with skepticism, and accepts that any member who experienced exit as coerced may file a Challenge under SCBP-08 contesting this declaration.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — operations at one facility in [CITY, STATE].

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Hiring and admission decisions (membership vote)
- Wage, hours, and working condition decisions (member vote at General Assembly)
- Discipline ranging from informal warning, written warning, suspension with pay, suspension without pay, to expulsion (peer review committee, with appeal to General Assembly)
- Termination of employment (only available against members through the expulsion process; against non-member candidates, standard at-will employment within [STATE] limits)
- Distribution of patronage dividends or surplus losses
- Sale, lease, or disposition of cooperative assets (member supermajority vote)

The maximum sanction available against any individual is expulsion: loss of membership, loss of employment, return of capital contribution per redemption schedule. The cooperative cannot levy fines, file liens, restrict members' employment elsewhere, restrict members' speech outside the workplace, or impose obligations beyond the membership agreement.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: ~$2.4M annually (member-set; subject to General Assembly approval)
- Staff: 38 worker-owners + 12 candidate workers + 1 part-time bookkeeper (contracted)
- Capital: ~$680K in member equity + ~$240K in collective indivisible reserve
- Physical: leased production facility, approximately $310K in equipment

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not extend authority to members' or candidates' lives outside the workplace and the cooperative's lawful business activities.
- **Action:** May not enforce non-compete, non-solicit, or non-disparagement clauses beyond the limited disclosure protections in the membership agreement; may not impose monetary fines on members beyond agreed dues and capital obligations; may not restrict members' protected concerted activity under [STATE] labor law; may not retaliate against members or candidates for raising safety, wage, or discrimination complaints.
- **Data:** May not collect or retain member personal information beyond what employment, payroll, tax, and membership records require; may not share member information outside the cooperative without consent except as legally required; may not surveil members outside the workplace.
- **Procedural:** May not impose discipline without notice and opportunity to be heard per bylaws; may not conduct a disciplinary proceeding in which a member of the peer review committee has a personal conflict; may not expel a member without supermajority assembly vote.
- **Other:** May not adopt rules that discriminate on protected characteristics; may not adopt bylaws amendments that bind a member to obligations not in effect at the time of their membership without their affirmative consent.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Limited at this scale. Cooperative wages and conditions modestly influence the local labor market for the trade; cooperative practices are documented in [REGIONAL COOPERATIVE FEDERATION] case studies and may influence other cooperatives' practices.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The cooperative commits to not using:
- *Proxy for race, national origin, language, or other protected characteristic in admission:* Admission decisions are by recorded member vote with reasons documented; aggregate admission outcomes are reviewed annually for disparate impact. R66/R74 commitment.
- *Proxy for personal animus in discipline:* Peer review committee members must recuse from any matter involving a member with whom they have a personal conflict (financial, romantic, family, prior dispute). The committee operates by recorded vote with reasons.
- *Proxy for political dissent in discipline:* The cooperative does not discipline members for protected concerted activity, for advocacy of bylaw changes the member disagrees with, or for opposition to incumbent Board members. Discipline triggered within 90 days of a member's public dissent is reviewed by a designated outside cooperative-federation observer.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Workplace injury rate exceeding peer benchmarks for the trade (per [STATE] OSHA equivalent or industry data) for two consecutive quarters triggers review. Any single serious injury (lost-time injury exceeding 30 days) triggers an internal incident review with public summary in the next member assembly minutes.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any member is unable to participate in General Assembly due to lack of accommodation (accessibility, language, scheduling) and the accommodation was requested in advance
- Any disciplinary proceeding occurs without bylaws-required notice, hearing, or appeal access
- Voting is conducted in a manner that fails to accommodate members in good standing
- Bylaws amendments are adopted in a manner that fails to provide notice and opportunity for member input

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Per the cooperative's environmental compliance commitments. Any [STATE] environmental violation, any complaint from a neighboring property owner about cooperative operations, or any failure of the annual self-audit triggers review.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** The cooperative does not undertake commitments binding members beyond their tenure except for the indivisible reserve (~$240K) which by [STATE] cooperative law remains with the cooperative on dissolution. Any proposed long-term obligation exceeding 5 years requires explicit member assembly approval; failure to seek that approval triggers review.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any member is disciplined for speech about cooperative practices, governance, working conditions, or labor rights
- The cooperative restricts members' use of personal communication channels (email, phone, in-person conversation) about cooperative matters
- Information needed for an informed General Assembly vote (financial reports, proposal text, comparative data) is withheld from members

The cooperative explicitly commits that members may speak publicly about their experience, including critically, without retaliation. Confidentiality protections exist for individual personnel matters and trade-sensitive financial information; they do not extend to suppressing dissent.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Member personal information shared outside the cooperative without consent or legal requirement — finding
- Member workplace activity surveilled beyond what production and safety require — finding
- Member records retained beyond the cooperative's stated retention schedule (employment records, 7 years; membership records, 10 years post-departure; financial records per tax law) — finding
- Disciplinary records made available to non-members or to members not on the relevant committee — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Disparate discipline threshold:* If discipline outcomes correlate with race, gender, age, national origin, language, or family status at p<0.05 over rolling 2-year periods, this triggers review.

*Capture-by-tenure threshold:* If the same individuals occupy Board seats for more than 6 of any 10 consecutive years, or if any single member directly or indirectly controls more than 28% of operational decision-making over a 12-month period, this triggers review.

*Customer harm threshold:* If customer complaints exceed peer benchmarks for the trade or any single customer experiences material undisclosed harm from cooperative goods or services, this triggers review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — General Assembly action by members**
- *Activator:* Any member may call for special assembly with petition of 25% of membership; any member may propose a motion at regular assembly.
- *Trigger:* Member determination of bylaws violation, governance failure, or operational concern.
- *What "stopped" means:* General Assembly may suspend specific operations, recall Board members, void disciplinary actions, amend bylaws, or dissolve the cooperative.
- *Independence:* Members are independent of Board (Board exists at members' direction). The Board cannot prevent a properly-noticed assembly.
- *Timeline:* Per bylaws — typically 14 days from petition to assembly.

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil litigation under [STATE] cooperative and employment law**
- *Activator:* Any current or former member, candidate worker, or customer with standing.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of violation of bylaws, membership agreement, employment law, contract, or [STATE] cooperative law.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders may halt practices, void actions, impose damages, or in extreme cases dissolve the cooperative.
- *Independence:* Courts independent.
- *Timeline:* Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — [STATE] regulatory action and administrative complaint**
- *Activator:* [STATE] labor regulator (wage-and-hour, anti-discrimination); [STATE] cooperative or corporate regulator; [STATE] OSHA equivalent; federal EEOC, NLRB, OSHA where applicable; [LOCAL] consumer protection.
- *Trigger:* Regulatory determination of violation.
- *What "stopped" means:* Specific operations may be enjoined; penalties imposed; remedial orders issued.
- *Independence:* State and federal agencies independent of the cooperative.
- *Timeline:* Per agency procedures.

The cooperative acknowledges that all three stop paths function in practice. Member assembly action has historically been used (one Board recall in cooperative history, several bylaws amendments). Civil litigation has not been used; the cooperative does not interpret this as evidence of independence and acknowledges that absence of use does not prove path effectiveness. Regulatory paths are external and operative.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. Members invoking grievance procedures, filing regulatory complaints, or filing civil suits may not be retaliated against, disciplined, or expelled for the activation. This is committed in the bylaws and protected under [STATE] and federal anti-retaliation law.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** [x] Yes for all three paths. Member assembly cannot be prevented by Board; courts and regulators are external.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged.

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies:**
- Production facility lease — if landlord declines renewal, the cooperative cannot operate at current location; new location can be sought but transition is operational disruption, not a compromise of stop paths
- Trade-specific equipment service contract — if vendor terminated, equipment maintenance becomes harder; other vendors exist; not a stop-path compromise

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

- *Tenure capture:* Long-tenured members may exercise influence beyond their formal vote share through institutional knowledge, relationships, and informal authority. Mitigation: 2-term Board limit; candidacy process pairs candidates with mentors who rotate; bylaws explicitly require Board to provide all members with the same information access.
- *Founder capture:* Three of the cooperative's seven founders remain active. Mitigation: founder-status confers no formal advantage; founders are subject to the same bylaws and discipline as any other member.
- *Outside-funder capture:* The cooperative's largest single customer accounts for 28% of revenue in the most recent fiscal year; threshold for review set at 33% per form §6.4.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:** Lease and equipment commitments. Member capital must be redeemable on dissolution per [STATE] cooperative law.

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:** Membership in [REGIONAL COOPERATIVE FEDERATION] is voluntary and confers no operational obligation. The cooperative is not part of any consortium or joint venture that would constitute R55/R59 functional continuity with another entity.

**6.7 Disclosure completeness assertion:** [x] Asserted.

Customers' decisions about whether to purchase remain customers'; suppliers' terms are negotiated and accepted by the cooperative as such; the cooperative does not adopt any third-party scoring, screening, or classification system as a basis for its own actions.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring:** Monthly Board meetings (open to all members); quarterly all-member operational review; annual General Assembly with full financial and governance review; annual self-audit using [REGIONAL COOPERATIVE FEDERATION] template; biennial peer review by sister cooperative.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Annual conflict-of-interest disclosures by Board; tenure tracking; annual review of customer concentration.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:** All financial records, meeting minutes, bylaws, and aggregate disciplinary statistics are available to any member on request. Aggregate financial statements, member count, and bylaws are available to the public on request. Individual personnel records and individual disciplinary records are restricted to the subject and the relevant committee.

**8.4 Adversarial exposure:** Strong at member level. ## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 12 months. Justification: orderly wind-down requires lease transition, equipment disposition, member capital redemption, customer relationship close-out, and final tax filing. Dissolution requires supermajority member vote per [STATE] cooperative law.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Months 1–3: Member vote to dissolve; notice to customers, suppliers, regulators
- Months 4–8: Wind-down of production; fulfillment of outstanding customer commitments; equipment sale or transfer
- Months 9–11: Member capital redemption per bylaws; final accounting; payment of outstanding obligations
- Month 12: Final filings; entity dissolution; indivisible reserve disposed per [STATE] cooperative law (typically transferred to another cooperative or to a cooperative development fund)

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:**
- Members' livelihoods depend on the cooperative; dissolution affects 50 households
- Lease and equipment commitments
- Customer relationships
- Indivisible reserve disposition

These are real but small-scale. The cooperative is not so embedded in any external system that its dissolution would create cascading effects beyond its members and immediate counterparties.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Member personnel records retained per [STATE] employment law (typically 7 years post-departure); customer records purged within 90 days of dissolution except as tax law requires
- *Institutional records:* Bylaws, meeting minutes, financial records archived per [STATE] cooperative dissolution law
- *Derived outputs:* None of structural significance

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — past disciplinary decisions, expulsions, and admission denials cannot be reversed. The cooperative acknowledges this and commits to: (a) preserving disciplinary and admission records to enable historical review by affected former members; (b) providing reference letters in good faith to former members and candidates regardless of cause of departure.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Yes. The cooperative's products and services are available from other producers; customers would not lose access to the goods. Members would lose this specific cooperative employment but the labor market for the trade exists. R51 met at this scale.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal dismantling rehearsal. The cooperative has discussed dissolution scenarios at General Assembly but has not conducted a desktop exercise. R43 gap acknowledged. At this scale, the gap is acknowledged as acceptable but not erased; formal rehearsal is committed within 24 months of registration.
