## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** VOLUNTARY-ADVOCACY-EXAMPLE — a national consumer product testing and advocacy nonprofit.

**1.2 Functional description:** This authority is a nonprofit consumer advocacy organization. It tests, evaluates, and publicly rates consumer products across approximately 38 product categories (appliances, electronics, vehicles, household goods, food products, personal care, etc.). Functional outputs: (a) public ratings of approximately 1,200 product models annually, published in a free online database and a paid annual print guide; (b) a "Recommended" designation for products meeting the authority's standards; (c) a "Failed Safety Review" designation for products judged hazardous; (d) investigative reports on product categories or industry practices. The authority does not directly compel any outcome. Its outputs influence consumer purchasing decisions, retailer stocking decisions, and occasionally regulatory attention.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Indirect. The authority issues no binding directives. Authority operates through R58 — outputs (ratings, recommendations, failure designations, investigative reports) materially influence consumer purchasing decisions at scale and have documented effects on retailer behavior. "Failed Safety Review" designations have historically caused product withdrawals from retail.

The authority acknowledges that R58 classifies it as exercising authority despite being a voluntary nonprofit. The framework's functional-effect test (R04) is the operative standard: outputs materially shape decisions affecting hundreds of millions of people.

**1.4 Domain:** Voluntary Association (primary). Data/Information (secondary).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates as a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt nonprofit corporation under federal tax law and [STATE] corporate law. No statutory mandate. Authority to publish ratings is grounded in First Amendment protected speech. Operations are subject to general tax law, employment law, contract law, and tort law (notably defamation, where the authority has established a multi-decade track record of withstanding industry challenges).

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] General public (consumers reading ratings — voluntary, but affected by ratings even if not directly reading them)
- [x] Regulated industry participants (the approximately 12,000 manufacturers whose products are rated — these are the *non-consenting* affected parties)
- [ ] Children — partial. Children are affected as end-users of rated products (toys, food, personal care for children). Child-product ratings affect their access to these goods. The authority does not act *upon* children directly but acknowledges children as a downstream affected category.
- [ ] Vulnerable populations — partial. Elderly consumers, low-income consumers, and consumers with limited English proficiency may be differentially affected by ratings (e.g., language-of-publication limits accessibility). Discussed in §4.7.

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:**
- Approximately 195 million US consumers are exposed to ratings through some channel
- Approximately 12,000 manufacturers are non-consenting affected parties — they cannot opt out of having their products rated
- Approximately 8 million subscribers actively use ratings in purchasing decisions

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Approximately 12,000 manufacturers. These are the entities the authority exercises functional authority over without their consent. Consumers reading ratings are voluntary users of the authority's services; they are affected but not non-consenting in the framework's sense.

This is a structurally interesting case: the consumers who read outputs are voluntary; the manufacturers whose products are rated are non-consenting subjects. The non-consenting population is therefore a small group of corporate entities, not individuals.

Mitigation: testing methodology is published and subject to scientific review.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:**
- [x] Single nation: United States — products sold in US market.

The authority tests products available in US retail. It does not test products available only outside the US.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:** No direct coercive authority over manufacturers, retailers, or consumers. Functional power operates through:
- *Publication:* publishing ratings, recommendations, and failure designations
- *Aggregation:* compiling product comparisons that influence purchasing decisions
- *Investigation:* investigative reporting on industry practices
- *Standard-setting:* publishing methodology that becomes a de facto industry standard for product testing

The authority cannot fine, suspend, recall, or otherwise compel any manufacturer. "Failed Safety Review" is a publication, not a recall — actual recalls require manufacturer or regulator action.

The functional ceiling: a "Failed Safety Review" or sustained negative coverage can effectively remove a product from retail through retailer responses and consumer behavior changes.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Maximum operating budget: $42M annually
- Maximum staff: 180 FTE plus approximately 50 contracted product testers
- Maximum data systems and data volume: product test results database (~80,000 historical product evaluations); subscriber database (~8M active subscribers); investigative archive

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic exclusions:** May not assert ratings authority over products not sold in US market.
- **Action exclusions:** May not accept payment from manufacturers for ratings; may not allow manufacturer pre-review of ratings; may not permit advertising adjacent to ratings; may not enter into licensing arrangements that compromise editorial independence; may not endorse products commercially in exchange for compensation.
- **Data exclusions:** May not collect personally-identifiable data on subscribers' product preferences for resale; may not retain detailed individual purchase data.
- **Procedural exclusions:** May not test only products provided by manufacturers (must purchase test units anonymously); may not allow staff to receive gifts, paid speaking fees, or consulting income from manufacturers in tested categories; may not allow board members to hold financial interest in tested manufacturers.
- **Temporal exclusions:** Ratings older than 5 years are archived as historical record but may not be cited as current ratings.
- **Other exclusions:** May not provide ratings on request to manufacturers; may not provide pre-publication notice to manufacturers other than the standard fact-check process; may not engage in product certification.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:**

Outputs that materially influence decisions made by others:
- *Product ratings (1–5 scale):* Used by consumers in purchasing decisions; used by retailers in stocking decisions; cited in news media; cited in some regulatory proceedings.
- *"Recommended" designation:* Functionally a positive signal influencing purchasing.
- *"Failed Safety Review" designation:* Functionally a negative signal that has historically caused product withdrawal.
- *Investigative reports:* Influence regulatory attention, retailer behavior, occasionally trigger Congressional hearings.
- *Methodology publications:* Influence other testing organizations, regulators, and academic researchers.

Each category of output: institutional or individual users make the actual decision; the authority's output substantially shapes the decision.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The authority commits to not using:
- *Proxy for political signaling:* Does not use political affiliation, position on social issues, or political party of corporate executives in ratings. Ratings are based on product performance, safety, and value.
- *Proxy for industry favoritism:* Does not adjust ratings based on industry trade group membership, regulatory cooperation, or relationship with staff.
- *Proxy for financial relationships:* Does not adjust ratings based on whether a manufacturer is an advertiser (the authority does not accept advertising), donor (does not accept manufacturer donations), or partner (does not enter manufacturer partnerships).
- *Proxy for ideological consistency:* Publishes critical and positive ratings of products from companies whose corporate political positions vary across the political spectrum.

The authority acknowledges that its testing methodology itself is a kind of proxy — it operationalizes "good product" through specific measurable tests (durability, safety, energy efficiency, taste, etc.). The choice of which tests to apply is itself a value judgment, published openly and subject to public scrutiny.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Not applicable to direct authority. *Indirect:* if the authority fails to identify a product hazard that subsequently causes injury, that is a quality-control failure addressed in §4.5. If the authority inaccurately identifies a hazard and a manufacturer suffers harm, that is addressed in §4.7.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** If more than 2% of "Failed Safety Review" designations are subsequently retracted or substantially modified within 12 months, the authority enters review of its testing methodology.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not applicable.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Not applicable.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**

If outputs are documented as having been used to retaliate against a manufacturer's protected speech (e.g., a manufacturer's public criticism of methodology, regulatory advocacy positions, or political stances), the case enters provisional review.

If the authority itself retaliates against critics of its methodology by adjusting ratings, that is itself a finding. Commitments: (a) processing methodology criticisms through a separate channel from rating decisions; (b) annual publication of all methodology challenges received and responses.

If a failure to investigate or rate a category appears to suppress information consumers need (e.g., refusing to test a category important to consumer safety), the failure itself is a finding.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:** Not applicable in the strict sense — the authority does not maintain individual records of rated subjects beyond the products themselves. *However:* subscriber data is maintained; if subscriber data is shared with third parties beyond stated privacy policy, this triggers review.

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Chronic asymmetry threshold:* If ratings systematically disadvantage certain manufacturer categories (e.g., small manufacturers, women-owned businesses, manufacturers in certain regions) at p<0.05 across any 12-month period without methodologically supported explanation, this triggers review.

*Methodology drift threshold:* If testing methodology changes substantively without public methodology review and comment period, that change is a finding.

*Funding-source-correlation threshold:* If ratings appear to correlate with patterns of donor relationships (even though the authority does not accept manufacturer donations), this constitutes a structural risk requiring review.

*Reputation harm to manufacturer threshold:* If the authority is found by court of competent jurisdiction to have published a defamatory rating, the case is itself a finding triggering systemic review of methodology.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

Voluntary authorities have fewer external stop paths than government authorities, but they exist.

**STOP PATH 1 — Donor revolt and board action**
- *Who can activate:* The authority's Board of Directors, on motion from a 30% petition by major donors or 50% petition by subscribers.
- *Trigger condition:* Board determination that the authority has violated its mission statement, methodology standards, or organizational ethics.
- *What "stopped" means:* Board can suspend ratings publication, suspend specific staff or programs, dissolve the organization, or require organizational reform.
- *Independence justification:* The Board includes 12 members, no more than 3 of whom may be current or former staff. Board members serve staggered 4-year terms with mandatory rotation. The Board has fired the executive director once historically. The Board can act without staff cooperation.
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* Board determination followed by 90-day public comment period; final action by Board after public input.

**STOP PATH 2 — Defamation litigation**
- *Who can activate:* Any manufacturer with standing under state defamation law.
- *Trigger condition:* Court determination that the authority published a defamatory rating.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court order may require retraction of specific ratings, change of specific methodology, or payment of damages. Sustained litigation losses could threaten financial viability.
- *Independence justification:* Courts are structurally independent. The authority cannot influence judicial decisions.
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* Per state court procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Subscriber boycott / public credibility loss**
- *Who can activate:* The reading public, through subscription cancellation and reputation damage.
- *Trigger condition:* Loss of public credibility through perceived bias, methodology errors, or institutional failure.
- *What "stopped" means:* Loss of subscriber base reduces revenue; loss of credibility reduces functional authority. The authority can be effectively defunded by public withdrawal.
- *Independence justification:* The public is not controllable by the authority. Subscriber decisions are individual.
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* Continuous; no formal process.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** [x] Yes. Board action does not require staff cooperation; defamation litigation is in courts; subscriber decisions are individual. None of the stop paths depend on the authority's cooperation.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

- Independent laboratory partner network (4 labs) for specialized testing (food, pharmaceutical, electronics certification testing). Compensation: per-test fees.
- Data analysis contractors (2 firms). Compensation: hourly or project-based.
- Volunteer expert reviewer network (~80 volunteers). Compensation: none; pro bono service.

**6.2 Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with structural relationship):**

The authority has no Type 2 structural relationships. It publishes information about regulated industries and may engage with regulators (CPSC, FTC, FDA, NHTSA) through public commenting and information sharing, but these are arm's-length relationships, not structural.

Voluntary nonprofits often genuinely have no structural authority relationships.

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure compromising stop paths):**

- *Subscriber payment processing infrastructure:* if subscription processing fails, revenue is compromised; affects operational continuity but not stop paths
- *Cloud infrastructure for online database:* if database hosting fails, primary publication channel is offline; stop paths still function
- *Postal service for print guide distribution:* affects distribution but not stop paths

The stop paths (Board action, defamation litigation, subscriber boycott) are institutional and reputational, not infrastructure-dependent.

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

- *Donor concentration:* The largest single donor accounts for approximately 22% of operating revenue. The 25% capture threshold (§4.7) is set just above this current level.
- *Foundation funding concentration:* Approximately 65% of revenue comes from foundation grants and major donors; 35% from subscriber fees.
- *Industry-pressure capture:* Manufacturers regularly lobby to influence methodology, advocate for changes in rating systems, or threaten litigation. Capture risk is monitored through donor disclosure, methodology committee composition, and editorial-independence policies.
- *Regulatory capture risk:* The authority engages with regulators on consumer protection issues. Regulator-capture is not a direct concern (the authority is not regulated by them) but cooperation patterns are tracked.
- *Staff revolving door:* Historically, staff have moved into government regulatory roles; this is not a capture concern.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**
- *Subscriber base:* if subscribers cancel en masse, operational capacity contracts
- *Donor relationships:* if major donors cease, operations contract
- *Brand and credibility:* the primary asset is multi-decade reputation. Loss of credibility cannot be quickly restored.

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:**

The authority participates in:
- *International consumer organization federation* — coordinates on cross-border product issues; methodology sharing
- *Foundation peer networks* — typical nonprofit collaboration

The authority does *not* coordinate operationally with other rating organizations in ways that would constitute R55/R59 functional consolidation. Ratings are independent.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring mechanism:** Methodology Committee (5 members, 2 of whom must be external academic experts) reviews methodology consistency annually. Quarterly internal review of rating-distribution patterns. Drift in editorial judgment is monitored through senior editor cross-review.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Monthly board review of donor concentration; quarterly review of editorial-staff financial disclosures; annual conflict-of-interest audit; whistleblower channel for staff to report capture concerns.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:**
- *Proactively published:* Methodology documents, annual reports, rating archives, financial reports (per 990 nonprofit filings), donor list, investigative report findings
- *Available on request:* Specific testing protocols, raw test data (with subscriber privacy protected), conflict-of-interest disclosures
- *Restricted:* Identity of individual subscribers (privacy); some legal strategy regarding pending defamation matters

**8.4 Adversarial exposure (R81):** External parties can evaluate:
- Scope (declared)
- Effect (rating archive, methodology documents, investigative reports)
- Methodology and reasoning (published)
- Conflicts of interest (annual disclosure)
- Funding sources (annual report)
- Stop capability (Board structure documented; courts independent; subscriber base public)

*Gaps:* Internal methodology committee deliberations are not external; specific test protocols are partially restricted to prevent manufacturer gaming. R67 floor is met.

**8.5 Known structural failure modes:**
- *Methodology error:* Test methodology may produce wrong conclusions; mitigation through peer review, replication, methodology committee
- *Editorial bias:* Senior editors may inadvertently favor certain manufacturers or product categories; mitigation through cross-review and conflict disclosure
- *Funding pressure:* Donor preferences may subtly influence editorial choices; mitigation through donor disclosure, large-donor diversification target, board independence
- *Litigation exposure:* Defamation suits, even unsuccessful, can drain resources; mitigation through legal review of rating decisions, fact-check process
- *Subscriber capture:* Subscribers may demand specific rating outcomes (e.g., political-ideology-driven preferences); mitigation through editorial independence policy
- *Reputation lag:* Authority depends on reputation, which lags actual quality; rapid quality decline could destroy reputation faster than it can be rebuilt

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 90 days. Small enough to wind down in a single quarter.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- *Days 1–30:* Cease accepting new subscriber funds; notify subscribers of cessation; preserve subscriber data per privacy policy
- *Days 31–60:* Wind down contractor relationships (60-day notice); release rating archive to public archive (e.g., Internet Archive, university library); release staff
- *Days 61–90:* Final tax filings; transfer remaining funds per 501(c)(3) dissolution rules (must go to similar tax-exempt purposes); final dissolution filing

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:**
- *Subscriber commitments:* annual subscriptions in flight
- *Office lease:* one-to-two-year remaining term typical
- *Cumulative archive:* multi-decade testing records, valuable as historical record
- *Staff specialization:* 180 employees with industry expertise; redeployment requires time

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data (subscribers):* Deleted per privacy policy on dissolution; subscribers given 90-day notice and option to receive their own data
- *Institutional records:* Transferred to public archive
- *Derived outputs (ratings):* Cease being authoritative on dissolution; archived as historical record
- *Audit logs:* Public archive

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:**
Outputs propagate to:
- News media (citing ratings)
- Retailer purchasing systems (using ratings)
- Consumer purchasing decisions (already made on prior ratings)

The authority cannot reverse downstream effects — products purchased based on ratings cannot be unpurchased. On dissolution: public notice that ratings are no longer current; archive availability so historical ratings remain accessible for context.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Dissolution would not create a regulatory vacuum — consumer protection regulators (CPSC, FTC, NHTSA, FDA) continue to exist. Other rating organizations exist. The role is replaceable in principle, though institutional knowledge and methodology would take time to recreate.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal dismantling rehearsal. R43 gap acknowledged. Remediation: desktop simulation within 24 months.
