## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** MUTUAL-AID-NETWORK-EXAMPLE — a neighborhood-scale volunteer-run mutual aid network.

**1.2 Functional description:** A volunteer-run mutual aid network operating in a defined neighborhood service area of [CITY, STATE]. Functional authority:
- Receives requests for material support (groceries, prepared meals, diapers, hygiene supplies, transportation, small cash assistance, emergency rent or utility help) through a public request form, a phone hotline, and word-of-mouth
- Coordinates volunteer responses through a shared roster (~80 active volunteers) and a small coordinating group (~9 rotating coordinators)
- Maintains a fund of neighborhood-pooled cash (typical balance ~$8,000; annual flow ~$45,000) used for direct cash assistance and bulk supply purchases
- Operates a free pantry / community fridge at one location and runs periodic distribution events
- Maintains a contact list of approximately 1,400 neighbors who have either requested support, given support, or both — many neighbors have done both

The network does not screen requests for income eligibility, citizenship status, household composition, or "deservingness." Its operating principle is solidarity: requests are filled to the extent capacity allows. When capacity is constrained, the coordinating group decides allocation through informal practice — first-come for low-cost requests, deliberation for larger-cost or recurring requests.

The network is consequential because for some neighbors, mutual aid is the difference between rent paid and eviction filed, between groceries and skipped meals, between making it to a medical appointment and not. Decisions about whom to support and how — including informal decisions, including "no" decisions — affect material conditions for neighbors in real ways. The framework treats this as authority even at this scale.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting access to material support that the network controls (allocation decisions, exclusion from ongoing support, restrictions on participation as a volunteer or recipient).

**1.4 Domain:** Voluntary Association.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** The network is informal: not incorporated, not registered as a 501(c)(3), no formal governance documents. It operates as an unincorporated association under [STATE] law. The pooled fund is held in a fiscal-sponsorship arrangement with [FISCAL SPONSOR — typically a local 501(c)(3) acting as fiscal sponsor for the cash side], or alternatively in a personal account held by a designated coordinator with a written informal-trust agreement. Subject to general law: gift and tax law on cash flows; employment law (none — all labor is volunteer); contract law as applicable.

The network has chosen to remain informal. This is acknowledged as a structural feature: informality reduces administrative burden and lowers exit cost for participants but also reduces the structural protections that incorporation provides. The framework does not require incorporation; it requires honesty about what informal structure does and does not provide.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (~1,400 neighbors who have interacted with the network in some capacity)
- [x] Members (voluntary affiliation) — ~80 active volunteers and ~9 coordinators
- [x] Vulnerable populations: neighbors in housing instability; neighbors with limited income; elderly neighbors; neighbors with disabilities; neighbors with limited English proficiency; undocumented neighbors; neighbors leaving abusive relationships; neighbors recently released from incarceration
- [x] Children — children in households the network supports; children of volunteers who participate alongside their parents

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** ~1,400 neighbors who have been on the contact list at some point; ~250 in active recipient relationship in any given quarter; ~80 active volunteers.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Zero.

The network's operating principle is voluntary mutual aid. Neighbors choose to request support or not. Volunteers choose to participate or not. No one is required to be on the contact list; anyone on it may ask to be removed. Exit is genuinely costless: a neighbor who stops engaging with the network experiences no penalty beyond loss of network support, which they had no entitlement to in the first place.

The network notes the framework's voluntary-but-stuck warning. For neighbors in acute material need, the network's support may be one of few available resources, and a perceived risk of being "cut off" could create informal coercion even where formal coercion does not exist. The network commits to a written practice of: (a) not conditioning support on agreement with network views or behavior; (b) not retaliating against neighbors who criticize the network; (c) explicit notice to recipients that they are not required to participate in network activities to remain eligible for support.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes*. Children in supported households are affected indirectly by support decisions (groceries received or not, rent paid or not). The network does not interact with children directly except through their parents or caregivers; children's information is not collected separately. Volunteers' children who participate alongside parents are affected as participants.
- [ ] Patients during medical care — not applicable.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — not applicable as a direct authority surface; the network does support neighbors recently released from incarceration through reentry assistance, but this is recipient-driven.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Recipients in acute material need are by definition in coercive economic relationships with respect to subsistence; the network's support is offered as solidarity rather than exchange, but the underlying coercion shapes the relationship. Mitigation: explicit anti-conditioning practice (above); anonymous request channel; multi-coordinator decision-making for sensitive cases.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Neighbors with limited English proficiency, neighbors with cognitive or developmental disabilities, neighbors in crisis. Mitigations: request form available in [LANGUAGE 1] and [LANGUAGE 2] in addition to English; volunteers with relevant language and interpretation capacity rostered for response; designated check-in process for recipients where ongoing support has been requested. R26 partial gap acknowledged for neighbors whose languages or accessibility needs fall outside current capacity.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — defined neighborhood service area in [CITY, STATE]. Service area is approximately [N] census tracts; the network occasionally responds outside the defined area for connected neighbors (e.g., a former neighbor who relocated nearby) but does not actively recruit or maintain operations outside.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Decisions on which support requests to fill, in what amount, and how quickly
- Decisions on whether to extend ongoing support beyond initial request
- Decisions on whether to include or exclude particular neighbors from the contact list
- Decisions on whether to accept or decline volunteer participation from particular individuals (including for safety reasons — e.g., volunteers credibly accused of harm to neighbors)
- Decisions on whether and how to disburse pooled funds
- Decisions on whether to maintain or terminate the network itself

The maximum sanction available is exclusion from network participation. The network cannot impose obligations, fines, liens, or restrictions on neighbors beyond their voluntary engagement with it; cannot file legal action against neighbors; cannot compel any behavior.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Cash: typical balance ~$8,000; annual flow ~$45,000
- Material: free pantry / community fridge inventory; pooled supplies (diapers, hygiene, etc.) in coordinator basements/garages
- Volunteer labor: ~80 active volunteers (varying intensity), ~9 rotating coordinators
- No paid staff; no contracted services beyond fiscal sponsorship arrangement

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** Will not actively recruit or expand outside the defined service area without coordinator group consensus.
- **Action:** Will not condition material support on agreement with network views, participation in network activities, religious affiliation, citizenship status, sobriety, employment status, family composition, or "deservingness" criteria. Will not impose fees or repayment obligations on recipients. Will not threaten or retaliate against neighbors who criticize the network.
- **Data:** Will not collect or retain neighbor information beyond what coordination requires. Will not share neighbor information outside the network without consent. Will not maintain records of immigration status, citizenship, or related information. Will not share information with law enforcement absent legal process and coordinator-group consultation.
- **Procedural:** Coordinator-group decisions on contested matters require consensus or supermajority per practice; no individual coordinator may make exclusion decisions affecting ongoing support unilaterally.
- **Other:** Will not partner with organizations that condition support on requirements the network would not impose itself (e.g., work requirements, sobriety requirements, religious observance requirements).

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Limited at this scale. The network's existence and practices may modestly influence neighborhood relationships, may shape how connected neighbors think about reciprocity and mutual support, and may serve as a reference for similar networks in other neighborhoods. R58 acknowledged at small scale.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The network commits to not using:
- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin, language, immigration status:* Support decisions may not use any of these as criteria or as proxy. Network does not collect this information from recipients. Coordinator-group periodically reviews aggregate patterns of who is and is not being reached for evidence of disparate access. R66/R74.
- *Proxy for "deservingness":* Decisions about whom to support may not use markers of "deserving" vs. "undeserving" recipient (employment status, sobriety, family configuration, prior receipt). Solidarity is the operating principle; the form of need does not affect eligibility.
- *Proxy for personal animus:* Coordinators must recuse from decisions involving neighbors with whom they have personal conflict. Coordinator-group decisions on exclusion (volunteer or recipient) are documented with reasons and reviewed by full coordinator group rather than left to individual coordinator.
- *Proxy for political or religious affiliation:* Support decisions may not vary based on neighbors' political views, religion, or organizational affiliations.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect. The network's primary surface is material support, not direct physical action. Two indirect harm pathways:
- *Failure to deliver promised support:* If a neighbor relies on a committed support delivery (food, medication-support, transport to medical appointment) that does not arrive, this can produce bodily harm. The network commits to: (a) explicit confirmation of commitments before recipient relies; (b) early notice of any inability to deliver; (c) backup volunteer roster for time-sensitive commitments. Failure of this practice triggers internal review.
- *Support of unsafe practices:* The network does not provide medical care or substance-use supplies; if it expanded into harm-reduction supplies (e.g., naloxone, clean syringes), explicit threshold-setting would be required.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Coordinator-group decisions are made without affected coordinators (or affected recipients in cases of recipient exclusion) having opportunity to provide input
- Volunteer participation is excluded without notice and opportunity to respond
- Recipient is excluded from ongoing support without explanation or opportunity to address
- Coordinator-group meetings are conducted in ways that systematically exclude particular coordinators (timing, location, format)

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not directly applicable. Pantry/fridge food-waste minimization is committed as practice but not as a measurable threshold at this scale.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Not substantively applicable at this scale. The network does not undertake long-duration commitments binding future participants. If the network develops physical infrastructure (community space lease, owned property, etc.), this section requires re-evaluation at registration update.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any neighbor is excluded from ongoing support on the basis of criticism of the network or its coordinators
- Volunteers are excluded for criticism of network practice
- Information needed for coordinator-group decisions (financial status, allocation patterns, exclusion decisions) is withheld from coordinators or active volunteers
- Network communications are restricted in ways that prevent participants from raising concerns

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Neighbor information shared outside the network without consent — finding
- Records retained beyond stated retention (typically: contact list maintained until removal requested; specific request records purged after 12 months; financial records per fiscal sponsor's requirements) — finding
- Information shared with law enforcement absent legal process and coordinator-group consultation — finding
- Photographs or identifying information published without explicit consent — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Disparate access threshold:* If recipient demographics consistently underrepresent particular neighborhood populations relative to neighborhood demographics (e.g., Spanish-speaking households, undocumented households, particular blocks), this triggers coordinator-group review of outreach and request channels.

*Exclusion-decision threshold:* Any exclusion of a recipient from ongoing support, or any exclusion of a volunteer, requires coordinator-group decision with documented reasons and notification to the affected person of: the decision, the reasons in plain language, and any path to review.

*Coordinator-tenure threshold:* If the same individuals occupy coordinator roles for more than 18 of any 24 consecutive months, this triggers review of rotation practice. The network's stated practice is rotating coordinators; persistence beyond rotation indicates structural drift.

*Volunteer-of-color-and-marginalized-volunteer threshold:* The network commits to ongoing attention to whether volunteer composition reflects the neighborhood it serves and whether decision-making space is genuinely shared rather than nominally shared. Persistent disparate-voice patterns trigger review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

The network's stop paths are weaker than incorporated organizations. This is structurally honest.

**STOP PATH 1 — Coordinator-group dissolution or restructuring**
- *Activator:* Any coordinator may propose; supermajority or consensus of coordinator group, or full active-volunteer meeting, may decide.
- *Trigger:* Coordinator or volunteer determination of network failure, capture, or need to dissolve/restart.
- *What "stopped" means:* Coordinator group may suspend specific operations, change practice, restructure, or wind down the network; pooled funds disposition by coordinator group consensus per fiscal sponsor's requirements.
- *Independence:* Coordinator group is the network's structure. Active volunteers can replace coordinators by withdrawing participation and forming alternative coordination. *Acknowledged limitation:* This is not externally independent — it is internal restructuring. External independence requires Stop Path 2 or 3.
- *Timeline:* Days to weeks, depending on convening logistics.

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil action or legal complaint**
- *Activator:* Any participant or affected neighbor with standing.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of fraud, conversion, breach of fiscal-sponsor agreement, or violation of [STATE] law.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders may halt practices, void actions, impose obligations on individual coordinators or fiscal sponsor.
- *Independence:* Courts independent.
- *Timeline:* Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Withdrawal of fiscal sponsorship and informal community accountability**
- *Activator:* Fiscal sponsor may terminate the sponsorship relationship; participants may withdraw collectively; neighborhood-level accountability through public conversation, organized concern, or external observation by sister networks.
- *Trigger:* Fiscal sponsor determination of misuse; participant determination; community-accountability process.
- *What "stopped" means:* Loss of fiscal sponsorship means the network cannot maintain pooled-fund infrastructure as currently structured. Participant withdrawal means the network loses operational capacity. Community-accountability processes may produce coordinator changes, practice changes, or dissolution.
- *Independence:* Fiscal sponsor is independent of the network. Community accountability is informal and depends on local capacity. *Acknowledged limitation:* This stop path is real but informal; in jurisdictions or neighborhoods without strong sister-network or accountability ecology, this path may not function in practice.
- *Timeline:* Per actor.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies as a matter of network practice. *Acknowledged structural concern:* Informal organizations have weaker structural protections against retaliation than incorporated ones. The network's commitment is non-retaliation; its capacity to enforce that commitment depends on coordinator-group integrity.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** Stop Path 2 (courts) is genuinely independent. Stop Paths 1 and 3 are partially independent — they involve internal action (1) or informal external action (3) rather than fully external authority. The network acknowledges this as a structural feature of unincorporated mutual aid networks; remediation would require incorporation, which the network has chosen not to pursue. R32 partial gap acknowledged honestly.

**6.2 Type 2 dependencies:** None of structural significance. The network's interactions with municipal services (free pantry placement, occasional event permits) are transactional rather than structural.

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies:**
- Fiscal sponsor relationship — if terminated, the cash-fund infrastructure as currently configured cannot continue without alternative arrangement (incorporation, transfer to another sponsor, or shift to all-direct-volunteer-handling)

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

- *Coordinator-tenure capture:* Long-tenured coordinators may exercise influence beyond formal practice. Mitigation: rotation commitment; explicit term framing in coordinator-group practice.
- *Volunteer-clique capture:* Active volunteers who are also social peers may form an in-group that shapes network practice. Mitigation: outreach to neighbors not currently in volunteer roles; periodic check on volunteer composition vs. neighborhood composition.
- *Fiscal sponsor influence:* The fiscal sponsor's requirements shape financial practice. Mitigation: written agreement specifying scope of sponsor authority; coordinator-group review of any sponsor-imposed condition.
- *Recipient-coordinator overlap:* Many coordinators have been or are recipients; this is a strength of mutual aid practice but creates the question of whose voice carries in allocation decisions. The network embraces this overlap and commits to documented reasons for decisions, particularly where coordinators have direct interest.
- *Funder concentration:* Largest single funder accounts for 24% of annual flow in the most recent year. Threshold for review at 33% per form §6.4.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:** Limited. The network can wind down without substantial entrenchment beyond pooled-fund disposition.

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:** Voluntary peer relationship with sister mutual aid networks in nearby neighborhoods — sharing of practice, occasional joint distribution events. No formal consortium, no R55/R59 functional continuity.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring:** Quarterly coordinator-group review of practice, capacity, and patterns; semi-annual all-volunteer meeting; informal community-level conversation through ongoing relationships and through sister-network peer exchange.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Coordinator-group periodically reviews tenure, decision patterns, funder concentration, and volunteer-vs-neighborhood-composition.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:** Aggregate cash-flow data, request volume, fulfillment patterns are available to participants on request. The network publishes an annual snapshot of activity (no individual recipient data; aggregate dollars flowed, requests fulfilled, volunteer count, coordinator turnover). Individual records (specific requests, specific exclusion decisions) are restricted to the subject and the coordinator group.

**8.4 Adversarial exposure:** Limited at this scale. External review by sister networks or peer mutual aid practitioners is informal but real. The network commits to reasonable cooperation with researchers studying mutual aid practice within privacy limits.

**8.5 Known structural failure modes:**
- Coordinator burnout reducing decision-making capacity
- Volunteer-clique drift reducing inclusion of newer or differently-situated neighbors
- Drift toward "deservingness" criteria as informal practice
- Cash-handling errors or misappropriation
- Disparate access patterns by language, immigration status, neighborhood block, or other characteristic
- Coordinator personal relationships shaping decisions
- Information leaks (formal or informal) to law enforcement or other parties
- Fiscal sponsor relationship strain
- Mission drift toward charity-style allocation rather than solidarity

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 90 days. Justification: the network has limited entrenchment. Wind-down requires: notification to active volunteers and contact list; disbursement or transfer of pooled fund per fiscal sponsor's requirements; transfer of physical infrastructure (free pantry, fridge) to neighborhood successor or removal; termination of fiscal sponsorship arrangement.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Days 1–30: Coordinator-group decision; notice to volunteers and contact list; identification of successor structure or alternative resources for active recipients
- Days 31–60: Disbursement or transfer of pooled fund (typically: distribution to current acute-need recipients; transfer to sister network; donation to fiscal sponsor for similar use)
- Days 61–90: Final accounting; physical-infrastructure disposition; archive or destruction of records per retention practice; fiscal sponsorship termination

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Minimal at this scale. Active recipient relationships are the most consequential factor — recipients who relied on ongoing support need transition resources or referral. The network commits to active referral and transition support during wind-down.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Contact list deleted unless individual neighbors request transfer to successor; specific request records purged; financial records per fiscal sponsor and tax requirements
- *Institutional records:* Coordinator-group meeting notes, practice documents archived at neighborhood archive or with sister network if requested
- *Derived outputs:* None of structural significance

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — past allocation and exclusion decisions cannot be reversed. The network acknowledges this and commits to: (a) preserving exclusion-decision records to enable affected former recipients to seek review; (b) providing referrals in good faith.

The network's primary downstream effects are positive (material support delivered, neighbor relationships built) but include real adverse effects (recipients who were excluded; recipients who relied and were not supported; volunteers who experienced exclusion). These cannot be undone.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Yes. Mutual aid is a practice, not an institution; the practice can be reconstituted by any group of neighbors with the will to begin. R51 met at this scale.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. At this scale, the gap is acknowledged as acceptable but not erased; the coordinator group commits to a desktop discussion of wind-down logistics within 12 months of registration as a low-cost form of rehearsal appropriate to the network's scale.
