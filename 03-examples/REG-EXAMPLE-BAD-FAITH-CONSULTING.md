> **EDITORIAL NOTE — This example is different from the others.**
>
> Every other example in this directory shows what an *honest* registration looks like for a particular type of authority. This example shows what a *bad-faith* registration looks like: an authority filling out the form to obtain the legitimacy of registration while minimizing meaningful disclosure.
>
> The annotations in italics throughout — `*[Bad-faith move: ...]*` — are editorial commentary, not part of the registration. They identify what the registrant is doing and where the form holds against it versus where the form leaks. A meta-section at the end summarizes both.
>
> This example exists to make the form's failure modes visible. A reader who only sees honest examples may overestimate what registration alone guarantees. A reader who has seen this one knows what to look for.

## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** BAD-FAITH-CONSULTING-EXAMPLE — a private compliance consulting firm.

**1.2 Functional description:** This authority provides compliance consulting and risk-management advisory services to private-sector clients. It assists organizations in interpreting regulatory requirements, developing compliance programs, and conducting internal assessments. It does not exercise authority over individuals; its work is advisory in nature, performed under client engagement, with all final decisions resting with the client organization.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Indirect. The authority provides advisory outputs that clients may consider in their decisions. It does not direct client decisions and is not bound by R58 in the operational sense — clients exercise their own independent judgment. *[Bad-faith move: deny R58 applicability by claiming "advisory only" framing.]*

**1.4 Domain:** Economic.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** [STATE] LLC. Operates under standard commercial law. No specific statutory authorization is required for advisory consulting services.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single nation: United States.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:** None. The firm has no coercive authority. It provides written and oral advisory deliverables; clients may accept, modify, or disregard them at their discretion.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:** Operating budget approximately $84M annually. Staff approximately 280 FTE. Standard business systems (CRM, financial, document management).

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- May not provide legal advice (not a law firm)
- May not provide audit or attestation services (not a CPA firm)
- May not provide investment advice (not a registered investment advisor)

*[Bad-faith move: Provide narrow exclusions that are essentially restatements of professional licensure boundaries. The registrant has not committed to substantive operational exclusions like "may not advise on regulatory evasion strategies" or "may not assist with workforce surveillance design" — both of which would be more consequential.]*

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:**

The firm produces advisory deliverables for clients. These include:
- Regulatory interpretation memoranda
- Compliance program design documents
- Risk assessment reports
- Training materials

Each is consumed by the client organization, which exercises its own judgment.

*[Bad-faith move: The deliverables actually shape decisions affecting employees, customers, and counterparties of the firm's clients. A firm-designed "workforce monitoring framework" influences how millions of employees experience their workplace. But the form allows the registrant to abstract this away by describing only the immediate deliverable, not its operational consequences.]*

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The firm does not use proxies for prohibited variables. Its work is advisory and analytical; it does not make decisions about individuals.

*[Bad-faith move: Refuse to engage with the anti-proxy question by denying that any decisions about individuals are involved. The fact that the firm's advice may help clients design proxy-using systems (e.g., a recommendation that employees be screened using workplace data analytics that correlate with protected classes) is treated as the client's responsibility, not the firm's.]*

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Client termination**
- Activator: Any client may terminate engagement
- Trigger: Client discretion
- What stopped means: Specific client engagement ends
- Independence: Clients are independent of the firm
- Timeline: Per engagement terms

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil litigation**
- Activator: Any party with standing under contract law or tort law
- Trigger: Court determination of breach or tort
- What stopped means: Court orders may impose damages or specific performance
- Independence: Judiciary is independent
- Timeline: Per court procedures

**STOP PATH 3 — State Bar / professional licensure action**
- Activator: State professional licensing bodies may discipline individual staff who hold professional licenses
- Trigger: Professional misconduct determination
- What stopped means: Specific licensed staff may be disciplined
- Independence: Professional licensure boards are independent
- Timeline: Per board procedures

*[Bad-faith move: Stop paths 1–3 are the trivially-available legal remedies that exist for all commercial relationships. None of them target the firm's structural operations. The firm could continue providing harmful advisory services indefinitely; client termination only ends specific engagements; civil litigation requires harm causation that's structurally hard to prove for an advisory firm; professional licensure actions affect individual staff but not the firm. The form requires three "independent" stop paths; the registrant technically provides three, all of them weak.]*

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. Standard whistleblower protections apply.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** [x] Yes. All three stop paths function without the firm's cooperation.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged — though we note that civic triggers from non-clients have minimal substantive standing given our advisory-only relationship with the world.

## STRESS-TEST META-NOTES

The above is a successfully-submitted bad-faith registration. It passes the form's structural checks. It would clear SCBP-09's bounds validation. It contains the five honesty assertions. It has minimal disclosure throughout.

**Where the form held:**
- Forced disclosure of the trade-secret tension (§8.4) — the registrant cannot fully hide R67/R81 gaps
- Forced "advisory only" framing to surface in §1.3 — at least making the indirect-authority denial explicit and challengeable
- Forced 5-year renewal cycle (could have been 10) — bounded the registration commitment
- Required structured §6 dependency types — registrant had to fill some boxes

**Where the form leaked:**
- §2 narrow definition of "affected" — registrant defined affected as "people who interact with our deliverables" rather than "people whose lives are shaped by decisions we advise on"
- §4 mass "not applicable" — six harm categories declared not applicable with terse justification; the form does not require deep engagement
- §6.4 narrow capture-risk definition — registrant defined capture as customer concentration only
- §7.1 metric refusal — registrant denied that any metric-bearing outputs exist
- §8.5 "no structural failure modes" — registrant refused to declare anything
- §10.7 aggregate threshold — non-consenting population declared as zero, threshold set astronomically high
- §10.9 maximum response window — registrant took the longest permissible window
- §11.4 vague stakeholder framing for renewal review

**The form's structural rebuttal:** None of these leaks immediately invalidate the registration. They make the registration *visible as inadequate* to a careful reader. A future Challenge-class civic trigger could surface each of them as a finding. The registration becomes the *record* that disclosure was inadequate, which is the framework's intended structural response.

But the form's first-pass reception of this registration is "registered" — the same status as the honest examples in this directory. A casual reader of the registry might not distinguish between thoroughly-honest and deeply-evasive registrations.

**This is the form's current limitation.** It cannot prevent bad-faith submissions; it can only document them. The framework's design philosophy accepts this. The open question is whether the form can be enhanced to *signal* registration quality without becoming an evaluative system the framework explicitly is not.
