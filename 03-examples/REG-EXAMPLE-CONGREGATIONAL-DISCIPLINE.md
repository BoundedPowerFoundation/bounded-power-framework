## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** CONGREGATIONAL-DISCIPLINE-EXAMPLE — the disciplinary council of a religious assembly.

**1.2 Functional description:** The Disciplinary Council is the body within the religious assembly that adjudicates matters of doctrine, conduct, and membership status. It may: investigate alleged conduct violations; conduct hearings; issue findings of doctrinal violation; impose ecclesiastical discipline including admonition, restriction from communion, restriction from participation in church functions, public censure, and excommunication; recommend marriage dissolution within the church's covenant framework; recommend custody dispositions within church tribunals (subject to civil law); require disclosure of personal information for membership review.

The Council does not have civil legal authority. Its authority operates within the religious community and through the social, family, and economic relationships that follow from membership.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct, within its religious sphere. The Council issues binding determinations affecting members' standing within the assembly and within the broader religious community. Members who reject the Council's authority face excommunication, which has substantial real-world consequences within the closed religious community.

**1.4 Domain:** Cultural / Religious.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** The assembly operates as a religious nonprofit corporation under [STATE] state law. The Disciplinary Council's authority derives from the assembly's bylaws, which are themselves derived from interpretation of religious texts. The First Amendment's Free Exercise and Establishment Clauses protect the Council's authority within ecclesiastical matters; civil courts generally decline to adjudicate purely religious disciplinary matters under the ministerial exception and ecclesiastical abstention doctrines.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Members (voluntary affiliation, but with significant exit costs — see §2.3)
- [x] Children (children of members are subject to the assembly's spiritual formation and discipline structures)
- [x] Vulnerable populations (members in coercive economic relationships with other members; members whose entire social world is the assembly; members with no family outside the assembly)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties: spouses of members in marriage cases; family members whose relationships are affected by excommunication decisions

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** Approximately 4,800 members across affiliated congregations.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Approximately 1,300 individuals are non-consenting in a meaningful sense:
- Children of members (~900) — born into the community without choosing membership
- Members whose entire social, economic, and family network is within the assembly (~250) — exit means total social loss
- Spouses of members where one spouse's discipline affects both (~150)

These are individuals whose technical "voluntary" affiliation does not reflect meaningful capacity to exit. R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) is interpreted by the Council to apply to these populations because their exit options are structurally constrained by the community's design.

The Council acknowledges that this declaration is a substantial concession — many religious institutions do not openly characterize their high-cost-exit members as non-consenting. Honest registration requires acknowledging the actual relationship between authority and exit.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes, substantively*. Children born into the assembly do not consent to membership; they are subject to disciplinary structures from infancy. Commits to: (a) special procedures for any disciplinary matter involving minors; (b) mandatory reporting of suspected child abuse; (c) preservation of educational access independent of disciplinary status; (d) no enforcement of discipline that requires parents to physically punish children.
- [ ] Patients during medical care — *partially*. The Council does not direct medical decisions but may discipline members who decline religious counsel about medical decisions. Gap area.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — not applicable.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Many members are economically integrated with other members through employment, housing, or business relationships. Discipline can rupture economic standing.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Members with limited education, limited resources, or cognitive impairment may face Council proceedings without effective advocacy. The Council does not currently provide formal advocacy support; R26 gap acknowledged.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Multi-state — 7 states. Authority follows membership, not territory; a member moving to a new state remains under the Council's authority.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Investigation of alleged conduct
- Findings of doctrinal violation
- Imposition of ecclesiastical discipline (admonition, communion restriction, function restriction, public censure, excommunication)
- Recommendation regarding marriage dissolution within church framework
- Adjudication of custody recommendations within church tribunals (subject to civil law)
- Member status determinations
- Disclosure requirements for membership review

May not: impose civil law penalties; physically restrain or detain; compel testimony from non-members; access information beyond what members provide; impose excommunication without due process per assembly bylaws; require members to commit civil law violations; refuse mandated reporting of child abuse.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:** Operating budget approximately $180K annually (Council operations only; broader assembly budget approximately $4.2M). Staff: 2 paid (administrator, hearing coordinator); ~12 lay members serve on rotating Council. Data systems: membership records, hearing records.

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not exercise authority over non-members.
- **Action:** May not impose civil law penalties; may not require physical punishment; may not require sexual contact; may not require disclosure of confidential professional information (medical, legal, etc.).
- **Data:** May not share member records with civil authorities except per legal compulsion; may not retain disciplinary records beyond stated retention periods; may not collect information about members' civil-life activities not relevant to membership.
- **Procedural:** May not impose discipline without notice and hearing per bylaws; may not impose discipline based on protected speech outside doctrinal areas; may not impose discipline as retaliation for civil-law-protected actions (e.g., reporting abuse).
- **Other:** May not direct members' civil-law decisions (voting, civic participation, employment outside of religious-vocation contexts); may not interfere with mandated reporting.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** The Council's findings affect members' standing in marriage, family, employment, and social relationships. Excommunicated members face shunning from current members per assembly practice. This is a substantial real-world effect of nominally "religious" authority.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:** The Council commits to not using doctrinal categories as proxies for civil-rights-prohibited discrimination. Specifically: doctrinal interpretations will not be applied differentially based on race, ethnicity, national origin, or political activity outside doctrinal areas. This commitment is structurally tested by the inherent doctrinal nature of religious authority.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect threshold. The Council does not directly cause bodily harm but its decisions can produce: spousal/family violence (in some communities, discipline produces violence within families); medical harm (discipline that leads members to refuse medical care); suicide risk (in shunning cases). Threshold: any documented case of suicide following Council discipline triggers immediate review of disciplinary practices in that case.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** If members are unable to access representation in disciplinary proceedings (member has no advocate, language barrier, cognitive impairment), this triggers review.

If hearings are conducted in violation of bylaws procedural requirements, those cases are findings.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not applicable.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantive. Council decisions affect members' children, families, and descendants. Threshold: if discipline produces multi-generational membership exit (entire family lines leave the community after disciplinary actions), this triggers review of disciplinary practices.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**

If discipline is imposed on members for protected speech (whistleblowing, public criticism of the assembly, civil rights advocacy, journalism), this triggers immediate review.

If members are pressured not to speak to civil authorities about matters within Council jurisdiction (including suspected abuse), this is a finding.

If members are pressured to recant truthful statements under threat of discipline, this is a finding.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Council records of confidential pastoral matters disclosed to non-clergy without member consent triggers review.
- Membership records shared with non-assembly parties without consent triggers review.
- Confessional disclosures used as basis for disciplinary action without member consent triggers review.

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Shunning intensity threshold:* If documented shunning practices following excommunication produce specific harms (homelessness for ex-member, loss of employment caused by member-employer relationship, custody loss), each case is a finding.

*Vulnerable-member discipline threshold:* If discipline disproportionately affects elderly, disabled, or low-resource members at p<0.05, this triggers review.

*Childhood-discipline threshold:* If discipline of minors involves practices outside community-recognized norms, this triggers review.

*Failure-to-report threshold:* If suspected abuse is not reported per mandated reporter requirements, each case is a finding.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Civil litigation under tort or civil rights law**
- Activator: Any party with standing.
- Trigger: Court determination of tort (defamation, IIED), child abuse, financial fraud, or civil rights violation.
- What "stopped" means: Court orders may impose damages, enjoin specific practices, or in extreme cases dissolve corporate entity.
- Independence: Civil judiciary.
- Timeline: Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 2 — Mandated reporter / criminal law action**
- Activator: Law enforcement on credible reports of crimes (abuse, financial crimes, civil rights violations).
- Trigger: Criminal investigation.
- What "stopped" means: Specific criminal proceedings may halt practices; prosecutions may discipline individuals.
- Independence: Law enforcement is independent.
- Timeline: Per criminal procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Mass member exit**
- Activator: Members collectively withdrawing.
- Trigger: Members determining the institution has lost legitimacy.
- What "stopped" means: The assembly's continued existence depends on member participation; mass exit dissolves the institution operationally.
- Independence: Members decide individually; collective action emerges from individual decisions.
- Timeline: Variable; can be slow.

Religious institutions face a structural challenge in providing formal stop paths given First Amendment protections from civil interference in ecclesiastical matters. Stop Paths 1 and 2 address civil-law violations; Stop Path 3 addresses the institution's underlying voluntary basis. These three are the realistically available stops.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. The assembly acknowledges that members who report concerns to civil authorities may not be disciplined for that reporting; this commitment is binding.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** [x] Yes for all three paths.

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 90 days. The Council can be wound down by Board action or by assembly dissolution.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Days 1–30: Notify members; halt new disciplinary proceedings.
- Days 31–90: Resolve pending cases or transfer to successor mechanism; archive records per state law.

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:**
- Pending disciplinary cases.
- Members' ongoing relationships shaped by past disciplinary decisions.

**9.4 Data disposition:** Per state law and assembly bylaws.

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — disciplinary decisions affect members' relationships, family standing, and social position permanently. Cannot reverse social effects of past excommunications.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** The assembly could establish a successor body or operate without a formal Disciplinary Council; institutional continuity does not depend on this specific structure.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. R43 gap acknowledged.
