## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** COMMUNITY-LAND-TRUST-EXAMPLE — a single-jurisdiction community land trust holding land for permanently affordable housing.

**1.2 Functional description:** A community land trust ("CLT") holding approximately 84 parcels in [CITY, STATE], on which sit 112 residential units (a mix of single-family homes, duplexes, and one small apartment building) and 3 small commercial spaces. The CLT separates land ownership from improvement ownership: the CLT owns the land in perpetuity; residents own (or rent) the improvements through a 99-year ground lease that restricts resale price and use. Functional authority:
- Selects which households are admitted to homeownership and rental units (income-eligibility screening, application review, member vote)
- Sets and enforces ground-lease terms, including occupancy requirements, owner-occupancy mandate, resale price formula, capital improvement approval, and right of first refusal on resale
- Approves or declines proposed property modifications, additions, and uses per ground-lease standards
- Initiates lease enforcement, including notice of cure, lease termination proceedings, and (in extreme cases) eviction or forced sale at the formula price
- Sets monthly ground rent and assesses maintenance fees for common areas
- Acquires, holds, and disposes of property within its service area
- Operates a tripartite Board: residents (one-third), service-area community members (one-third), public-interest representatives (one-third — typically housing advocates, public officials acting in personal capacity, civic representatives)
- Conducts CLT-wide member meetings; members may vote on bylaws amendments, board appointments, and major property decisions

The CLT's authority is consequential because it can ultimately terminate a household's housing situation through ground-lease enforcement, can constrain a homeowner's ability to recover full market appreciation on their improvements, and can shape who lives in the neighborhood through admission decisions. These are real exercises of housing authority, and the framework treats them as such regardless of the CLT's stated mission of expanding access to affordable housing.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting housing tenure, financial obligations, property use, and household admission.

**1.4 Domain:** Voluntary Association (operating in real estate / housing space).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates as a [STATE] 501(c)(3) nonprofit corporation. Authority derives from: (a) the CLT's articles of incorporation and bylaws (adopted by member vote, amendable by member vote); (b) recorded ground leases on each parcel, which run with the land; (c) any restrictive covenants recorded against parcels at acquisition; (d) federal and state housing law (Fair Housing Act, [STATE] landlord-tenant law, [STATE] real property law, federal HOME and CDBG program requirements where applicable). The CLT is a member of the [NATIONAL CLT NETWORK], a voluntary association.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (~280 residents in CLT housing — homeowners, renters, family members)
- [x] Members (voluntary affiliation) — the CLT has ~340 voting members; ~120 are residents, ~140 are service-area community members, ~80 are non-resident supporters
- [x] Non-consenting third parties — minor children of resident households, household members other than the lease signatory, applicants on the waitlist, neighboring property owners affected by CLT decisions
- [x] Vulnerable populations: low-income and very-low-income households (admission criteria target this population), elderly residents, residents with disabilities, single-parent households, immigrant households, persons leaving homelessness or housing instability
- [x] Children (under age of majority): approximately 95 children live in CLT housing; affected by admission, lease enforcement, occupancy rules, and any displacement
- [x] Future generations: the 99-year ground lease structure binds future homebuyers and future generations of residents to the resale formula and use restrictions

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** ~280 residents in CLT housing; ~340 voting members; ~60 households on the waitlist at any given time. Total population materially affected by CLT decisions: approximately 480.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Approximately 220.

The CLT's membership is voluntary and exit is meaningful for members who do not also live in CLT housing — they may resign membership at any time without consequence. Resident members are in a different position: they affirmatively chose CLT housing through the application process and signed ground leases, but the framework's voluntary-but-stuck test (form §2.3 note) applies. Exit from CLT housing requires either selling the improvements at the formula price (which may produce less proceeds than market) and finding alternative housing in a tight rental market, or terminating tenancy. The financial and life-disruption cost of exit is substantial.

The CLT therefore declares as non-consenting:
- Children and household members other than the lease signatory (~95 children + ~25 non-signatory adult household members = ~120) — they did not consent to the lease
- Resident lease signatories whose exit options are constrained by the CLT's resale formula and the affordable housing market generally (~100) — they consented to the original terms but the framework treats high exit cost as constructive non-consent for ongoing micro-governance

Total non-consenting: approximately 220.

This is an honest disclosure. A CLT acting in good faith on its mission may still be an authority over residents whose exit is constrained. The framework's R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) applies to that population regardless of mission alignment.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes*. ~95 children in CLT households. The CLT commits to: (a) lease enforcement procedures that consider impact on minor household members; (b) priority placement of households with children when waitlist position is otherwise tied; (c) age-appropriate notification when household lease enforcement is in process; (d) coordination with school district for displacement situations to minimize school disruption.
- [ ] Patients during medical care — not applicable as a core authority surface; the CLT may interact with medically vulnerable residents but does not act upon them in a healthcare capacity.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — not applicable.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Residents in financial hardship, residents with limited employment options, and residents whose immigration status creates pressure to avoid official disputes are all in coercive economic relationships with respect to housing tenure. Mitigations: hardship payment plan available for ground rent; legal aid referral provided at any lease-enforcement notice; the CLT does not inquire into immigration status and does not share resident information with immigration authorities absent legal process.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Elderly residents, residents with disabilities, residents with limited English proficiency. Mitigations: interpreter services for member meetings and lease-enforcement proceedings; designated resident advocate available on request; bylaws and ground-lease summary translated into [LANGUAGE 1] and [LANGUAGE 2]; accessibility accommodations for meetings. R26 partial gap acknowledged for residents whose languages or accommodations fall outside current capacity.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — the CLT's defined service area within [CITY, STATE], comprising approximately [N] census tracts.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Admission and waitlist placement decisions
- Approval or denial of property modifications, additions, capital improvements
- Approval or denial of changes in occupancy or use
- Setting of ground rent (per ground-lease formula; cannot exceed bounds set in lease)
- Assessment of maintenance fees for common areas
- Notice of lease violation; required cure
- Lease termination proceedings (per [STATE] landlord-tenant law for renters; per ground-lease enforcement provisions for owners)
- Right of first refusal on resale (the CLT may purchase the improvements at the formula price)
- Forced sale at formula price under defined ground-lease default conditions
- Eviction (renters; per [STATE] law and lease) or termination of ownership and forced sale (owners; per ground-lease and [STATE] law)

The maximum sanction is loss of housing tenure. The CLT cannot levy fines beyond the lease, file general liens beyond ground rent and maintenance fees, restrict residents' speech or political activity, or impose obligations beyond the recorded ground lease and bylaws.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: ~$1.6M annually
- Staff: 4 FTE (executive director, housing manager, community organizer, finance) plus 1 part-time contractor
- Real property: 84 parcels with 112 residential units and 3 commercial spaces
- Capital reserves: ~$420K stewardship fund + ~$180K acquisition fund

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not extend authority outside the defined service area without member vote to expand bylaws.
- **Action:** May not enforce restrictions that violate fair housing law; may not enforce ground-lease provisions in ways that violate [STATE] landlord-tenant or eviction law; may not initiate enforcement during pending good-faith dispute without notice and opportunity to cure; may not foreclose or force sale without statutory notice and judicial process where required.
- **Data:** May not collect or retain resident or applicant information beyond what admission, lease administration, and lawful reporting require; may not share resident information outside the CLT absent consent or legal process; may not inquire into immigration status; may not share information with immigration enforcement absent legal process.
- **Procedural:** May not impose lease enforcement without bylaws-required notice, hearing, and appeal; may not deny admission on grounds not in the published admission criteria; Board members must recuse from decisions affecting their own household, immediate family, or business interests.
- **Other:** May not adopt rules or admission criteria that discriminate on protected characteristics; may not amend ground-lease terms for existing residents without their affirmative consent; may not amend bylaws in ways that bind existing residents to obligations not in effect at the time of their tenancy without their affirmative consent.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** The CLT's existence and practices modestly influence local housing market dynamics, neighborhood composition, and local affordable-housing policy. Member meetings and CLT participation in public policy processes reach local officials. R58 acknowledged at small scale.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The CLT commits to not using:
- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin, family status:* Admission criteria are defined and published; admission decisions are documented with reasons. Annual disparate-impact testing of admissions, lease enforcement, and waitlist movement is committed; results published. R66/R74.
- *Proxy for political activity in lease enforcement:* The CLT does not enforce lease provisions differentially based on residents' speech, advocacy, or organizing — including organizing critical of CLT practices. Enforcement triggered within 90 days of a resident's public dissent is reviewed by an outside reviewer drawn from [NATIONAL CLT NETWORK].
- *Proxy for personal animus:* Board members and staff must recuse from any matter involving conflicts; documented reasons for enforcement decisions; appeal available to full Board for any contested enforcement.
- *Proxy for immigration status:* The CLT does not inquire into immigration status. Admission, lease, and enforcement decisions may not use citizenship status, language, or perceived national origin as basis or proxy.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect. The CLT's authority is housing-tenure and financial, not direct physical action. However: lease enforcement leading to housing loss has documented bodily-harm consequences for vulnerable residents (medically vulnerable, elderly, families with young children). Threshold: any displacement of a household with documented medical, disability, or child-welfare circumstances without exhaustion of alternatives (payment plan, transitional support, referral) triggers individual review.

Habitability conditions in CLT-owned units: any [STATE] habitability code violation, any failure to repair life-safety conditions within statutory timeframes, triggers review.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any resident is unable to participate in member meetings or enforcement proceedings due to lack of accommodation (accessibility, language, scheduling, childcare) and the accommodation was requested in advance
- Any lease enforcement occurs without bylaws-required notice, hearing, or appeal
- Voting on bylaws or major decisions is conducted in a manner that fails to accommodate members
- Information needed for an informed member vote (financial reports, proposal text, comparative data) is withheld

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Limited but real. CLT-managed properties must comply with [STATE] environmental and habitability standards. Lead, asbestos, mold, and other environmental hazards trigger immediate remediation review.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial. This is a load-bearing section.

The CLT's 99-year ground-lease structure binds future generations of residents to the resale formula and use restrictions. This is the structural feature that makes the CLT a CLT — without intergenerational binding, permanently affordable housing dissolves into market housing. The framework treats this honestly.

R28 (Expiration Default) establishes that all authority expires absent affirmative renewal. The 99-year ground lease is intentionally long-duration and is not subject to easy renewal in the framework's sense. The CLT acknowledges this tension and discloses:
- Ground-lease terms can be amended only with affirmative consent of the affected resident (existing residents) or by member vote applying to future leases (prospective)
- The resale formula is published and amendable by member vote per a defined process
- Bylaws are subject to member vote and a 5-year framework renewal cycle
- The CLT itself can be dissolved by member supermajority vote, with land disposed per bylaws (typically transferred to another CLT or community land entity)

Threshold: any proposed amendment to ground-lease terms, resale formula, or use restrictions affecting more than 5 residents in a 12-month period triggers public notice, member-meeting deliberation, and external review by [NATIONAL CLT NETWORK] or peer CLT before adoption.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any resident is subject to lease enforcement, admission denial, or waitlist movement on the basis of speech about CLT practices, organizing, or advocacy
- The CLT restricts residents' use of personal communication channels regarding CLT matters
- Information needed for member governance is withheld from members

The CLT explicitly commits that residents may speak publicly about their experience, including critically, without retaliation.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Resident or applicant personal information shared outside the CLT without consent or legal process — finding
- Records retained beyond the CLT's stated retention schedule (admission records 7 years; lease records per [STATE] real property law; financial records per tax law) — finding
- Information shared with immigration enforcement absent legal process — finding
- Surveillance of residents beyond what property management requires — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Disparate admission threshold:* If admission outcomes correlate with race, ethnicity, family status, disability, or national origin at p<0.05 over rolling 24-month periods, this triggers review. Waitlist movement reviewed on the same basis.

*Disparate enforcement threshold:* If lease enforcement actions correlate with protected characteristics or with resident advocacy activity, this triggers review.

*Displacement threshold:* If aggregate displacement (lease termination, forced sale, voluntary departure under enforcement pressure) exceeds 3% of resident households per year, or if any year produces displacement clusters in particular households (immigrant, disabled, elderly, single-parent), this triggers systemic review.

*Capture-by-Board-tenure threshold:* If the same individuals occupy Board seats beyond the bylaws' term limits, or if any constituency (resident, community, public-interest) loses its one-third representation, this triggers review.

*Funder capture threshold:* If any single funding source exceeds 33% of operating support over a 12-month period, this triggers review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Member action**
- *Activator:* Any member may petition for special meeting (signatures from 25% of voting members); any member may propose motions at regular meetings; the tripartite Board may be recalled by member vote per bylaws.
- *Trigger:* Member determination of bylaws violation, governance failure, or structural concern.
- *What "stopped" means:* Member meeting may suspend specific operations, recall Board members, void decisions, amend bylaws, or in supermajority dissolve the CLT.
- *Independence:* Members are independent of Board (Board exists at members' direction). The tripartite structure ensures no constituency holds majority control.
- *Timeline:* Per bylaws — typically 30 days from petition to meeting.

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil litigation under [STATE] landlord-tenant, real property, fair housing, and nonprofit law**
- *Activator:* Any resident, applicant, member, or other party with standing.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of violation of bylaws, ground lease, [STATE] law, or federal fair housing law.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders may halt practices, void actions, impose damages, or in extreme cases trigger bylaws or governance changes.
- *Independence:* Courts independent.
- *Timeline:* Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Regulatory action**
- *Activator:* HUD or state housing authority (where federal or state funding is involved); [STATE] Attorney General (charitable trust oversight); [STATE] fair housing enforcement; [STATE] consumer protection where applicable.
- *Trigger:* Regulatory determination of violation.
- *What "stopped" means:* Funding conditions imposed; corrective orders; in extreme cases withdrawal of funding or charitable status.
- *Independence:* State and federal agencies independent.
- *Timeline:* Per agency procedures.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. The CLT commits in bylaws and ground leases that no resident, member, or applicant may face retaliation for filing complaints, civil suits, or regulatory complaints.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 36 months. Justification: dissolution requires member supermajority vote, transfer of 84 parcels and ground leases to successor entity, settlement of resident equity per resale formula, settlement of lender obligations, regulatory wind-down, and final accounting.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Months 1–6: Member vote to dissolve; transition planning; identification of successor entity (typically another CLT, regional land trust, municipal housing authority, or community housing entity); notice to residents, lenders, regulators, funders
- Months 7–18: Negotiation and execution of property and ground-lease transfer; resident assurance regarding continuity of tenure under successor; lender consent and refinancing where required
- Months 19–30: Asset transfer; staff transition; regulatory and tax wind-down
- Months 31–36: Final accounting, residual asset disposition (per bylaws — typically to successor or to community housing trust fund), entity dissolution

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:**
- 84 recorded ground leases run with the land and must be honored by successor
- 280 residents' housing tenure depends on continuity through dissolution
- Lender obligations and refinancing requirements
- HUD or state funding compliance obligations
- Regulatory and tax obligations
- Community trust built over years cannot be transferred mechanically

These are real and material at this scale. The CLT is structurally entrenched in the lives of its residents in ways that any dismantling must address; this is the structural feature that makes long-duration housing tenure work, and the framework treats it as such.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Application records purged 7 years post-decision per [STATE] law except where ongoing relationships require retention; lease and financial records transferred to successor or archived per [STATE] real property law
- *Institutional records:* Bylaws, meeting minutes, audit reports archived; transferred to successor or community archive
- *Derived outputs:* None of structural significance

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — past admission decisions, lease enforcement, displacements, and admission denials cannot be reversed. The CLT acknowledges this and commits to: (a) preserving records sufficient for affected former residents and applicants to seek review or remediation; (b) supporting expungement or correction of any erroneous records; (c) providing references and documentation in good faith to former residents and applicants.

The CLT's primary downstream effects are positive (long-term affordable housing tenure for resident households) but include real adverse effects (displaced households, denied applicants, residents whose appreciation was constrained by the resale formula in ways they may have come to regret). R63 applies fully; the framework does not exempt mission-aligned authorities from reversibility duties.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Yes. The CLT's housing function can be replaced through transfer to another CLT, regional land trust, municipal housing authority, or community housing entity. Residents would experience continuity of tenure under a successor with equivalent ground-lease commitments. R51 met at this scale.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal dismantling rehearsal. The CLT has discussed succession scenarios at Board level but has not conducted a desktop exercise. R43 gap acknowledged; rehearsal committed within 24 months of registration. At a CLT's scale and with binding ground leases, R43 has particular weight — succession planning is the literal expression of reversibility for permanently-bound assets.
