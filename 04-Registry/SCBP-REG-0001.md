## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** Bounded Power Foundation — Public Registry and Framework

**1.2 Functional description:** The Foundation maintains a public registry of authority systems, publishes framework documents defining structural constraints on authority, receives and logs stop trigger submissions, and notifies registered authorities of triggers filed against them.

The Foundation cannot compel, fine, suspend, or terminate any authority. It cannot enforce compliance. It cannot take any coercive action. All outputs are informational and public record only. The Foundation's outputs operate through R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition) — its records and findings shape decisions made by others, including stakeholders evaluating registered authorities, civic actors filing triggers, and institutions considering registration.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Indirect. The Foundation issues no binding directives. It operates as a structured visibility instrument — its outputs influence decisions made by others without itself making binding determinations about any registered authority.

**1.4 Domain:** Information / Governance Infrastructure.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** The Foundation operates as a self-organized voluntary entity, not incorporated under any specific statute. Its authority derives from voluntary participation by registrants, public hosting infrastructure (GitHub), and the framework documents it maintains. It claims no governmental authority and no statutory privilege. It exists at the discretion of its operator and the willingness of registrants to participate.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] General public (anyone who accesses the registry voluntarily)

The Foundation does not act upon any other party. All interaction is voluntary: authorities register voluntarily; the public reads voluntarily; civic triggers are filed voluntarily.

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:**

- *Acted upon (coercive sense):* Zero. The Foundation has no coercive ceiling and cannot compel anyone.
- *Affected through indirect influence (R58 sense):* The Foundation's framework documents, Mechanism Records, and civic-trigger logs are read by an open population — anyone with internet access — and shape decisions made by readers who use them. The Foundation cannot estimate this population precisely. Categories of readers known to engage with the construction include: civic actors who have used or referenced the registry, registered authorities, scholars of governance and accountability, civil society organizations, technologists familiar with public-record infrastructure, and journalists. These are the same categories §11.4 names as the population from which a renewal review would be drawn.

The two counts above are intentionally separated. The acted-upon count is zero because the Foundation has no coercive surface. The indirect-influence count is positive but indeterminate, and is the population the framework's R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition) treats as affected. Renewal review draws from the second category.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Zero.

The Foundation has no coercive ceiling. It cannot compel anyone to do anything. Reading the framework, registering an authority, filing a stop trigger — all are voluntary acts. There is no population subject to the Foundation's authority without consent because the Foundation has no authority to be subject to.

*Note on voluntary-but-stuck populations:* The Foundation acknowledges the framework's R26 emphasis on populations who are technically voluntary but structurally cannot exit. This pattern does not apply to the Foundation. There is no network-effect lock-in; alternative information sources exist; non-participation has no cost.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [ ] All categories not applicable. The Foundation does not act upon any vulnerable population. Vulnerable populations are *served* by the Foundation's existence (because the framework's structural-honesty principle is meant to surface authority's behavior toward them) but they are not *subject to* the Foundation.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:**
- [x] Global, with acknowledgment of jurisdictional limits.

The Foundation's records are accessible globally. Authority registrations are not restricted by jurisdiction. Stop triggers may be filed from anywhere.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:** None. The Foundation has no coercive authority. It cannot compel, penalize, suspend, or terminate any other authority. Its sole output is structured public information.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: ~$0 (no external funding accepted)
- Staffing: operational matter; not a constitutional commitment
- Data systems: GitHub repository (registry, framework documents); ProtonMail (communications); no proprietary systems

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** None — global reach by design
- **Action:** May not charge fees for registration or stop trigger filing; may not suppress, edit, or remove any submitted record after public posting; may not accept payment from any authority it registers; may not grant exemptions from the framework; may not make binding determinations of compliance or non-compliance; may not be transferred to any authority it governs (per Charter §3 — public-domain-content + non-transferable-instance distinction)
- **Data:** May not retain private data beyond what submitters voluntarily include; may not share submitter contact information beyond registry follow-up
- **Procedural:** May not require submitter identification for stop trigger filing (anonymous filing permitted per SCBP-08); may not delay registration for substantive review (registry is custodial, not evaluative)
- **Other:** May not commercialize the registry; may not enter into any arrangement that would compromise the registry's neutrality with respect to any registered authority

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** The Foundation's outputs that materially influence decisions made by others:
- Framework documents (read and applied by authorities, civic actors, scholars)
- Mechanism Records (read by stakeholders evaluating registered authorities)
- Stop trigger logs (read by stakeholders, civic actors, registered authorities)
- Aggregate review findings (when applicable)

For each: the institutional or individual reader makes the actual decision. The Foundation's output substantially shapes the decision through structured visibility.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:** The Foundation commits to not using:
- *Proxy for political alignment:* Will not differentially process registrations or stop triggers based on the registrant's or filer's political alignment
- *Proxy for institutional preference:* Will not preferentially treat governmental authorities over private authorities, or vice versa
- *Proxy for size:* Will not preferentially treat large authorities over small authorities, or vice versa
- *Proxy for financial relationship:* The Foundation does not accept payment from any registered authority and will not allow informal financial relationships to influence registry treatment

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Not applicable. The Foundation produces records and documents; it has no physical interaction with persons.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** If submitters are unable to file registrations or stop triggers due to Foundation unavailability for >7 consecutive days, this triggers review.

If Foundation processes are unclear or inaccessible to good-faith submitters (e.g., language barriers, technical complexity), this triggers review of access mechanisms.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not applicable.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Not applicable. The Foundation's records persist (which is structurally desirable for visibility purposes), but the Foundation does not bind future generations to any obligations.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** This is one of the Foundation's primary harm categories.

If the Foundation suppresses, edits, or removes any submitted record without a logged public explanation, this is a finding.

If the Foundation fails to log a registration, stop trigger, or update within 7 days of submission, the delay is itself a finding.

If the Foundation differentially processes submissions based on the political content, viewpoint, or identity of the submitter, this is a finding.

If the Foundation excludes submitters in good-faith retaliation or out of personal disagreement, this is a finding.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- If submitter contact information is shared beyond registry follow-up purposes, this is a finding
- If submitter identity is disclosed when anonymous filing was elected, this is a finding
- If records are altered post-publication without logged public explanation, this is a finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Capture threshold:* If any single funding source exceeds 20% of operating costs, this triggers an independent review of registry independence. (Currently zero external funding; threshold is structurally not approachable until and unless funding is accepted.)

*Conflict threshold:* If any registry operator holds a formal role in any registered authority, that operator must recuse from all decisions involving that authority's record. Failure to recuse is a finding.

*Suppression threshold:* If any submission is removed, edited, or withheld from public view without a logged public explanation, the registry enters provisional review.

*Non-response threshold:* If the registry fails to log a submitted record within 7 days, the delay is itself logged as a finding.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Public stop trigger**
- *Who can activate:* Any person may file a stop trigger against the Foundation using the standard stop trigger form
- *Trigger condition:* Discretionary or threshold-based per SCBP-08
- *What "stopped" means:* Foundation must respond publicly within 30 days; failure to respond is itself a public finding
- *Independence justification:* Stop triggers come from outside the Foundation; the operator cannot prevent filing
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* 30-day response window per Foundation declaration; aggregation review per SCBP-08 if 5 or more triggers within 90 days

**STOP PATH 2 — Constitutional non-compliance finding**
- *Who can activate:* Any affected party may initiate a Testing Protocol evaluation
- *Trigger condition:* Reasonable belief of constitutional non-compliance
- *What "stopped" means:* The Foundation must comply with the evaluation; cannot block or delay
- *Independence justification:* Testing Protocol is the framework's diagnostic layer, structurally separate from any single authority's preferences
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* Per SCBP-05 procedures

**STOP PATH 3 — Expiration default**
- *Who can activate:* Time
- *Trigger condition:* Authorization expires 2028-05-05
- *What "stopped" means:* Foundation ceases operation automatically if renewal evidence is not submitted and independently reviewed before expiration
- *Independence justification:* Expiration is automatic, not subject to operator discretion
- *Post-stop review process and timeline:* Pending review does not authorize continued operation per R57

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. No person may be penalized for filing a stop trigger against the registry, initiating a Testing Protocol evaluation, or any other good-faith stop activation.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** [x] Yes for all three paths. Public stop triggers cannot be blocked by the Foundation. Testing Protocol evaluations are framework-level. Expiration is automatic.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged. The Foundation acknowledges that any party affected by it may file a civic trigger (Tripwire, Challenge, or contribute to Aggregate) per SCBP-08, and commits to publicly responding within 30 days.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):** None.

The Foundation has no operational contractors or extensions acting under its authority. All registry functions are performed directly by the Foundation; volunteer contributors operate independently rather than under the Foundation's direction.

**6.2 Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with structural relationship):** None.

The Foundation has no authorities with structural relationships in the Type 2 sense. This is a legitimate "no Type 2" disclosure for a fully-independent voluntary infrastructure.

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure compromising stop paths):**

- *GitHub:* Repository hosting. If GitHub removes or restricts the repository, all records must be restored via mirrors within 30 days. **Stop path compromise:** if GitHub becomes unavailable and no mirror exists, stop triggers cannot be filed via the standard channel until alternative hosting is established.

- *ProtonMail:* Communications channel. If email access is lost, an alternative contact must be established and posted within 7 days. **Stop path compromise:** stop triggers can still be filed via GitHub even if email fails; this dependency is bounded.

**Mirror requirement:** Registry data must be maintained in at least one independent mirror at all times. Mirror locations are documented in MIRRORS.md. *Currently: zero mirrors. Commitment to establish at least one mirror by 2026-08-03.* This gap is itself a structural finding acknowledged at registration.

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

- *Operator-availability risk:* The Foundation's responsiveness depends on available operator capacity. If operator capacity is interrupted, the Foundation's responsiveness is compromised. The Charter's pause-on-unavailability provision (Charter §7) addresses this structurally — the registry pauses rather than transferring to a successor.
- *No external funding:* The Foundation has no funding capture risk because it has no external funding. The 20% capture threshold (§4.7) is structurally not approachable until funding is accepted, at which point it becomes operative.
- *Hosting platform capture:* Dependence on GitHub creates a capture risk if GitHub's policies change. Mitigation: mirror commitment (above); content is public domain and forkable.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**
- GitHub repository — primary record storage; if GitHub removes the repository, records must be restored from mirrors
- Records are append-only by design (Charter §6), so records themselves cannot be reversed in any case — they accumulate

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:** None. The Foundation does not coordinate with other authorities in ways that constitute R55/R59 functional consolidation.

**6.7 Disclosure completeness assertion:** [x] Asserted.

**6.8 External authority operationalization (R72):** None.

The Foundation does not operationalize any external authority's decisions within its own operations. Its outputs are entirely produced by its own processes (registration intake, civic trigger logging, framework document publication).

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 30 days. The Foundation is small enough to wind down in a single month.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Days 1–7: Notice posted publicly that authorization is expiring or being voluntarily terminated
- Days 8–14: All records archived in publicly downloadable format
- Days 15–21: Mirrors notified and given access to final archive
- Days 22–30: Final operations cease; archive remains accessible

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Minimal. The Foundation has no employees, no contracts, no facilities. The primary entrenchment is reputational — registered authorities and stakeholders rely on the registry's continued availability. Mitigation: append-only records ensure that even on dissolution, the historical record persists.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data (submitter contact information):* Retained permanently per the public-record nature of submissions, with anonymous filings remaining anonymous
- *Institutional records:* Archived publicly
- *Derived outputs:* The Foundation produces no derived outputs in the R60 sense
- *Audit logs:* Archived publicly; retained permanently

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — Mechanism Records propagate to readers, including civic actors who may have based decisions on them. The records cannot be reversed (append-only by design). Mitigation: dissolution notice ensures readers know records are no longer current; archive ensures historical context remains.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** The Foundation's dissolution would not create a regulatory vacuum (the Foundation does not regulate). Its records and framework documents are public domain and forkable; any successor entity can establish a new instance using the same framework. The non-transferable-instance principle (Charter §3) means the *specific* Foundation instance cannot transfer, but the *content* persists openly.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. R43 gap acknowledged. Remediation: desktop simulation within 18 months of registration.

**10.6 Necessity-decay acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged. The Foundation commits to demonstrating reduction in scope, dependency, opacity, persistence, or downstream impact at each renewal, unless conditions independently require continuation. **10.7 Aggregate trigger threshold:** 5 triggers in 90 days. SCBP-09 §II places non-consenting populations under 1,000 in the floor band (5-25). The Foundation selects 5 — the floor — because as the framework's own first registered entity, it should hold itself to the most accessible threshold for civic concern.

**10.8 Aggregate threshold justification:** The Foundation's structural-honesty principle requires that it apply the same standards to itself it applies to other authorities, and that it treat civic concerns about its own operation as low-noise, high-signal.

> **Self-applies-to-itself note on §10.7:** The bad-faith counter-example ([Bad-Faith-Consulting](../03-Examples/Bad-Faith-Consulting.md)) identifies §10.7 as the form's most exploitable leak: an authority can declare zero non-consenting population (§2.3) and then set the aggregate threshold at the high end of what SCBP-09 will accept, making aggregate review structurally unreachable. The Foundation's §2.3 also declares zero non-consenting population, and the Foundation has selected its threshold (5 in 90 days) by good-faith assertion rather than by structural compulsion. Structurally, the Foundation's choice of 5 is indistinguishable from a hypothetical bad-faith Foundation's choice of 1,000 — the form's bounds permit either, and the same justifying paragraph would frame either selection. The Foundation flags this parallel deliberately. The framework's premise is that some leaks can only close through registrant honesty; the Foundation, as the framework's first registered entity, is exposed to that limit before any other authority. This declaration does not close the leak — it makes the leak visible at the registration most likely to be read.

**10.9 Response time window:** 30 days.
