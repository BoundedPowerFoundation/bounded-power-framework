# SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Cuyahoga County Corrections Center (CCCC), operated by the Cuyahoga County Sheriff's Office.

**1.2 — Functional description:**
The authority operates a pre-trial and short-sentence jail facility in downtown Cleveland, Ohio (Jail I and Jail II at 1215 W 3rd Street), with capacity for approximately 1,436 individuals at a time and intake of approximately 26,000 individuals annually. The authority can: receive and book individuals delivered into custody on arrest warrants, court orders, or law enforcement booking; restrict the physical liberty and movement of detained individuals; control detained individuals' access to communication (mail, phone, video visitation), to medical care, to legal counsel, and to family; apply use of force and physical restraint per declared protocols; conduct intake screening including medical and mental health screening; transfer detained individuals to medical facilities for treatment; release individuals upon court order, posting of bond, or completion of sentence; and house federal detainees and detainees from other jurisdictions under contract. The authority does not adjudicate guilt, set bond, or determine length of detention — those decisions are made by upstream courts.

# SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Court order**
- Who can activate: Federal courts (Northern District of Ohio), Cuyahoga County Common Pleas Court, Cuyahoga County Municipal Court. Notable activation: March 2024 Clay v. Cuyahoga County federal class-action settlement requiring inspections and monitors.
- Trigger condition: Successful civil rights litigation under 42 U.S.C. § 1983; class-action settlement; habeas corpus order; injunctive order on specific practices.
- What "stopped" means: Specific practices enjoined; specific conditions ordered remediated; in extreme cases, individual release ordered. Court orders to date have ordered conditions reform and oversight; they have not closed the facility.
- Independence justification: Federal and state courts are structurally independent of the Sheriff's Office and the County Executive. The 2024 Clay settlement includes independent monitors of medical (MetroHealth) and food (Trinity Services Group) contracts, with public reports.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Class-action settlement specifies 180-day inspection cycles with public reporting; specific injunctive orders carry their own timelines.

**STOP PATH 2 — Ohio Department of Rehabilitation and Correction, Bureau of Adult Detention**
- Who can activate: ODRC Bureau of Adult Detention inspectors, following mandatory 24-hour death-reporting and standard-violation review under Ohio Administrative Code 5120:1-8.
- Trigger condition: Death in custody, reported violations of Ohio Minimum Jail Standards, or routine inspection findings.
- What "stopped" means: Issuance of corrective action plan with required remediation steps; in theory, decertification or order to reduce population, though such actions have not occurred for CCCC despite repeated findings. The state has placed CCCC on corrective action plans repeatedly, including April 2024 following the Williams death and 2025 following the Papp death.
- Independence justification: ODRC is a state agency structurally separate from Cuyahoga County government. ANNOTATION: Practical independence is partially limited — Ohio law does not currently authorize the state to close a failing county jail; a 2020 bill by state Sen. Antonio and Rep. Crossman to provide this authority died in committee.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: ODRC corrective action plans specify required remediation; no statutory hard deadline for the jail to come into compliance is publicly evident in the Bureau's process.

**STOP PATH 3 — U.S. Marshals Service inspection (for federal detainees)**
- Who can activate: U.S. Marshals Service, which contracts with the facility to house federal detainees.
- Trigger condition: Inspection findings of unfit conditions for federal detainees. The 2018 U.S. Marshals investigation was the most consequential public finding to date.
- What "stopped" means: U.S. Marshals can remove federal detainees from the facility and decline to use it for future federal detentions. This was actively considered after the 2018 report.
- Independence justification: U.S. Marshals Service is a federal agency, structurally independent of the County Sheriff and County Executive.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: USMS inspection cycles are not publicly fixed; the 2018 inspection followed a series of in-custody deaths.

**5.4 — Penalty for activating stop:**
[X] Confirmed — no formal penalty applies to outside parties activating these stop paths. ANNOTATION: Detainees who file grievances or pursue litigation while in custody face the structural risk that complaints are processed by the authority's own grievance system. R15 (Stop Friction Prohibition) and R33 (No Penalty for Good-Faith Challenge) compliance at the detainee level is not publicly attested.

**5.5 — Stop-path independence verification:**
[X] Yes — explanation: Federal courts, state ODRC, and the U.S. Marshals Service can act on findings without the authority's cooperation. ANNOTATION: However, public reporting documents instances where the authority did not provide body camera footage to ODRC inspectors reviewing the Wade and Papp deaths, materially reducing the inspectors' ability to make findings. This functions as friction on stop-path 2 even if not a formal penalty.

**5.6 — Civic trigger acknowledgment:**
