> **EDITORIAL NOTE — high-existing-disclosure example.**
>
> Public university boards of trustees in the United States operate within unusually thick disclosure infrastructure: state open-meetings ("sunshine") laws requiring advance notice and public attendance at board meetings; state public-records laws requiring disclosure of board materials; accreditation reporting requirements through bodies such as the Higher Learning Commission or the Southern Association of Colleges and Schools; federal Title IV reporting; IPEDS data submissions; financial audits required by state law; and substantial press attention at flagship state institutions. Most board agendas, meeting minutes, votes, and budget documents are already published as a matter of state law.
>
> The question this example tests: against a disclosure regime this developed, does the registration form's structure surface anything not currently public? The answer below is "yes, in a small but concentrated set of areas" — and the answer is informed by recent litigation (notably the 2024-2025 Pennsylvania Sunshine Act dispute over Penn State's board practices) suggesting that even within thick disclosure regimes, structural transparency gaps survive.
>
> This example is illustrative of the type, not a portrait of any particular institution. Population counts, governance structures, and disclosure practices reflect what an honest application of the form to a flagship-state-university board would plausibly disclose, drawn from publicly known information about how authorities of this type operate.

## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** PUBLIC-UNIVERSITY-TRUSTEES-EXAMPLE — the Board of Trustees of a flagship state research university.

**1.2 Functional description:** A board of trustees governing a state research university with approximately 50,000 students across one main campus and four branch campuses, approximately 7,000 faculty and 16,000 staff, and an annual operating budget of approximately $4.2 billion. Functional authority:

- Hires and removes the university president; approves senior administrative appointments
- Approves the annual operating budget, capital budget, and tuition rates
- Sets and amends university bylaws and major policies
- Approves academic program creation, modification, and discontinuation
- Approves real estate transactions, major construction, and debt issuance
- Approves collective bargaining agreements and senior compensation packages
- Receives and acts on disciplinary matters elevated from administrative review (faculty dismissals; presidential search and removal; significant Title IX matters reviewed at board level)
- Receives accreditation findings and oversees institutional response
- Holds fiduciary authority over the university endowment (~$3.8 billion)
- Acts as the legal entity in litigation, contracts, and dealings with state government
- Exercises ultimate governance authority over branch campuses, including the authority to consolidate, restructure, or close them

The board's authority is consequential because: tuition decisions affect access for ~50,000 current students and applicants; campus consolidation or closure decisions affect ~10,000 students and ~3,000 employees per affected campus; presidential selection shapes institutional direction for years; faculty dismissal decisions are career-determining; and disciplinary outcomes for students can affect lifetime educational and employment trajectories.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting employment, education access, financial obligations, and institutional governance.

**1.4 Domain:** Government (operating in Education, with significant Economic and Cultural cross-domain effect).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** [STATE] enabling statute establishing the university as a [STATE] state-related or state-owned institution; [STATE] open-meetings law (Sunshine Act or equivalent); [STATE] right-to-know or public-records law; trustee composition specified in enabling statute (mix of governor-appointed, alumni-elected, and ex officio members); accreditation through [REGIONAL ACCREDITOR]; federal Higher Education Act compliance; Title IX; FERPA. Trustees serve under fiduciary duty to manage the institution in the public interest.

**2.1 Affected party categories:**

- [x] Residents of geographic area (campus-host communities; ~28,000 residents in primary host municipality)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties (host-community residents; family members of students and employees; vendors; surrounding-area businesses)
- [x] Vulnerable populations: first-generation college students; students with disabilities; international students (immigration-status-dependent); students from low-income households; minor-age students (younger high-achievement applicants); employees in non-tenure tracks; community members displaced by university expansion
- [x] Children (minors enrolled; children of student-parents and employees; minors using university facilities)

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** ~95,000 directly through enrollment, employment, or host-community residency. Indirectly: ~700,000 alumni; substantially more through research outputs, healthcare services where the university operates a hospital system, and economic spillover.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:**
- *Voluntary participants:* Students choose to enroll; employees choose to work for the institution.

Family members of students and employees subject to institutional decisions affecting their relatives (e.g., a campus closure decision affecting employed parents) are non-consenting. Vendors in concentrated relationships are partially consenting but operationally dependent.

R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) applies to the host-community residents most directly. The form surfaces this population as a category of structural concern that the existing disclosure regime — which centers on board transparency rather than affected-population mapping — does not foreground.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes*. Minor-age students (high school dual enrollment, summer programs, athletes recruited at high-school age); children of employees in campus childcare or housing.
- [x] Patients during medical care — *yes, where applicable*. Where the university operates a hospital system, the patient population is subject to hospital-system authority that is structurally connected to board governance.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — not applicable.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Students whose enrollment is dependent on continued financial aid; employees in concentrated labor markets where the university is a dominant employer; renters in housing markets where university expansion has constrained supply.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. International students with limited English; students experiencing mental health crises; employees in non-tenure roles facing disciplinary processes; first-generation students unfamiliar with administrative procedures.

> *Diagnostic observation on §2:* The form's incremental yield in this section is concentrated in the host-community population. State open-meetings laws disclose what the board does; they do not surface the population subject to its authority who never consented. The recent Penn State / Spotlight PA litigation surfaced part of this gap: even with thorough sunshine-law compliance, the *people* subject to board decisions (in that case, residents of the seven campuses being closed) were less visible than the *board's procedures* for making those decisions.

- Endowment investment policies (climate, weapons, particular companies) influence other institutional investors

- Branch campus consolidation decisions shape regional economic conditions in affected communities

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:**
- *Sexual misconduct:* Title IX outcomes that systematically fail to protect complainants or that retaliate against reporters trigger review. Aggregate Title IX dismissal rates, retention rates of complainants in their programs, and adjudication-time benchmarks are tracked.
- *Campus safety:* Crime statistics, particularly sexual assault and stalking; campus police use-of-force incidents; mental health crisis response outcomes.
- *Healthcare:* Where the university operates a hospital system, patient-safety thresholds carry from the hospital system's separate disclosure framework into board-level oversight responsibility.
- *Athletics:* Concussion protocols and traumatic brain injury rates in high-contact sports; aggregate injury rates relative to peer benchmarks.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:**
- *Faculty dismissal:* Faculty dismissed for protected speech (academic freedom violations) trigger review.
- *Student discipline:* Disciplinary outcomes that lack due process; aggregate disparate impact in disciplinary decisions by race, gender, or socioeconomic status; expulsion outcomes affecting visa status for international students.
- *Whistleblower retaliation:* Employees who report institutional misconduct facing employment consequences trigger review.
- *Campus-community impairment:* Decisions that materially affect housing, public services, or economic conditions in host communities without consultation.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:**
- Energy use, emissions, and climate commitments relative to declared targets
- Land use and habitat impact of physical-plant expansion
- Lab waste, animal research, and research infrastructure environmental impact

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial.

- *Student debt:* Tuition and financial-aid decisions bind students to debt that affects life trajectory for decades. Aggregate student debt-load relative to graduate earning outcomes is tracked; cohort-specific outcomes for low-income graduates are tracked.
- *Pension obligations:* Decisions about employment terms create long-term pension liabilities affecting future budget flexibility for decades.
- *Endowment decisions:* Investment policies and spending-rate decisions affect intergenerational equity between current and future students.
- *Capital projects:* Long-lived infrastructure investments commit future operating budgets to maintenance and debt service.
- *Land transactions:* University landholdings, once committed to particular uses, are difficult to reverse.

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) applies to student debt outcomes especially. The board cannot reverse the debt loads of past graduates; it can only support the mechanisms by which graduates can seek relief (loan forgiveness programs, employment-pathway support, academic-quality investments improving graduate earnings).

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**

Public university boards operate at the intersection of state authority and academic freedom. The relevant thresholds:
- Suppression of faculty research, teaching, or speech on grounds other than academic-quality review by peers
- Restriction of student speech, publication, or assembly beyond legally-permissible bounds
- Pressure on the student press, including through funding, publication-channel control, or disciplinary action against student journalists
- Punishment of employees who disclose institutional information of public concern through legitimate channels
- Restriction of public records access through technical or procedural means inconsistent with state law

R66 (Anti-Proxy) applies particularly to disciplinary actions framed in non-speech terms (resource use, time-place-manner) that effectively suppress speech.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Student records (FERPA-protected) released outside FERPA's permitted exceptions trigger finding
- Personnel records released or withheld inconsistently with [STATE] public-records law
- Research data treated inconsistently with research-ethics protocols
- Records sealed beyond legally-permissible windows
- Public-records denials that do not survive [STATE] open-records office review

The recent Penn State / Spotlight PA litigation illustrates the threshold operationally: Court-ordered release of records following denial is itself a finding under this category.

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Open-meetings compliance threshold:* Use of executive session, closed conference sessions, or "training" sessions to avoid public deliberation triggers review. The threshold is not zero closed sessions (some are statutorily permitted) but pattern of misuse.

*Branch-campus closure threshold:* Decisions to close, consolidate, or restructure branch campuses materially affect the host community and student/employee population. The board commits to public disclosure of consolidation analyses, host-community engagement processes, and student/employee transition support.

*Donor-influence threshold:* If a single donor's continued giving has become operationally necessary to maintain a defined function (a department, a building maintenance, a faculty line), this triggers review of operational dependence per R03.

*Athletic-program independence threshold:* If athletic-program decisions consistently override or conflict with academic-mission decisions (admission standards for athletes, faculty employment in athletic-adjacent programs, schedule conflicts with academic calendar), this triggers review.

*Title IX systemic threshold:* If aggregate Title IX outcomes show patterns indicating systemic failure (dismissal rates, complainant retention, adjudicator independence) exceeding peer-institution benchmarks, this triggers review.

**4.8 Threshold change-log commitment:** [x] Yes — committed.

> *Diagnostic observation on §4:* The form's incremental yield in this section is moderate. State open-meetings laws disclose what the board does; accreditation reports discuss institutional outcomes; financial audits cover budget integrity. None of these regimes catalogue *harm thresholds* in the framework's threshold-language sense. The form forces explicit measurable conditions in places where existing disclosure regimes use narrative discussion. The Title IX threshold and the donor-influence threshold are particularly form-specific yields.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — State legislative action**
- *Activator:* [STATE] legislature
- *Trigger:* Statutory amendment to enabling statute; appropriation conditioning; investigative committee findings
- *What "stopped" means:* Range from technical statutory amendment through structural reorganization to repeal of enabling statute
- *Independence:* Legislature is structurally independent of the university; appointment relationships and donor relationships create informal influence
- *Timeline:* Per legislative process

**STOP PATH 2 — Governor's removal authority and appointment power**
- *Activator:* Governor (with state-specific procedural requirements)
- *Trigger:* Cause-based removal where statutorily permitted; appointment of new trustees as terms expire
- *What "stopped" means:* Trustees individually replaceable; institutional direction shifts as appointments accumulate
- *Independence:* Governor is structurally independent; political alignment between governor and trustee appointees creates informal alignment
- *Timeline:* Per [STATE] removal process; appointment cycle multi-year

**STOP PATH 3 — Public-records litigation; open-meetings litigation; accreditation review**
- *Activator:* Citizens, journalists, advocacy organizations (records and open-meetings); accreditor (institutional review)
- *Trigger:* Records denials; suspected open-meetings violations; accreditation deficiency findings
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders compelling disclosure; accreditation sanctions including probation or loss of accreditation; injunctive relief
- *Independence:* Genuinely independent — courts and accreditors are external to the board
- *Timeline:* Litigation typically 6-24 months; accreditation review on 5-10 year cycles
- *Acknowledged effectiveness:* These paths have demonstrated effectiveness in surfacing structural transparency gaps (the Penn State / Spotlight PA case is illustrative). Not commonly used to stop the institution itself; commonly used to constrain board procedural practice.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed in policy. *Acknowledged structural concern:* Faculty members who criticize board decisions, or community members who file public-records requests against the institution, have historically faced informal consequences (procedural friction, exclusion from informal channels). R33 acknowledged at registration.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** Path 1 (legislature) and Path 2 (governor) are structurally independent but politically connected. Path 3 (litigation and accreditation) is genuinely independent and is the most effective practical stop path for procedural matters.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged.

> *Diagnostic observation on §5:* Public university boards have more independent stop paths than the FOMC example, owing to thicker statutory and judicial overlay. The form's incremental yield is in surfacing that the most effective practical stop is the public-records / open-meetings litigation pathway — which the existing disclosure regime documents but does not frame as a stop mechanism.

- *Industry capture:* Where the institution operates a hospital system or substantial corporate-research relationships, industry influence over priorities is a structural risk.
- *Administration capture:* Boards heavily reliant on the president and senior administration for information about institutional matters can effectively cede oversight to the administration. Recent disputes (Penn State, others) illustrate this risk operationally.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**

- Tenured faculty appointments commit budget for decades
- Real estate transactions are typically irreversible

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring mechanism:** Accreditation review (5-10 year cycle); state legislative oversight (continuous); financial audit (annual); internal audit; public-records and press scrutiny.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Annual disclosure of trustee conflicts; donor relationship disclosure to the extent state law requires; recusal practices.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:**
- *Proactively published:* board agendas; meeting minutes; financial audits; IPEDS data; accreditation reports; budget materials; salary data (for public employees in most states)
- *Available on request:* public records subject to [STATE] public-records law
- *Restricted:* student records (FERPA); personnel records (state-specific); attorney-client communications; pre-decisional deliberative materials in some states

**8.4 Adversarial exposure (R81):** Substantial. Public university boards have higher adversarial exposure than most private institutions and most agencies. Press coverage at flagship institutions is intensive.

**8.5 Known structural failure modes:**
- *Sunshine Act compliance gaps:* Recent litigation (Penn State and others) demonstrates that even well-resourced boards can drift into procedural patterns that exceed statutory limits on closed deliberation. Pattern of executive sessions used for matters not covered by statutory exceptions; "conference" or "training" sessions used to discuss substantive matters; pre-meeting private discussions among trustees that effectively decide matters before public meetings.
- *Donor-influence opacity:* Donor relationships shape institutional priorities in ways not always visible in formal disclosure.
- *Administration capture:* Trustees relying on the administration for institutional information may effectively cede oversight.
- *Athletic-program drift:* Athletic-program autonomy can grow until board oversight is nominal.
- *Branch-campus consolidation patterns:* Decisions affecting host communities made without adequate community engagement are a structural failure mode.

**8.6 Trade-secret / confidentiality declarations:**
- FERPA (student records — federally required)
- Attorney-client privilege (legal counsel communications)
- Personnel records (per [STATE] law)
- Pre-decisional deliberative materials (where state law permits)
- Donor anonymity (where statutorily protected)

> *Diagnostic observation on §8:* §8.5 is where the form's incremental yield is significant. Existing disclosure regimes document what the board does but do not catalogue the *failure modes* the board's structure is prone to. The Penn State / Spotlight PA litigation made the failure modes visible after-the-fact through litigation; the form's framing surfaces them at-registration as structural conditions to be monitored.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** Multi-decade. Public universities are among the most institutionally embedded entities in their states and rarely close.

**9.2 Dismantling process:** Statutory action by the legislature would be required for full dissolution. Branch-campus closure is more common (months to years for an individual closure); full institutional closure has occurred but is rare for flagship institutions.

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Substantial.
- Alumni base of hundreds of thousands creates political and financial inertia
- Capital plant valued in billions
- Pension obligations to current and former employees
- Active research grants representing multi-year commitments
- Student enrollments mid-degree representing direct obligations
- Local economy of host communities tied to institutional operation

**9.4 Data disposition:** Student records retained per FERPA and state archive law; personnel records per state archive law; institutional records to state archives.

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — student debt outcomes, alumni credentials, faculty career investments, and host-community economic dependencies all persist beyond the institution's operations. The board cannot reverse the lifetime debt outcomes of past students; it can support relief mechanisms.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Branch campuses have been replaced or consolidated; full institutional replacement would require multi-decade reconstitution. Successor institutions could be established but would not inherit existing capital, faculty, or research relationships.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. R43 gap acknowledged; institutional closure planning would be a board-level governance exercise the board has not conducted.

The reverse argument — that low thresholds would surface genuine pattern concerns about sunshine-law compliance, donor influence, or branch-campus decisions — is also available. A stakeholder Challenge contesting the threshold is the form's mechanism for adjudicating between these.*

## STRESS-TEST META-NOTES

This example tests the form against an authority with a thick existing disclosure regime: state open-meetings law, state public-records law, accreditation reporting, federal compliance reporting, and sustained press attention. The findings parallel the FOMC example but at smaller scale.

**Where the form yields little new:**

- §1.5, §3.2, §3.3, §6.1–6.3: enabling statute, coercive ceiling, resource ceiling, and dependency relationships are extensively documented in existing materials.
- §7.1: most institutional metrics are published in IPEDS, accreditation reports, and state reporting.
- §8.3: audit interfaces are largely already public.

**Where the form yields something:**

- §2 (affected parties): host-community population framing surfaces a non-consenting population that existing disclosures do not foreground.
- §4.7 (additional thresholds): donor-influence threshold, athletic-program independence threshold, and Title IX systemic threshold are form-specific findings.
- §5.3 (stop paths): the form surfaces public-records / open-meetings litigation as a stop mechanism, which existing materials document operationally but do not frame as such.
- §7.3 (algorithmic systems): admissions, aid, and student-success algorithms used in board-level decisions are not currently visible in most boards' disclosure regimes.
- §8.5 (failure modes): the form catalogues structural failure modes (sunshine compliance gaps, administration capture, athletic-program drift) that existing disclosure regimes document only through litigation or after-the-fact press attention.
- §10.3 (expiration): same finding as FOMC — no statutory sunset, framework R28 non-compliance.

**Net assessment:**

The form's incremental yield against a public university board's existing disclosure regime is concentrated in failure-mode cataloguing, harm-threshold language, algorithmic-system disclosure, and the framework's expiration-default question. State open-meetings and public-records law provide procedural transparency about board operations; the form provides structural framing about board *vulnerabilities*. These are complementary rather than redundant.

The Penn State / Spotlight PA litigation pattern is informative: even with thorough sunshine-law compliance, structural transparency gaps survived for years and required litigation to surface. The form's framing — particularly §4 thresholds, §5 stop paths, and §8.5 failure modes — would have made many of those gaps visible at-registration as structural conditions, before they became litigation.
