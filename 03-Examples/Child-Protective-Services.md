## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** CHILD-PROTECTIVE-SERVICES-EXAMPLE — the state child welfare agency exercising authority over child protection investigations, family supervision, child removal, foster care placement, and termination of parental rights.

**1.2 Functional description:** A state administrative agency (typically a department within a larger health and human services umbrella) that operates the state's child welfare system. Caseworkers, supervisors, foster care licensing staff, and contracted service providers implement the agency's authority across approximately 80 county or regional offices. This registration covers the central authority and its dependent foster care, kinship care, and contracted-services systems.

The state's family court (separately registered as REG-EXAMPLE-FAMILY-COURT-CUSTODY) is the judicial counterpart that adjudicates dependency, custody-on-removal, and termination-of-parental-rights petitions. The two systems operate in coordination; the framework's R55/R59 recognizes the agency-and-court combination as a coordinated authority system for many purposes.

Functional authority:

- *Receives reports of suspected child abuse or neglect* through a state hotline, mandated-reporter channels, and law-enforcement referral. Approximately 200,000–400,000 reports per year for a typical state; varies substantially by population and reporting practice
- *Screens reports and decides which to investigate* — screening decisions are made by hotline staff using state guidelines; approximately 50–60% of reports are screened in for investigation
- *Conducts investigations* — caseworkers visit the family home, interview children (often without parents present, in some states without guardian-ad-litem present), interview parents, contact schools, medical providers, and other collateral sources, and determine whether allegations are "substantiated," "unfounded," "indicated," or other state-specific findings
- *Removes children on emergency basis* where caseworker (with supervisor concurrence) determines imminent risk — emergency removal often occurs without prior judicial authorization; judicial review follows within 24–72 hours per state law
- *Petitions family court* for dependency findings, formal removal, supervised reunification plans, termination of parental rights, and adoption finalization
- *Operates ongoing case management* for families under court-ordered supervision (in-home services, parenting classes, substance-use treatment referrals, mental-health-treatment referrals, regular caseworker visits)
- *Operates the state foster care system* — licensing foster homes (~5,000–15,000 licensed foster homes for a typical state), placing children in foster homes or congregate care, monitoring placements, supporting kinship placements, transitioning youth out of care
- *Maintains the state child welfare information system* — records of all reports, investigations, dispositions, placements, services, and outcomes; shared with federal child welfare data systems (NCANDS, AFCARS); feeds the central registry of substantiated maltreatment
- *Operates contracted foster care, residential treatment, group home, and adoption services* — substantial portion of direct service provision is contracted to private agencies (some nonprofit, some for-profit)
- *Coordinates with adjacent authorities* — courts, schools, law enforcement, medical providers, mental health providers, federal Title IV-E foster care funding, ICWA tribal authorities, ICPC interstate compact, immigration enforcement (in some configurations), juvenile justice, school districts

The agency's authority is consequential because it can remove children from their parents — including emergency removal without prior judicial authorization — and can pursue termination of parental rights through the courts. Removal during a child's developmental years cannot be undone by subsequent reunification; adoption following termination is permanent. Children spend cumulatively millions of childhood-years in foster placement nationally; in any individual case, the years are years of a specific child's life.

The agency operates under a "child welfare" mandate. The framework requires honest description of the operational reality, not the mandate's preferred framing. Operationally:

- The agency's primary investigations and removals fall disproportionately on Black and Native families. Black children are roughly twice as likely to enter foster care nationally; Native children at substantially higher rates than population share would predict; this pattern is documented across federal data (AFCARS) and academic literature, has persisted across decades, and exists in nearly every state at varying magnitude
- Investigations and removals correlate strongly with poverty: families in housing instability, with limited income, with substance use, with mental illness, with disabilities, are disproportionately drawn into the system. Distinguishing poverty from neglect in the agency's operational practice is a documented structural problem
- Reunification rates, while substantial, mean a substantial minority of children entering foster care do not return to their parents — through termination of parental rights, adoption, or aging out
- Children who age out of foster care without permanency face elevated rates of homelessness, incarceration, mental illness, sexual exploitation, suicidal ideation, and economic precarity
- The Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) was passed in 1978 to address documented agency practices of removing Native children from Native families and placing them with non-Native families; ICWA compliance has been uneven across states and remains contested
- The agency's caseworkers are typically young, recently educated, often under-resourced, and turn over rapidly; the same caseworker may not remain on a family's case from initial investigation through final outcome

The framework treats both the formal "child welfare" mandate and these operational realities as the authority being registered. The form does not let one stand in for the other.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding administrative determinations affecting parental rights, child residence, family relationships, and through the dependent foster care system, children's daily lives, education, healthcare, and identity development. Many agency determinations operate as judicial determinations through court adoption of agency recommendations.

**1.4 Domain:** Government (primary).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates under [STATE] child welfare and dependency statutes (typically a child welfare code or analogous statute), [STATE] administrative procedure law, and federal authorities including: the Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA, 42 U.S.C. §5101 et seq.); the Adoption and Safe Families Act (ASFA, codified at 42 U.S.C. §671 et seq., notably the 15-of-22-months termination-petition requirement); Title IV-E of the Social Security Act (foster care funding); the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA, 25 U.S.C. §1901 et seq.); the Multiethnic Placement Act (MEPA); the Interstate Compact on the Placement of Children (ICPC); the Family First Prevention Services Act. Subject to: substantive due process protection of parental rights (Santosky v. Kramer establishing clear-and-convincing-evidence standard for termination); procedural due process; equal protection; ICWA's heightened standards for Indian children. Authority operates under permanent statutory authorization subject to legislative appropriations and reauthorization cycles.

The framework's R28 (Expiration Default) requires that all authority expire absent affirmative renewal. Current state and federal law does not provide a renewal mechanism in the framework's sense; the 2-year framework renewal cycle is a structural commitment beyond statutory requirement.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (any family within state jurisdiction subject to investigation)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties — children whose lives are determined by the agency without their consent; parents subject to investigation, supervision, removal, and termination; siblings affected by removal and placement decisions; extended family members whose homes may be assessed for kinship placement; foster parents whose homes are subject to ongoing licensing and oversight
- [x] Vulnerable populations: children (always); children in foster care (substantially more so); parents experiencing poverty; parents with mental illness; parents with substance use disorder; parents with cognitive or developmental disability; parents experiencing domestic violence; parents leaving abusive relationships; parents with limited English proficiency; immigrant parents; LGBTQ+ parents in some jurisdictions; incarcerated parents; Black families and Native families (as populations disproportionately drawn into the system); youth in foster care, particularly LGBTQ+ youth, youth of color, youth with disabilities, and youth approaching age-out
- [x] Children (under age of majority) — all children subject to investigation, supervision, removal, foster placement, or termination proceedings; children interviewed by caseworkers; children in foster care, kinship care, congregate care, residential treatment; siblings separated by placement decisions; children adopted following termination; former foster youth post-age-out
- [x] Future generations — former foster youth's own children disproportionately enter the system in subsequent generations; documented intergenerational pattern; the system's authority over current children shapes their capacity to parent in adulthood

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** For a typical mid-size state:

- Children investigated annually: ~50,000–150,000
- Children with substantiated findings: ~10,000–30,000
- Children in active out-of-home placement at any given time: ~5,000–25,000
- Children entering care annually: ~5,000–20,000
- Parents under investigation, supervision, or proceedings: substantial multiple of the child counts (most cases involve more than one parent)
- Foster parents, kinship caregivers: ~5,000–15,000
- Former foster youth in transition (in care or aged-out within prior 5 years): ~10,000–30,000

Cumulative population with prior agency contact is substantially larger; estimates suggest approximately 30–50% of all American children are subject to a CPS investigation at some point during childhood, with substantially higher rates for Black children, Native children, and poor children. For framework purposes this registration uses an approximate figure of ~850,000 people in active or recent agency contact in a mid-size state (children, parents, kin, foster caregivers, former foster youth).

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Essentially all 850,000.

Children never consent to agency authority. Parents subject to investigation, supervision, or proceedings did not consent. Siblings, extended family members, and foster parents (who chose to be foster parents but did not consent to specific placements or oversight terms) are non-consenting. Reporters of suspected abuse or neglect (mandated reporters, anonymous reporters, family members) are not subjects of authority but the reports they submit operationalize agency authority over the families reported.

R14 (Non-Participant Exposure Mapping) and R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) apply with full force across the entire affected population. R26 applies with particular force to children, who are the primary subjects of "best interest" determinations and have the least capacity to participate in shaping the proceedings that determine their lives.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**

- [x] Children — *yes; this is the central vulnerable population, across multiple sub-categories*. Children encounter the agency as: subjects of investigation (interviewed, often separately from parents); subjects of removal (including emergency removal); placed children (in foster homes, kinship care, congregate care, residential treatment); subjects of termination proceedings; adopted children post-termination. The agency commits to: caseworker training on child development and trauma-informed interviewing; limits on interview practices that may produce false or distorted statements; coordination with multidisciplinary teams (child advocacy centers) for interviews in serious cases; access to therapy and trauma services for children in care; ICWA compliance for Indian children. R26 substantial gaps acknowledged: training quality varies by office and caseworker; multidisciplinary coordination varies; therapeutic capacity is limited; ICWA compliance has been documented as inadequate across many states; long-term outcome data on children whose lives the agency determines is fragmented across federal and state systems and is not used to drive practice change at the level needed.
- [x] Patients during medical care — *yes*. Children in foster care are often prescribed psychotropic medications at rates substantially higher than the general pediatric population; this practice has been the subject of GAO findings, state reform efforts, and litigation. Foster youth medical care is administered through Medicaid and contracted providers; continuity of care is structurally challenged by placement changes. Pregnant and parenting youth in foster care face additional medical-and-system challenges.
- [x] Detained or confined persons — *yes*. Youth in residential treatment facilities, group homes, and (in some jurisdictions) juvenile detention placed by or in coordination with the agency are confined. Conditions in some such facilities have been the subject of class-action litigation, federal investigation, and licensing failures. R26 substantial gap.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Parents under investigation or supervision face coercive demands for compliance with case plans (parenting classes, drug testing, supervised visitation, mental-health and substance-use treatment, employment, housing) that frequently exceed the parents' practical capacity given the same conditions (poverty, housing instability, employment precarity, mental health) that drew them into the system. Failure to complete the case plan within the ASFA-mandated 15-of-22-months timeline triggers termination petition; this creates structural pressure for parents to perform compliance regardless of underlying capacity. R26 substantial structural gap.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Parents with limited English proficiency, cognitive or developmental disabilities, mental illness, or other capacity-affecting conditions face substantial advocacy disadvantage. Indigent parents in dependency proceedings have constitutional or statutory right to appointed counsel in most states (varies); appointed counsel quality, caseload, and timing of appointment vary substantially. Children's right to counsel or guardian ad litem also varies. R26 substantial gaps acknowledged.

The agency acknowledges that R26 (stricter harm tolerance for non-participants) applies with particular weight because nearly all affected parties are non-consenting and substantial portions are vulnerable across multiple categories simultaneously.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** [STATE] — statewide jurisdiction over residents, with ICPC coordination for interstate placement and ICWA coordination with tribes.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**

- *Investigate:* Enter homes (with consent or court order), interview children (in many states without parental presence and without notice in initial investigations), interview parents and collateral sources, review medical and educational records (subject to applicable privacy law)
- *Substantiate findings:* Determine whether allegations are substantiated, indicated, or unfounded; substantiated findings enter the central registry, propagate to background-check systems, and may bar future employment in child-care, healthcare, education, and other fields
- *Initiate court proceedings:* Petition for dependency findings, removal, supervised reunification, termination of parental rights, adoption
- *Remove on emergency basis:* In imminent-risk situations, remove children with supervisor concurrence; judicial review typically within 24–72 hours per state law
- *Place children:* In foster homes, kinship care, congregate care, residential treatment; placement decisions are administrative subject to court oversight
- *Set case plan conditions:* Parenting classes, drug testing, supervised visitation, mental-health and substance-use treatment, employment, housing — failure to comply triggers escalation
- *Recommend court action:* Courts typically follow agency recommendations on case-plan changes, visitation, reunification, and termination; the agency's recommendations functionally are decisions in many cases
- *License foster homes:* Approve, monitor, and revoke foster home licenses
- *Contract with service providers:* Foster care agencies, residential providers, treatment providers, in-home services
- *Maintain records:* The central child welfare information system contains records that propagate across multiple federal and state databases

The agency may NOT (per constitutional and statutory law):
- Remove children without statutory grounds and either prior judicial authorization or post-removal judicial review within statutory window
- Substantiate findings without preponderance of evidence (or higher standard in some states)
- Petition for termination without statutory grounds and clear-and-convincing-evidence threshold
- Operate in violation of ICWA where Indian children are involved
- Discriminate on race, religion, sex, or disability in placement (though documented disparate-outcome patterns exist; aggregate data limited)
- Compel parental case-plan compliance through criminal contempt absent court order
- Retaliate against parents exercising their right to appeal, to counsel, to refuse interview, to relocate within statutory limits
- Place children outside ICPC requirements

**3.3 Resource ceiling:** For a typical mid-size state child welfare agency:
- Operating budget: ~$500M–$2.5B annually (varies substantially by state; federal Title IV-E reimburses substantial portion)
- Staff: caseworkers ~2,000–10,000; supervisors and administrative ~500–3,000; foster care licensing ~hundreds; central administration
- Foster care capacity: ~5,000–25,000 active placements
- Contracted services: substantial portion of direct service provision contracted to private agencies
- Information systems: state child welfare information system (SACWIS or successor CCWIS); contributing data to federal NCANDS and AFCARS

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** Bounded by [STATE] jurisdiction subject to ICPC and ICWA.
- **Action:** May not exceed statutory authority; may not remove without grounds and process; may not maintain registry entries without due process; may not violate ICWA.
- **Data:** Subject to confidentiality of child welfare records under [STATE] law; subject to federal Title IV-E confidentiality requirements; sharing with non-authorized parties prohibited.
- **Procedural:** Investigations subject to procedural requirements per state law; emergency removal subject to post-removal judicial review timeline; case-plan changes subject to procedural review; termination subject to statutory grounds and judicial process.
- **Other:** Compliance with ICWA in cases involving Indian children; compliance with ASFA timelines; compliance with MEPA prohibitions on race-based placement; compliance with disability accommodation requirements.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Substantial.

The agency's outputs propagate widely:
- *Substantiated findings* enter the central registry of suspected child maltreatment, are queryable in employment background checks for child-care, healthcare, education, and adjacent fields, and may bar parents from such work for years or for life
- *Investigation records* propagate even where unsubstantiated; the record of investigation itself may be visible to subsequent agencies, schools, healthcare providers, and (depending on confidentiality enforcement) other parties
- *Foster care placements* shape children's school district, healthcare provider, friendships, religious and cultural environment, and developmental trajectory
- *Case-plan determinations* shape parents' employment availability (court-ordered classes during work hours), housing decisions, healthcare access, and life trajectory
- *Termination of parental rights* is permanent and irrevocable
- *Reports to federal NCANDS and AFCARS* propagate state agency findings into permanent federal records
- *Coordination with immigration enforcement* (in some configurations and jurisdictions) means agency contact may produce immigration consequences for parents
- *Coordination with school districts, law enforcement, healthcare* propagates findings across sectors

R58 applies despite the agency's classification as direct authority: the propagation of records, findings, and case-plan-driven life changes materially shapes downstream decisions for years to decades after originating events.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The agency commits to not using:

- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin:* Investigations, substantiations, removals, placements, and termination recommendations may not be based on race or ethnicity. Acknowledged that operational practice has produced documented racial disparities of substantial magnitude across decades, that ICWA was passed specifically to address racially-disparate practices toward Native families, and that current practice continues to produce racially-disparate outcomes. R66/R74 finding acknowledged at registration as a foundational structural finding rather than as an isolated concern; aggregate disparate-impact testing of investigation, substantiation, removal, placement, and termination committed; results published.
- *Proxy for poverty:* Determinations may not effectively use markers of poverty (housing instability, food insecurity, employment precarity, transportation limitation, "messy home," child going to school in worn clothing, child seen unsupervised) as proxy for neglect. Acknowledged that operational practice substantially correlates with economic resources; the same conditions that limit parental resources are treated by the system as evidence of inadequate parenting rather than as conditions the system might address through resource provision. R66 substantial structural finding; this is the second foundational structural finding in this registration.
- *Proxy for disability:* Parental disability (cognitive, developmental, mental health, physical) may not function as proxy for parental unfitness. Acknowledged that historical and current practice has produced documented patterns in which parents with disabilities lose custody at rates substantially exceeding what underlying parenting capacity would predict. R66 finding.
- *Proxy for mental illness or substance use:* Diagnostic labels may not function as proxy for unfitness. Treatment-engaged parents may not be disadvantaged solely on the basis of diagnosis. Acknowledged that operational practice varies and that parents in recovery face structural challenges in case plans designed for compliance demonstration rather than recovery support.
- *Proxy for non-conforming parenting:* Cultural and religious child-rearing practices that differ from caseworker expectations may not function as proxy for unfitness. Acknowledged that judicial and caseworker training varies in cultural-competence depth.
- *Proxy for parental advocacy or non-cooperation:* Parents who decline interviews, request counsel, refuse home entry without court order, or otherwise exercise their legal rights may not be disadvantaged on that basis alone. Acknowledged that operational practice has documented patterns of treating exercise of rights as evidence of "uncooperative" behavior in case-plan assessment.
- *Proxy for prior contact:* Prior CPS involvement may not function as proxy for current finding absent independent grounds.

The agency acknowledges that all seven proxy commitments require ongoing structural attention, that historical and current practice has produced repeated R66 findings on multiple of these dimensions, and that the system-level data needed to evaluate current operational practice is partially available but inadequately analyzed and acted upon.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Load-bearing.

- *Death of child known to the system:* Any death of a child during agency contact (open case, prior contact, in placement, transitioning out) triggers immediate external review with public summary; aggregate near-fatality and fatality statistics published per CAPTA requirements
- *Death of child in placement:* Any death in foster care, kinship care, congregate care, residential treatment triggers external investigation; provisional suspension of involved caregivers and providers pending findings; aggregate placement-fatality statistics published
- *Serious injury or sexual abuse in placement:* Any substantiated incident triggers individual review; aggregate placement-abuse statistics published; chronic over-rates by provider trigger contract review or termination
- *Use of psychotropic medication exceeding peer benchmarks for child population:* Aggregate prescription rates reviewed; any single child on three or more psychotropic medications without documented multidisciplinary review triggers individual review
- *Restraint and seclusion in residential placement:* Aggregate use rates monitored; any restraint resulting in injury triggers immediate review
- *Suicide and self-harm in care:* Aggregate rates published; placements correlated with self-harm trigger systemic review
- *Trauma from removal itself:* Removal is acknowledged in clinical literature as itself producing trauma, including for removals later determined unwarranted. Aggregate removal-then-return-without-finding rates tracked; high rates indicate that removal threshold is set too low

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** Substantial.

- *Right-to-counsel barriers:* Indigent parents have right to appointed counsel in dependency proceedings in most states (varies). Late appointment, high counsel caseload, or failure to appoint — finding for each instance; aggregate practice reviewed
- *Children's representation:* Children's right to counsel or guardian ad litem varies by state; quality and time investment by appointed counsel reviewed
- *Procedural failures:* Aggregate appellate reversal rates; aggregate findings of due process violation in registry proceedings
- *Language access failures:* Investigations, hearings, and case plans without competent interpretation — finding for each instance
- *Accommodation failures:* Disability, mental health, cognitive accommodations — findings for failures; aggregate review
- *Caseworker turnover:* High caseworker turnover during a family's case (multiple workers per case) is a documented practice failure affecting quality; aggregate turnover and per-family-worker-count tracked
- *Investigation-to-court timeline failures:* Failures of statutory timelines for emergency-removal judicial review, substantiation determination, court hearings — finding for each instance; aggregate review
- *Case plan coherence:* Case plans that exceed parents' practical capacity to comply (multiple court-ordered services during work hours; transportation requirements without transportation provision; childcare requirements without childcare provision) — review

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Limited applicability. Facility environmental compliance per applicable law.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial. Load-bearing.

The agency's actions produce intergenerational effects that no subsequent decision can reverse:

- *Childhood years lost:* Time in foster care during a child's developmental years cannot be regained. Each placement disruption adds a layer of attachment, educational, and social disruption
- *Severed relationships during formative years:* Removal severs parent-child relationship during the time when attachment patterns form. Subsequent reunification, where it occurs, does not restore the missed development
- *Termination of parental rights is permanent:* The legal parent-child relationship is severed for life. Adoption may follow; the parental rights cannot be reinstated
- *Sibling separation:* Where the agency or the placement system separates siblings, sibling relationships are disrupted in ways that reunification may not fully restore. Sibling separation is a documented and persistent practice in foster care despite policy preferences for keeping siblings together
- *Identity disruption from transracial and transcultural placement:* Children placed across racial, ethnic, religious, or cultural lines may experience identity disruption documented in clinical and developmental literature; this is the practice ICWA was passed to address for Native children
- *Aged-out former foster youth:* Youth who exit foster care without permanency face elevated rates of homelessness, incarceration, mental illness, sexual exploitation, and economic precarity into adulthood
- *Intergenerational system contact:* Former foster youth's own children disproportionately enter the system; the system's authority over current children shapes their capacity to parent in adulthood; the pattern persists across generations
- *Records of substantiated findings* propagate to background checks, employment, healthcare, immigration, and other contexts for parents' lifetimes

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) apply with full force. The agency cannot reverse these effects.

Specific operational thresholds:
- Termination of parental rights triggers individual review; aggregate termination rates by demographic and case category tracked
- Sibling separations require explicit on-the-record findings supporting separation
- Transracial and transcultural placements (where ICWA does not require placement preference) reviewed for ICWA-spirit application and outcome
- Aged-out youth tracked through transition; aggregate post-care outcomes published per Chafee Foster Care Independence Program

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**
- Restrictions on parents' communication with children during pending proceedings — review of necessity in each case
- Restrictions on children's communication with parents, siblings, or extended family — review
- Retaliation against parents who exercise rights, file complaints, or speak publicly — finding
- Retaliation against caseworkers, supervisors, or contracted providers who report internal misconduct — finding
- Restrictions on foster parents' communication with biological families where reunification is the goal — review
- Suppression of children's voice in proceedings — review of practice

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Records released beyond confidentiality protections — finding
- Substantiated-finding registry entries entered without due process — finding
- Records errors that affected parties cannot correct — finding for each substantiated instance; aggregate error rates published
- Records propagated to federal databases (NCANDS, AFCARS) and to background-check systems — these propagate beyond agency control; affected parties may not know which records propagated where
- Children's records released beyond authorized purpose — finding
- Sharing with immigration enforcement, employment background checks, or other parties beyond legal authority — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Chronic asymmetry threshold:* Investigation, substantiation, removal, placement, and termination rates that correlate with race, ethnicity, poverty, disability, language, immigration status, or sexual orientation at levels exceeding what case characteristics account for trigger systemic review. The framework treats persistent racial and economic disparities documented across decades as standing structural findings; review focuses on whether the disparity is narrowing, stable, or widening in the current period.

*ICWA compliance threshold:* Failure of ICWA active-efforts requirements, qualified-expert-witness requirements, placement-preference requirements, or notice-to-tribe requirements — finding for each instance; aggregate compliance reviewed annually.

*Wrongful removal threshold:* Removals subsequently determined unwarranted (return without finding; case closed; reunification within 30 days) — aggregate rate tracked; high rates indicate that removal threshold is set too low.

*Caseworker capacity threshold:* Caseloads exceeding peer benchmarks (Council on Accreditation, Child Welfare League of America, federal court orders in some states) trigger review.

*Foster care drift threshold:* Children in care exceeding ASFA's 15-of-22-months without permanency or termination petition — aggregate review.

*Re-entry threshold:* Children returning to care within 12 months of reunification — aggregate rate indicates whether reunifications are sustainable or premature.

*Aging-out threshold:* Youth aging out without permanency — aggregate rate; aggregate post-care outcomes (housing, employment, education, criminal justice contact, mental health, parenting).

*Medication threshold:* Foster care population on psychotropic medications at rates exceeding pediatric benchmarks — aggregate review.

*Restraint and seclusion threshold:* Aggregate use in residential placements — review.

**4.8 Threshold change-log commitment:** [x] Yes — committed.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

This section requires honest engagement with the structure of administrative authority over family relationships, where the legal protections that attach to government action are substantially attenuated relative to other government authorities of comparable consequence.

**STOP PATH 1 — Family court / dependency court oversight**
- *Activator:* Court reviews emergency removal within 24–72 hours; reviews substantiation findings on appeal; reviews case-plan changes; reviews termination petitions under clear-and-convincing-evidence standard.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of insufficient grounds, due process failure, or ICWA failure.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court may order return of child, dismiss petition, deny case-plan change, deny termination, order remediation.
- *Independence:* Family court is separately registered; it is structurally independent of the agency. *Acknowledged limitation:* Courts hear contested matters under abuse-of-discretion or substantial-evidence standards in many decisions; agency factual determinations (substantiation, risk assessment, placement appropriateness) are typically deferred to; courts adopt agency case-plan recommendations in the substantial majority of cases. R37 (Renewal Process Independence) is partial because the court depends on agency input and the agency depends on court adoption of recommendations; the symbiosis is structural.
- *Timeline:* Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 2 — Federal court action under §1983, Title IV-E compliance, ICWA, and class action**
- *Activator:* Any person with standing under federal civil rights statutes; tribal authority under ICWA; HHS Children's Bureau under Title IV-E; class action plaintiffs.
- *Trigger:* Federal court determination of constitutional violation, ICWA violation, Title IV-E noncompliance.
- *What "stopped" means:* Federal courts may issue orders affecting state agency conduct, declare specific practices unconstitutional, impose monitors, withhold federal funding for noncompliance, restructure agency practice. Most states have been or are currently subject to federal class-action consent decrees regarding child welfare practice; federal monitoring of multiple states is ongoing.
- *Independence:* Federal courts and HHS are structurally independent. *Acknowledged limitation:* Federal class action remedies often span decades; consent-decree compliance varies; federal Title IV-E enforcement has historically been limited; ICWA enforcement varies.
- *Timeline:* Per federal procedures; consent decrees typically 5–20 years.

**STOP PATH 3 — State legislative oversight, executive action, citizen review boards, ombudsperson**
- *Activator:* State legislature; governor; citizen review boards (where established); state child welfare ombudsperson (where established); media and advocacy organizations.
- *Trigger:* Legislative finding; executive determination; review-board recommendation.
- *What "stopped" means:* Legislative reform, appropriations changes, executive action, leadership changes.
- *Independence:* Legislatures and executives are structurally independent of the agency administratively but are politically accountable to constituencies that include affected families and advocates with conflicting positions. Citizen review boards and ombudspersons vary in independence and authority.
- *Timeline:* Per body procedures.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed in policy. *Acknowledged structural concerns:* Parents who file complaints, advocate publicly, or organize against the agency have faced informal disadvantage in their case proceedings. Agency staff who report internal misconduct have faced retaliation in some documented cases. Foster parents who advocate for biological-family interests beyond agency expectations have been removed from licensure. The stated commitment is non-retaliation; the documented gap is itself a recurring concern.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** This question requires direct engagement.

In the formal sense, the agency is a state administrative entity that could be restructured, transferred, or dissolved by state legislative action. In practice, child welfare authority is constitutionally and statutorily required at some level (federal CAPTA conditions state funding on child protective services existence; constitutional state interest in child welfare requires some state mechanism); "dismantling" in the framework's sense would mean substantial restructuring rather than literal cessation.

In the structural sense, the agency's outputs (records, registry entries, propagated findings, foster care placements that have shaped children's lives, terminations of parental rights, adoptions, intergenerational effects) cannot be dismantled at all; they have already propagated.

For framework purposes: 60 months from formal restructuring decision to substantive change, given constitutional, statutory, federal-funding, contractual, personnel, and case-pendency complexities. Restructuring possibilities discussed in policy literature include: investment shift toward family preservation and prevention rather than removal; reduction of contracted private foster care; reorganization of the substantiation and registry framework to align with due process; shift toward kinship-care primacy; substantial funding shift toward economic stabilization of families to reduce poverty-driven cases; alternative configurations involving community-based mutual aid, public health framing, and reduced administrative reach.

**9.2 Dismantling process (interpreted as restructuring):**
- Months 1–12: Legislative or executive action; transition planning; identification of restructured authority for continuing functions; pending case continuation planning; federal coordination
- Months 13–36: Phased transition to new structure; rule and statute changes; agency reorganization; contracted-provider transition; pending cases continued under transitional procedures
- Months 37–60: Full operation of restructured system; legacy case completion; outcome monitoring of restructured practice

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Substantial.
- Constitutional and federal statutory requirement of some state child protective services
- Pending dependency cases
- Multi-year statutory and rule-making processes
- Contracted-provider economy depends on current configuration
- Federal Title IV-E commitments
- Personnel and pension obligations to caseworkers and other staff
- Records propagation to federal databases, background-check systems, immigration, employment, healthcare cannot be recalled
- Termination of parental rights orders are permanent
- Adoptions completed cannot be reversed
- Foster youth in long-term placement
- Federal court consent decrees with multi-year monitoring

R61 (Anti-Entrenchment) finding acknowledged: state child welfare authority is structurally entrenched in ways that exceed any single restructuring decision. The framework treats this as a structural finding rather than as the agency's individual failure.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Records archived per [STATE] retention; confidentiality protections continue; some records propagate to federal databases and other systems and cannot be recalled
- *Institutional records:* Per [STATE] records law
- *Derived outputs:* Findings, registry entries, and foster care placement records propagated to other systems cannot be recalled. R63 finding fully applicable
- *Audit logs:* Per applicable law

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:**

This section requires the most direct engagement.

The agency's actions have caused effects that no restructuring or dismantling can reverse:

- *Years of childhood spent in foster care* cannot be regained. A child who entered care at age 4 and aged out at 18 spent fourteen years of childhood in placement; subsequent recognition of error or restructuring cannot return those years
- *Children separated from parents* during developmental years cannot be unseparated. Subsequent reunification, where it has occurred, does not undo the developmental, attachment, and emotional effects that the literature documents. For some children, reunification has not occurred
- *Termination of parental rights is permanent and irrevocable.* The legal parent-child relationship is severed for life. Adoptions following termination create new legal families; the original relationship is not restorable regardless of subsequent recognition that termination should not have occurred
- *Children adopted following termination* cannot have their adoption legally undone, even where the underlying termination is later recognized as error
- *Sibling separations* enacted during childhood cannot be fully undone in adulthood
- *Children placed across racial, ethnic, religious, or cultural lines* — the practice ICWA was passed to address for Native children, and that continues for non-Native children of color — experience identity disruptions documented in clinical literature; subsequent reconnection does not restore the years of identity disruption
- *Records of substantiated findings* propagate to background checks, employment, healthcare, immigration, and other contexts for parents' lifetimes. Affected parents may not know which records propagated where. Errors in these records may be uncorrectable
- *Aged-out former foster youth* face elevated lifetime rates of homelessness, incarceration, mental illness, exploitation, and economic precarity; these outcomes cannot be undone by subsequent system reform
- *Intergenerational effects* — former foster youth's own children disproportionately enter the system; the cycle perpetuates across generations in ways that subsequent reform cannot undo for the families already affected
- *Trauma from removal itself* — including for removals later determined unwarranted — cannot be undone
- *Records of investigation* even where unsubstantiated may be visible to subsequent agencies and may shape future contact
- *Coordination effects* with immigration enforcement, juvenile justice, and other systems have produced outcomes (deportations, criminal records, disrupted educations) that cannot be reversed

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) apply with particular force. The agency cannot reverse these effects. R65 (Propagation Duty) requires that on restructuring or cessation: (a) the agency notify downstream systems that prior findings may not be authoritative; (b) the agency support remediation for affected parties, including expungement, registry correction, and access to records; (c) the agency preserve records to enable historical investigation and remediation by affected persons.

The framework's reversibility requirement is, for child protective services, a commitment to mitigate, to acknowledge, and to support remediation — not a commitment that effects can be undone. The gap between the structural authority to remove children, terminate parental rights, and shape childhoods, and the structural capacity to prevent or reverse harm, is, in child protective services authority, among the largest of any authority type the framework registers.

The fact that the system operates under a "child welfare" mandate does not reduce the structural reality that its decisions produce irreversible effects on children's lives, and that those effects fall disproportionately on children who are Black, Native, poor, or otherwise marginalized. The mandate is the formal frame; the operational reality is the authority being registered.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:**

R51 (Institutional Replacement Feasibility) requires that this authority be replaceable without destabilizing services beyond authorized scope.

Child protective services function *could* be partially replaced through alternative configurations: substantial investment in family preservation and economic stabilization to reduce poverty-driven cases; shift toward community-based mutual aid frameworks for the substantial portion of current investigations driven by poverty rather than abuse; specialized response for safety-relevant cases; reduction of administrative substantiation infrastructure in favor of judicially-determined findings only; reorganization of foster care toward kinship primacy and away from contracted private agencies.

The constitutional and federal-statutory core — that some state mechanism for child protection must exist in some form — cannot be eliminated; it can be reconfigured. R51 partial gap acknowledged: constitutional and federal-funding constraints limit dismantling beyond restructuring.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:**

No formal restructuring rehearsal has been conducted by the agency. The agency has continuity-of-operations planning for technical disruption but no operational practice of simulating substantial structural change. R43 (Reversibility Verification) gap acknowledged at full scale.

The §9.1 timeline is estimated based on legislative process, federal coordination, contractual obligations, and case-pendency considerations. It is not verified.

The agency acknowledges that R43 applied honestly to child welfare authority is a substantial commitment, and that the question "what would substantial restructuring actually look like" has historically been treated as politically and professionally inadmissible rather than operationally engaged.
