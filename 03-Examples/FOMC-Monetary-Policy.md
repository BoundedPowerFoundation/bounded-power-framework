> **EDITORIAL NOTE — high-existing-disclosure example.**
>
> This example is included to test a specific question about the form: when an authority's existing public disclosures are already extensive, does the registration form yield anything beyond what is already public?
>
> The Federal Reserve's monetary policy authority is among the most heavily-disclosed governmental functions in the modern administrative state. The FOMC publishes a post-meeting statement the same day (since 2000), full meeting minutes three weeks later (since 2005), full meeting transcripts five years later (since 1995), a Summary of Economic Projections four times annually (since 2007), a Monetary Policy Report to Congress twice annually under Humphrey-Hawkins (since 1978), a Statement on Longer-Run Goals (since 2012), a quarterly chairman's press conference (since 2011), individual member speeches, weekly H.4.1 balance sheet releases, and substantial supporting research. This is not an authority that has been hiding from public view.
>
> If the registration form's structural questions add nothing here, the form's claim — that it surfaces information not currently visible — is weakened in proportion. If the form's questions add something, even against this volume of existing disclosure, the claim is strengthened. The example is included to make that test concrete.
>
> Where the form yields little new, this example says so. Where the form yields something the existing disclosures do not, this example says that too. Both findings are part of the test.

## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** FOMC-MONETARY-POLICY-EXAMPLE — the Federal Open Market Committee's monetary policy authority within the Federal Reserve System.

**1.2 Functional description:** The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) is the body within the Federal Reserve System that sets U.S. monetary policy. Functional authority:

- Sets the target range for the federal funds rate, which directly influences short-term interest rates throughout the U.S. financial system
- Directs open market operations through the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's trading desk: purchases and sales of U.S. Treasury securities, federal agency securities, and mortgage-backed securities
- Sets the size and composition of the Federal Reserve's balance sheet (currently approximately $7 trillion, having peaked above $9 trillion)
- Provides forward guidance through statements about the likely future path of policy
- Establishes the framework for emergency lending facilities under §13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act when activated
- Sets policy targets for inflation (currently 2 percent annual PCE inflation, declared January 2012) and operationalizes the dual mandate of maximum employment and price stability assigned by Congress
- Establishes the operational framework within which the Federal Reserve Banks conduct discount-window lending, payment system services, and bank supervision

The FOMC's authority is consequential because U.S. monetary policy directly shapes: borrowing costs for households, businesses, and governments; mortgage rates; credit card interest; auto loan rates; commercial bank lending; the value of the U.S. dollar against foreign currencies; the cost and availability of credit in emerging markets where dollar funding dominates; and the discount rate applied to all future cash flows in U.S. asset markets. Through these channels, FOMC decisions affect employment, household wealth, retirement security, the affordability of housing, and the ability of the U.S. and foreign governments to service debt.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Both direct and indirect.

- *Direct:* The FOMC binds the Federal Reserve Banks through directives that govern open market operations under Section 12A(b) of the Federal Reserve Act. No Federal Reserve Bank may engage in or decline open market operations except in accordance with the Committee's directives. Within the Federal Reserve System, FOMC authority is binding.
- *Indirect:* The FOMC's principal effect on the broader economy operates through R58 — its policy decisions shape decisions made by every other actor in the financial system, including private banks, asset managers, household borrowers, foreign central banks, and sovereign borrowers. The federal funds rate target is not a directive to private actors; it is a signal that, through arbitrage in the federal funds market and the structure of administered rates (interest on reserve balances, the overnight reverse repo rate), produces a defined cost of overnight funding to which private decisions adjust. The FOMC does not set mortgage rates; mortgage rates set themselves in response to FOMC decisions. The mechanism is indirect; the effect is direct.

**1.4 Domain:** Government, with substantial cross-domain effect across Economic, Infrastructure, and Information categories.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Federal Reserve Act of 1913, as amended, particularly:

- §2A (Monetary policy objectives — the dual mandate of "maximum employment, stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates")
- §2B (Appearances before and reports to Congress — the Humphrey-Hawkins testimony and Monetary Policy Report)
- §10 (Board of Governors)
- §12A (Federal Open Market Committee — composition, voting, internal organization)
- §13(3) (Emergency lending authority — substantially modified by Dodd-Frank, requiring Treasury Secretary approval)
- §14 (Open-market operations — the underlying transactional authority)
- 12 U.S.C. § 225a (the codified Humphrey-Hawkins reporting requirement)

The FOMC consists of 12 voting members: the seven members of the Board of Governors (presidentially appointed, Senate-confirmed, 14-year staggered terms); the president of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York (permanent voting member); and four rotating presidents of the other 11 Federal Reserve Banks, drawn one each from four geographic groupings on annual rotation.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] General public — every person living in the United States or holding U.S.-dollar-denominated assets is materially affected by monetary policy decisions
- [x] Residents of geographic area — the entire U.S. population
- [x] Non-consenting third parties — the entire affected population
- [x] Regulated industry participants — depository institutions, primary dealers, money market funds
- [x] International counterparts — foreign central banks, sovereign borrowers, dollar-denominated borrowers globally
- [x] Vulnerable populations: low-wealth households (most exposed to employment effects of monetary tightening); fixed-income retirees (most exposed to interest-income effects); first-time homebuyers (most exposed to mortgage-rate effects); workers in interest-sensitive sectors

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** Approximately 340 million U.S. residents. Indirectly, through dollar reserve-currency status and dollar-denominated debt held abroad, an additional several billion are materially affected by FOMC decisions in measurable ways.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Effectively all 340 million U.S. residents, plus the international population materially affected through indirect channels.

Monetary policy is the paradigm case of authority exercised over a population that has neither opted in nor any practical exit. There is no consent transaction at the household level to FOMC authority; participation is constitutive of being a person living in the U.S. economy. Even immediate exit (emigration, conversion of all wealth to non-dollar assets) does not insulate from FOMC effects, because the dollar's role in international finance means policy decisions propagate globally.

R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) applies to the full affected population.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes, indirectly*. Children in households affected by monetary-policy-induced employment changes, housing-cost changes, and intergenerational wealth changes are affected. The FOMC does not act upon children directly but its decisions shape the economic environment children are raised in.
- [ ] Patients during medical care — not applicable as direct surface; indirect through health-system financing.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — not applicable as direct surface.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Households for whom debt service is a binding constraint on consumption, workers in employment relationships where wage growth lags inflation, debtors whose debt is fixed-rate while income is variable.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Monetary policy's distributional effects fall hardest on populations with the least access to political channels of accountability: low-wealth households, recent immigrants, persons with limited financial literacy, persons whose primary connection to the financial system is wage employment and consumer credit.

> *Diagnostic observation on §2:* The Federal Reserve's existing public materials describe affected populations primarily in macroeconomic aggregate terms — employment rates, inflation rates, GDP growth — rather than as the population of persons subject to its authority. The form's framing of "people this authority can act upon" surfaces something the existing disclosures do not foreground: that the entire U.S. population is non-consenting and structurally cannot exit, and that the distributional incidence of monetary policy choices falls unevenly across that population. This is well-known to monetary economists; it is not the framing that appears in the FOMC's primary public communications.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single nation: United States. Global indirect effect acknowledged.

The FOMC's binding authority extends only to the Federal Reserve Banks under U.S. law. Its substantive economic effect extends globally through the dollar's role in international trade, debt, and reserves. Approximately 60% of foreign-currency reserves held by central banks worldwide are dollar-denominated; substantial portions of cross-border lending and emerging-market sovereign debt are dollar-denominated. FOMC decisions therefore have material effects on financial conditions outside the United States, including in jurisdictions whose own central banks have no representation in FOMC deliberations.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**

The FOMC's tools are:
- Federal funds rate target (set as a target range; operationalized through interest on reserve balances and the overnight reverse repo facility)
- Open market operations (Treasury and agency securities; quantitative easing and quantitative tightening)
- Forward guidance (statement language about likely future policy)
- Emergency lending under §13(3) (with Treasury Secretary approval; limited to broad-based programs since Dodd-Frank, not aid to individual non-bank firms)
- Discount window administration (rate set by Reserve Banks subject to Board approval; standing facility)

The FOMC may NOT (statutorily or by practice):
- Set fiscal policy (taxation, spending — these are Congressional)
- Set foreign exchange policy (this is the Treasury's authority; the New York Fed acts as fiscal agent for the Treasury in foreign exchange operations)
- Allocate credit to specific industries, firms, regions, or households (uniform rate; Section 13(3) limited to broad-based programs)
- Set capital requirements for banks (this is regulatory authority of the Board of Governors and FDIC, not the FOMC)
- Engage in primary-market lending to the U.S. Treasury (statutorily prohibited)
- Operate without statutory authority (the Federal Reserve Act is subject to Congressional amendment or repeal at any time)

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: the Federal Reserve System is self-funded from interest income on its securities portfolio; the FOMC itself has no separate appropriation. The System's net operating expenses are approximately $9-10 billion annually; the Board and FOMC operations are a fraction of this.
- Balance sheet: currently approximately $7 trillion in assets. There is no statutory ceiling on the size of the Federal Reserve's balance sheet. Its size is set by policy decisions of the FOMC itself within the framework of statutory authority.
- Staffing: Board of Governors has approximately 3,000 staff; the 12 Federal Reserve Banks have approximately 21,000 staff. FOMC operational support is a subset of this.
- Data systems: extensive — including the New York Fed's trading and operations infrastructure, the Board's data and research systems, and inter-District communications.

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not direct policy at specific U.S. regions or states; policy is uniform nationally.
- **Action:** May not allocate credit to specific firms, industries, or households; may not engage in fiscal policy; may not set foreign exchange policy without Treasury direction; may not engage in regulatory enforcement (separate authority).
- **Data:** Limited — the FOMC operates on extensive economic data but does not collect personal data from individuals; its data inputs are aggregated economic statistics produced by other federal agencies (BLS, Census, BEA), market data, and System-internal data.
- **Procedural:** Decisions require majority vote of the 12 voting members; the chair customarily chairs but does not break ties (ties have been historically rare). Voting member rotation among Reserve Bank presidents is statutorily fixed.
- **Other:** May not commercialize its operations; pays no dividends to private parties (Reserve Banks pay statutorily-fixed dividends to member banks; surplus is remitted to the Treasury).

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Substantial. The FOMC's outputs that materially influence decisions made by others:
- Federal funds rate target (sets short-term funding costs across the financial system)
- Forward guidance (shapes expectations of future rates, which shape long-term rates today)
- Statement language and press conference remarks (read closely by market participants for incremental signal)
- Summary of Economic Projections (shapes private-sector expectations of where rates and the economy will go)
- Monetary Policy Report and Humphrey-Hawkins testimony (sets the explanatory frame within which policy is understood)
- Speeches and writings of individual FOMC members (move markets to varying degrees; the Chair's speeches in particular are market-moving)

For each: the institutional or individual reader (banks, asset managers, households, foreign central banks, journalists, members of Congress) makes the actual decision. The FOMC's outputs substantially shape the decision through structured visibility.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:** The FOMC commits structurally to:
- *Proxy for political alignment:* Independence — the FOMC is structured for political insulation through 14-year governor terms, removability "for cause" only, self-funding, and a deliberative process not subject to Executive Branch direction. R03 acknowledged: the structure is designed against partisan capture, but is not immune from longer-term political pressures or from capture by the financial industry (see §6.4).
- *Proxy for industry preference:* The FOMC's policy is uniform across the economy — it does not target specific sectors. However, the *incidence* of monetary policy varies substantially across sectors (interest-sensitive sectors like housing and durable goods are more affected than others). This is a structural property of monetary policy, not a proxy violation under R66; the FOMC commits to documenting differential incidence in its public communications rather than concealing it.
- *Proxy for distributional preference:* The FOMC explicitly does not target distributional outcomes; its mandate is aggregate (employment, prices, long-term interest rates). The distributional consequences of policy are real, are studied internally, and have been increasingly documented in public communications since the 2010s. The framework does not require the FOMC to target distributional outcomes — that would conflict with the dual mandate — but R26 requires honest disclosure of the distributional incidence on non-consenting populations.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect, through downstream pathways.

Monetary policy's bodily-harm pathways are mediated through employment, income, and housing stability. Tight monetary policy that produces involuntary unemployment produces measurable increases in mortality (deaths of despair, deferred medical care, housing displacement). Loose monetary policy that produces asset-price inflation without wage growth produces measurable increases in housing instability among renters. These are well-documented in economic literature.

The FOMC commits to: (a) annual public reporting of the employment effects of policy decisions, including disaggregated by demographic group; (b) acknowledgment that the dual mandate's "maximum employment" target is a substantive commitment, not an aspirational one; (c) reference in policy decisions to the labor-market effects of policy paths considered.

*Honest acknowledgment:* The FOMC's existing communications do address employment effects extensively. The form's value-add at this threshold is modest: forcing explicit threshold language ("at what level of involuntary unemployment do we treat the policy as causing harm?") in a form the existing communications do not use.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:**

- Households whose ability to participate in the housing market is foreclosed by mortgage-rate spikes — when first-time-buyer affordability falls below a defined level, this is a finding for review.
- Small businesses whose access to working-capital credit is foreclosed by tight policy — when small-business lending volume contracts beyond a defined level relative to demand, this is a finding for review.
- Workers who are involuntarily unemployed by policy-induced contraction — when the gap between actual and "maximum employment" exceeds the FOMC's own estimate by a defined margin, this is a finding for review.

The FOMC's existing communications discuss these channels in macroeconomic terms; the form's structural framing surfaces them as harm thresholds, which the existing communications do not.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Limited direct applicability. The FOMC does not engage in environmental policy. Indirect effect through credit conditions affecting energy investment is acknowledged; the FOMC does not currently treat climate transition as a material variable in its primary policy framework, though staff research and Reserve Bank research extensively study it.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial.

Monetary policy decisions bind future generations through several channels:
- *Public debt:* Federal Reserve balance sheet expansion that monetizes federal deficits binds future taxpayers to debt service costs that depend on future rate paths
- *Asset-price levels:* Policy that produces sustained asset-price appreciation transfers wealth from future asset purchasers (often younger generations) to current asset holders (often older generations); this is a generational binding of the most material kind
- *Inflation expectations:* Once inflation expectations become unanchored, restoring them often requires policy actions whose costs fall on a different generation than the one that benefited from the expansion
- *Reserve currency status:* Policy choices that erode dollar reserve-currency credibility have multi-generational consequences for U.S. economic position

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) applies with substantial force here. Monetary policy's effects on asset prices, wealth distribution, and inflation expectations propagate across generations and are not reversible by reversing policy. The FOMC commits to acknowledging this in its renewal evidence: that the policy's effects are not fully captured by reversibility analysis at the policy-decision horizon.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** Limited.

The FOMC has no enforcement authority over speech and does not regulate communications. The relevant threshold is the FOMC's own communications transparency: if the FOMC's public communications materially misrepresent the substance of internal deliberations, this is a finding. The transcript-publication-with-five-year-lag mechanism makes this verifiable in retrospect, with the trade-off that real-time misrepresentation cannot be checked against the underlying deliberation until five years later.

The FOMC commits to: (a) faithful representation of the substance of internal disagreement in published statements and minutes; (b) acknowledgment in communications of policy paths considered but not adopted; (c) post-hoc verification of communication accuracy when transcripts are released.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**

The FOMC does not collect personal data and produces no individual-level outputs. The relevant threshold is information about the FOMC itself: failure to publish FOMC documents within stated timelines, failure to make Reserve Bank operations auditable, failure to respond to reasonable requests for information about policy implementation.

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Chronic asymmetry threshold:* The distributional incidence of monetary policy on wealth, employment, housing access, and intergenerational outcomes is uneven by race, ethnicity, age, and geography. The FOMC commits to disaggregated reporting of policy outcomes and to acknowledging in communications when the distributional incidence is material. *Note on the existing disclosures:* the FOMC has substantially expanded distributional analysis in its communications since 2020, but this analysis is not yet structurally integrated with policy-decision communications. The form's framing surfaces a gap the existing materials acknowledge but do not yet close.

*Capture threshold:* If informal influence by the financial industry over Reserve Bank board appointments, FOMC member selection, or staff research direction reaches a level that materially shapes policy, this is a finding. R03 acknowledged: the Federal Reserve System has structural features designed against capture, but the appointment of class A and B Reserve Bank directors by member banks is itself a structural feature that critics have identified as a capture risk.

*Independence-erosion threshold:* If political pressure on the FOMC reaches a level that materially shapes policy decisions in ways that depart from the dual mandate, this is a finding. The FOMC's structural insulation (14-year terms, removable for cause, self-funded) is designed against this; the threshold is not zero pressure but pressure sufficient to override the deliberative process.

**4.8 Threshold change-log commitment:** [x] Yes — committed.

> *Diagnostic observation on §4:* This section is where the form's incremental yield is largest. The FOMC's existing communications discuss harm channels (employment, housing, distributional incidence) in macroeconomic and policy-effect language. They do not currently use threshold language — explicit measurable conditions that, if crossed, trigger review. Forcing the FOMC to declare its own thresholds would surface decisions that are currently embedded in dual-mandate interpretation and made implicitly. Whether this is desirable is contested (see §10 on independence).

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

This section is structurally difficult. The FOMC is a central bank operating with explicit statutory insulation from immediate political pressure. Stop paths must be honest about the structural conditions under which they operate.

**STOP PATH 1 — Statutory amendment by Congress**
- *Activator:* Congress, through legislation
- *Trigger:* Political determination that the Federal Reserve Act requires amendment, repeal, or restructuring
- *What "stopped" means:* The full range — from narrow technical amendments through the various proposed "Audit the Fed" provisions, through statutory changes to the dual mandate (proposed periodically, never enacted), through hypothetical repeal of the Federal Reserve Act
- *Independence:* Congress is structurally independent of the Federal Reserve. The Fed cannot prevent Congressional amendment of the Federal Reserve Act.
- *Acknowledged limitation:* Congress is not structurally independent of political cycles, of financial-industry lobbying, or of Federal Reserve communications and informal influence. The Fed's relationship with Congress is itself a structural condition rather than a clean stop path.
- *Timeline:* Per Congressional procedures.

**STOP PATH 2 — Removal of governors "for cause"**
- *Activator:* The President, with Senate confirmation of replacements
- *Trigger:* "Cause" — the term is undefined in statute; historical practice has treated it as misconduct rather than policy disagreement
- *What "stopped" means:* Individual governors removed; replacement governors must be confirmed by Senate
- *Independence:* The President is structurally independent of the Federal Reserve, but the "for cause" standard is itself a structural feature; whether policy disagreement constitutes "cause" has been historically untested. *Acknowledged structural concern:* recent administrations have probed the boundaries of this standard; whether it holds depends on judicial interpretation that has not yet been definitive.
- *Timeline:* Constitutional process; Senate confirmation typically months to over a year.

**STOP PATH 3 — Internal: Federal Open Market Committee voting**
- *Activator:* The 12 voting members of the FOMC
- *Trigger:* Majority vote against a policy direction proposed by the Chair
- *What "stopped" means:* The proposed policy does not occur; an alternative is adopted
- *Independence:* This is *internal*, not external. The framework's R32 (Independent Stop Authority) requires structural independence from the institution being overseen. FOMC voting is not such a path. It is included here because it is a real mechanism — dissents do happen, and policy paths are constrained by anticipated voting — but it is properly classified as a *deliberative* mechanism rather than a *stop* mechanism in the framework's sense.
- *Acknowledged R32 finding:* Listing internal voting as a stop path overstates what stop paths normally mean. The FOMC's most genuine external stop is statutory amendment by Congress (Path 1); the Presidential removal power (Path 2) is contested and constrained; internal voting (Path 3) is process, not stop.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed in policy. *Acknowledged structural concern:* members of Congress who advocate for Federal Reserve restructuring sometimes face significant industry-funded electoral opposition. R33 (No Penalty for Good-Faith Challenge) is acknowledged at registration as a structural commitment; the gap between the formal absence of penalty and the informal political costs of challenging the Federal Reserve is itself a recurring R03 finding.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:**
- Path 1 (Congress): genuinely independent of the Federal Reserve, but not independent of political cycles or industry influence
- Path 2 (Presidential removal): structurally available, contested in practice
- Path 3 (Internal FOMC voting): not independent in the framework's sense

The FOMC has fewer structurally independent stop paths than R24 (Redundant Stop Activation) anticipates. This is not concealable; the structural insulation that gives the FOMC its independence from immediate political pressure is the same property that limits external stop paths. The framework treats this as a finding to be disclosed, not a defect to be papered over.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged.

> *Diagnostic observation on §5:* This is where the form's incremental yield is significant. The FOMC's existing communications do not frame its own stop paths in this way; the question "what stops this authority" is not one the FOMC's primary communications address. The form's framing forces the disclosure that the central bank's stop architecture is thinner than its disclosure architecture, and that the same statutory insulation that protects independence also limits stoppability.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions):**
- The 12 Federal Reserve Banks (operational implementation of FOMC directives)
- New York Fed Open Market Trading Desk (executes open market operations)
- Federal Reserve Board staff (research, economic analysis, statistical production)
- Federal Reserve Bank research staff (regional economic analysis)
- FOMC Secretariat (meeting administration, records production)
- Primary dealers (intermediate counterparties for open market operations; ~25 firms)

**6.2 Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with structural relationship):**
- U.S. Treasury (fiscal policy counterpart; foreign exchange operations partner; recipient of Fed surplus remittance; co-administrator of §13(3) emergency lending)
- Congress (statutory authority source; oversight via Humphrey-Hawkins testimony, GAO audit jurisdiction, appointment confirmation)
- The Executive Branch (governor appointment power; Treasury coordination; Fed staff are not subject to Executive direction but the institution operates within the federal government)
- Foreign central banks (counterparts in dollar swap lines, international policy coordination, reserve management)
- Federal Reserve Board of Governors as a separate body (regulatory and supervisory authority, distinct from FOMC's monetary policy authority but personnel-overlapping)
- FDIC (deposit insurance authority; coordination on bank stability)
- OCC (bank supervisor for nationally chartered banks; coordination)
- SEC (securities regulator; coordination on financial-stability matters)

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure compromising stop paths):**
- *Statutory authority:* The Federal Reserve Act is amendable by Congress; the FOMC operates entirely under statutory authority and would cease to exist if the Act were repealed
- *Dollar reserve-currency status:* The FOMC's effectiveness depends on the dollar's status in international finance; this status is itself maintained by U.S. policy choices and credibility, not by the FOMC alone
- *Federal Reserve Bank operational infrastructure:* The Reserve Banks' trading, payment, and operational systems are System-internal but their continuity is essential to FOMC implementation
- *Treasury market liquidity:* Open market operations require a functioning Treasury market; severe Treasury market dysfunction would compromise the FOMC's primary policy-implementation channel

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes.

- *Industry capture:* The Federal Reserve System's relationship with the financial industry is structural — Reserve Bank class A directors are elected by member banks; class B directors are elected by member banks but chosen for diverse-economy representation; class C directors are appointed by the Board of Governors. The class-A appointment structure is a structural feature critics have identified as a capture risk. Reserve Bank presidents are selected by their boards (with Board of Governors approval); the appointment process is opaque relative to many federal positions.
- *Academic capture:* The Federal Reserve System is the world's largest employer of monetary economists. The substantial fraction of academic monetary economics dependent on Federal Reserve research funding, conference participation, and visiting positions creates structural pressure on the field's relationship with the institution.
- *Political pressure:* The FOMC's structural insulation does not insulate the institution from sustained political pressure. Recent decades have seen escalating pressure on the FOMC from administrations of both parties; the institution's response to this pressure is itself a recurring matter of public concern.
- *Ideological capture:* The FOMC's framework for understanding the economy — including its model of the natural rate of interest, the Phillips curve, the dual mandate's interpretation — is itself a body of ideas that has evolved and can evolve. Capture by a particular school of macroeconomic thought is a longer-term structural risk that R62 (Necessity Decay) treats seriously.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**
- Balance-sheet operations have substantial path dependence; balance sheet expansion (QE) is more easily executed than balance sheet contraction (QT) for technical reasons related to bank reserve demand and Treasury market dynamics
- Forward guidance commitments, once made, are costly to reverse — abandoning forward guidance at high cost is itself a policy action with effects
- Inflation expectation anchoring, once lost, requires more aggressive restoration than maintenance — the trajectory is asymmetric

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:**
The FOMC coordinates with foreign central banks through the Bank for International Settlements, bilateral swap lines, and direct communications. Material coordination is disclosed in FOMC minutes and Bank statements. Standing dollar swap lines with the ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Japan, Bank of Canada, and Swiss National Bank are public.

**6.7 Disclosure completeness assertion:** [x] Asserted, with the standard caveat that confidential information received from foreign officials and counterparties is not disclosed.

**6.8 External authority operationalization (R72):** Limited.
- The FOMC operationalizes the Treasury's foreign exchange directives when conducting foreign exchange operations as fiscal agent
- The FOMC's emergency lending authority under §13(3) requires Treasury Secretary approval since Dodd-Frank; in this specific channel, the FOMC operationalizes external authorization

**7.1 Declared metrics:**

The FOMC's metric publication is among the most extensive in modern governance. Methodologies are documented in academic and System publications.

**7.2 Indirect signals:** Substantial — the FOMC's deliberation considers a wide range of signals beyond the declared metric set, including financial-stability indicators, labor market indicators beyond the unemployment rate (wage growth, JOLTS data, U-6 measures), housing indicators, and qualitative anecdotes from the Beige Book. The Statement on Longer-Run Goals frames the FOMC's interpretive approach.

**7.3 Algorithmic decision systems:** [ ] No. The FOMC's decisions are deliberative; algorithmic policy rules (Taylor rules, Friedman rules) are reference benchmarks discussed in deliberation, not decision rules.

**7.4 Outcome-versus-declaration check:** The FOMC's after-the-fact accountability is structured through the transcript-publication mechanism (five-year lag) and through the cumulative public record of decisions, statements, and outcomes. This is substantial but operates with delay; real-time accountability is constrained by the deliberation-protection rationale.

> *Diagnostic observation on §7:* This is where the FOMC's existing disclosure is closest to comprehensive. The form's incremental yield here is small. The FOMC's metric publication, methodology disclosure, and outcome accountability are already at or above what the form requires. The only finding the form surfaces is the trade-off between real-time accountability and deliberation protection, which the FOMC's existing disclosures discuss extensively.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring mechanism:**
- Internal: continuous self-monitoring by the FOMC and Board staff
- External: GAO audit authority (constrained by statutory exclusions of monetary policy deliberations from GAO review); Congressional oversight via Humphrey-Hawkins; academic monitoring; financial-press monitoring; market participant monitoring
- *R41 partial gap acknowledged:* Drift in monetary policy framework — for example, gradual changes in the FOMC's interpretation of "maximum employment" or its operational targets — is observable in retrospect but not currently subject to a structured drift-monitoring process. The form surfaces this as a finding.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Internal ethics and recusal processes; periodic structural review (e.g., the 2020 framework review). The framework review process is itself a real R54 (Constitutional Renewal) implementation: the FOMC has periodically re-examined its policy framework. This is a structural feature the form recognizes positively.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:**
- *Proactively published:* statements, minutes (3-week lag), transcripts (5-year lag), Summary of Economic Projections, Monetary Policy Report, balance sheet data (weekly), discount window borrowing data (with lag), Beige Book, individual member speeches, research publications
- *Available on request:* extensive — the Federal Reserve's FOIA process handles substantial volume
- *Restricted:* monetary policy deliberations are statutorily excluded from GAO audit; supervisory information is protected under banking law; certain emergency lending counterparty information is published with delays

**8.4 Adversarial exposure (R81):** Substantial. The FOMC's decisions, deliberations (with delay), reasoning, and outcomes are extensively available for external evaluation. This is among the highest adversarial-exposure profiles of any U.S. governmental authority.

**8.5 Known structural failure modes:**
- *Independence erosion under sustained political pressure:* an ongoing concern, addressed by structural insulation but not eliminated
- *Industry capture through appointment processes and academic-funding relationships:* structural risk acknowledged
- *Path dependence in balance sheet operations:* technical limitation on policy reversibility
- *Forward guidance commitment costs:* cost of reversing prior guidance is itself a policy constraint
- *Reserve currency dependency:* the FOMC's effectiveness depends on conditions outside its control

**8.6 Trade-secret / confidentiality declarations:**
- Monetary policy deliberations are protected from real-time disclosure for the deliberation-quality rationale; this is itself the trade-off the framework's R67 floor must evaluate
- Bank supervisory information is restricted under banking law
- Foreign-counterparty confidential information is protected

The FOMC commits to: (a) the five-year transcript publication standard; (b) full methodology publication for declared metrics; (c) acknowledgment in real-time communications of substantive disagreement at the meeting.

> *Diagnostic observation on §8:* The FOMC's adversarial exposure is genuinely high relative to most authority systems. The form's incremental yield here is in §8.5 (failure modes), where the form's framing surfaces structural risks the FOMC's existing communications discuss but do not catalogue as failure modes per se.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** Not directly applicable. The FOMC exists by statute; "dismantling" would mean Congressional repeal of the Federal Reserve Act, which is not an operational decision the FOMC could execute against itself.

The relevant question for the form is: how long would a transition from the current FOMC to a substantially different monetary policy regime take? The honest answer: 1-3 years for a substantial framework change executed deliberately; 5-10 years for a structural reorganization (e.g., changes to FOMC composition or voting structure); indeterminate for full statutory replacement.

**9.2 Dismantling process:** Not directly applicable in the framework's typical sense. The FOMC's "ending" would be Congressional action; the FOMC itself would execute a winding-down per statutory direction.

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Substantial.
- Dollar reserve-currency status creates strong international-policy reasons to preserve U.S. monetary policy continuity
- Federal Reserve balance sheet operations are deeply embedded in Treasury market functioning; Federal Reserve abolition without successor would be massively disruptive
- The institution has a 110+ year track record creating political and economic inertia
- Personnel, infrastructure, and intellectual frameworks are extensive and would not transfer cleanly to any successor

**9.4 Data disposition:** Federal records would be transferred to the National Archives per applicable law; current records would remain accessible. The Federal Reserve's research record is a significant scholarly archive.

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — substantial. Monetary policy effects on asset prices, wealth distribution, intergenerational outcomes, and inflation expectations cannot be reversed by reversing policy. The current FOMC's effects are baked into the economy and persist regardless of future policy. This is a structural feature of monetary policy, not a defect.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** A successor institution to the FOMC would inherit the same structural problems the current FOMC manages. Replacement is feasible (other central banks operate in different institutional forms); the question is what would be gained or lost. The form does not require this evaluation; it surfaces it as a question.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. R43 gap acknowledged — this is among the entries on the FOMC's adversarial-exposure profile that current practice does not address.

> *Diagnostic observation on §9:* This is where the form surfaces something genuinely uncomfortable. Most authorities can answer "how does it end" within their operational lifecycle. The FOMC, like most institutions of comparable embeddedness, cannot. The form's question forces the acknowledgment that monetary policy is among the least-reversible authority systems in modern governance — that this is a structural feature, and that the framework's reversibility commitments apply only attenuatedly to authorities of this kind.

## SECTION 10 — Authorization, renewal, and trigger declarations

**10.1 Authorizing body:** Congress, through the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 and subsequent amendments.

**10.2 Date of authorization:** 1913 (initial); 1935 (FOMC restructured to current form); 1978 (Humphrey-Hawkins); 2010 (Dodd-Frank §13(3) changes); subsequent amendments.

**10.3 Expiration date:** None specified by statute. The Federal Reserve Act has no sunset provision.

> *Diagnostic observation on §10.3:* This is the form's largest single finding for an authority of this type. The framework's R28 (Expiration Default) requires automatic expiration unless affirmatively renewed. The Federal Reserve Act has no expiration. Under the framework's structural rules, this is a constitutional non-compliance finding. The framework would treat the absence of a sunset provision as a structural condition requiring renewal — i.e., the framework's response to "no expiration date" is to assign one, with renewal required against R47 evidence standards.
>
> A registration of this authority under the framework would either: (a) decline to register because the underlying statute is non-compliant with R28, or (b) register with a *constructed* expiration date sufficient to surface the renewal question against the framework's evidence standards. This is the form's most substantive incremental yield in this example: it surfaces that the statutory structure of one of the most consequential governmental authorities lacks a feature the framework treats as load-bearing.

**10.4 Renewal interval:** Constructed at 5 years for the purpose of this example. The actual statutory framework has no renewal cycle.

**10.5 Renewal evidence declaration:** [x] All five domains, but with the acknowledgment that the FOMC's existing accountability mechanisms (Humphrey-Hawkins, GAO audit authority, Congressional oversight) do not cleanly map to the framework's renewal-evidence categories. The form's structural questions yield evidence the existing accountability framework does not produce.

**10.6 Necessity-decay acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged. The FOMC's necessity is high but not unconditional. R62 (Necessity Decay) requires evidence that the authority continues to be necessary against changing conditions; monetary policy frameworks evolve, and the FOMC's continued operation should not be presumed regardless of conditions.

**10.7 Aggregate trigger threshold:** 250 triggers in 90 days. SCBP-09 §II places non-consenting populations of 100M-1B in the band 25-250; the FOMC selects the maximum of this band given its scale.

**10.8 Aggregate threshold justification:** At an authority of this scale, with a non-consenting affected population effectively equal to the entire U.S. population, the noise floor of civic triggers is high. A threshold of 250 in 90 days corresponds to a per-capita rate of approximately 0.0001% per quarter — vanishingly small relative to population, but substantial in absolute terms. The FOMC selects the SCBP-09 band ceiling deliberately, acknowledging that the bad-faith counter-example identifies maximum-of-band selection as a leak; the alternative is a threshold so low (25) that ordinary financial-press criticism would generate constant aggregate triggers without indicating substantive concern.

> *Self-applies-to-itself note:* The bad-faith counter-example flags maximum-of-band threshold selection as the form's most exploitable §10.7 leak. The FOMC's selection of 250 is at the ceiling. The justification is that low thresholds would be triggered by ordinary financial commentary; the reverse argument — that low thresholds would surface genuine pattern concerns — is also available. The form cannot adjudicate between these. A stakeholder reviewing this registration could file a Challenge-class trigger contesting the threshold.

**10.9 Response time window:** 30 days.

**10.10 Response window justification:** Default value. Shorter would be impractical for an institution operating on 6-week meeting cycles; longer would defeat the purpose.

## STRESS-TEST META-NOTES

This example was constructed to test what the registration form yields when applied to an authority whose existing public disclosures are already among the most extensive of any modern governmental authority. The findings are mixed.

**Where the form yields little new:**

- §7 (metrics): the FOMC's metric publication is at or above what the form requires. Methodology, aggregate publication, and outcome documentation are already comprehensive.
- §1.5 (statutory basis): well-documented in existing materials.
- §3.2 (coercive ceiling): fully specified by statute and existing practice.
- §6.6 (coordination): material coordination already disclosed in standard FOMC channels.
- §8.3 (audit interfaces): the FOMC's existing audit interfaces are extensive.

**Where the form yields something:**

- §2 (affected parties): the form's framing of "people this authority can act upon" surfaces the entire-population, non-consenting, structurally-cannot-exit framing in a way the FOMC's own communications do not foreground.
- §4 (harm thresholds): the form's threshold-language requirement surfaces decisions currently embedded in dual-mandate interpretation. Whether explicit threshold declarations would be desirable for monetary policy is contested; the form forces the question.
- §5 (stop paths): the form's three-independent-paths requirement surfaces that the central bank's stop architecture is structurally thin. Internal voting is not a stop path in the framework's sense; statutory amendment by Congress is the only clean external stop. The FOMC's structural insulation, which protects independence, also limits stoppability.
- §8.5 (failure modes): the form's framing surfaces structural risks the FOMC's existing communications discuss but do not catalogue as failure modes.
- §9 (reversibility): the form forces acknowledgment that monetary policy is among the least-reversible authority systems in modern governance — this is a structural feature the existing communications do not foreground.
- §10.3 (expiration): the largest single finding. The Federal Reserve Act has no sunset; the framework's R28 treats this as constitutional non-compliance. Whether the framework's R28 should apply to authorities of this institutional kind is the substantive question the form surfaces.

**Net assessment:**

The form's incremental yield against an authority with this disclosure profile is modest but real, and concentrated in a small number of structurally meaningful sections. The form does not duplicate the FOMC's existing disclosures; it surfaces structural framings the existing disclosures do not use. The largest yield is in §10.3, where the form's expiration-default rule confronts a statutory structure that has no expiration — making visible a structural feature of the underlying institution that is not currently a public question.

This finding cuts both ways. A reader who concludes that R28 should apply to all authority including monetary policy will read this registration as evidence that the framework surfaces something important. A reader who concludes that monetary policy is structurally distinct in ways that R28's expiration default cannot handle will read this registration as evidence that the framework's breadth (R58 plus R28) does not gracefully accommodate authorities of this kind. The form does not adjudicate between these readings; it makes the disagreement visible.
