## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** HOA-EXAMPLE — a single-subdivision homeowners association.

**1.2 Functional description:** A homeowners association governing 287 single-family homes in a residential subdivision. Functional authority:
- Enforces architectural and use restrictions (building modifications, paint colors, landscaping, fence styles, accessory structures, vehicle parking, business activities from home)
- Enforces use restrictions (rental restrictions, occupancy limits, noise rules, pet rules, holiday decoration limits)
- Levies and collects assessments ($1,800/year regular plus special assessments)
- Imposes fines for violations ($25–$500 per violation per [STATE] HOA law and Association bylaws)
- Files liens on properties for unpaid assessments and fines
- Initiates foreclosure on persistent non-payment liens
- Maintains common areas (pool, clubhouse, playground, signage, entry features)
- Operates a Board (5 elected members) and committees (architectural, social, finance)

The HOA's authority is consequential because it can ultimately force foreclosure on a member's home for unpaid assessments — an escalation from a $25 paint-color violation to total loss of property.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting property rights, financial obligations, and property use.

**1.4 Domain:** Voluntary Association (operating in Real Estate space).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates as a [STATE] nonprofit corporation under the [STATE] Common Interest Community Association Act (or analogous statute). Authority derives from the Declaration of Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions (CC&Rs) recorded against each property at creation of the subdivision in [YEAR]. The CC&Rs run with the land — every owner is automatically a member, regardless of whether they read or accepted the CC&Rs at purchase.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (287 households, ~940 residents)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties (renters, family members, visitors of homeowners — affected by HOA decisions but not members)
- [x] Vulnerable populations: elderly homeowners on fixed income; renters who cannot influence HOA decisions; homeowners facing financial hardship
- [x] Children (~280 children in the subdivision; affected by HOA rules on play, noise, decorations, recreational facilities)

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** ~940 residents.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Effectively all 940 residents.

While homeowners technically consent to CC&Rs upon purchasing property, this consent is not meaningful in the framework's sense for several reasons:
- CC&Rs are extensive (often 50+ pages); average buyers do not read them
- CC&Rs are not negotiable — accept them or do not buy in the subdivision
- Once property is purchased, exit requires selling the house (substantial financial and life-disruption cost)
- Residents who are family members of homeowners (spouses, children, elderly parents) did not consent at all
- Renters did not consent to the CC&Rs that govern their housing
- Subsequent rule changes by the HOA Board may not have been part of original "consent" but bind all owners

R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) is interpreted to apply to the full resident population. Click-through-style consent at home purchase does not constitute meaningful consent to ongoing micro-governance of one's property and conduct.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes*. Children are subject to HOA rules on play, noise, decorations, recreational use. Rules can constrain children's outdoor play (basketball hoops in driveways, chalk on sidewalks, holiday decorations) in ways that affect childhood experience.
- [ ] Patients — partial; medical equipment outside (oxygen tanks, etc.) sometimes regulated.
- [ ] Detained — not applicable.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Owners facing financial hardship cannot exit without selling; renters depend on owner-landlord relationships shaped by HOA rules.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Elderly residents, residents with disabilities, residents with limited English may face HOA proceedings without effective advocacy.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — the [SUBDIVISION] subdivision specifically.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Architectural review approval/denial for modifications
- Citation of violations (warning, then fine)
- Fines $25–$500 per violation per occurrence
- Late fees on unpaid assessments
- Lien filing on property for unpaid assessments and fines ([STATE] law allows liens for HOA unpaid amounts)
- Foreclosure initiation on persistent unpaid liens (per [STATE] law, requires court process)
- Restriction of common-area access for members in violation
- Architectural enforcement (requirement to remove/restore unauthorized modifications)

May NOT (per [STATE] law and bylaws):
- Enter homes without consent or court order
- Enforce restrictions that violate fair housing law
- Enforce restrictions on protected speech (subject to commercial/aesthetic distinctions)
- Levy assessments or fines outside CC&Rs and bylaws authority
- Discriminate on protected characteristics
- Foreclose without court process and statutory cure opportunities

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: $580K annually (assessment income)
- Staff: 0 paid full-time; 1 part-time community manager (contracted firm); 5 volunteer Board members
- Capital reserves: ~$340K
- Common area facilities: pool, clubhouse, 2 acres landscaped commons

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not extend authority outside the subdivision.
- **Action:** May not impose criminal penalties; may not enter private homes without consent/order; may not enforce CC&R provisions superseded by federal law (e.g., satellite dish, antenna, religious display, solar panel rules).
- **Data:** May not share resident information beyond HOA business; may not maintain non-HOA-purpose records.
- **Procedural:** May not impose fines without notice and opportunity to cure; may not enforce selectively; may not conduct enforcement during Board member personal disputes.
- **Other:** May not adopt rules that discriminate on protected characteristics; may not pursue foreclosure for amounts below statutory threshold without court approval.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** HOA decisions affect property values for all subdivision homes (positive or negative), neighborhood reputation, residents' sense of community.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The Association commits to not using:
- *Proxy for race / national origin / language:* Will not use neighborhood reputation, "community standards," or "aesthetic concerns" as cover for differential treatment of homeowners by demographic characteristic.
- *Proxy for political signaling:* Will not enforce signage rules, flag rules, or holiday-decoration rules differentially based on political content (subject to lawful aesthetic distinctions).
- *Proxy for personal animus:* Board members must recuse from any enforcement matter involving personal disputes with the resident.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect. The HOA's primary direct authority is financial and aesthetic, not physical. However: HOA enforcement of medical-equipment-outside (oxygen, mobility aids) restrictions can cause bodily harm to residents who need them. Threshold: any case where HOA enforcement reasonably contributes to a resident's medical decline or injury triggers immediate review.

Foreclosure leading to housing loss has documented bodily-harm consequences for vulnerable residents; this is captured in §4.7 financial harm.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** If residents are unable to participate in Board meetings (no accessible meeting space, no language interpretation when needed, no remote access option), this triggers review.

If enforcement actions are imposed without notice and opportunity to cure per bylaws, those cases are findings.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not directly applicable.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantive at small scale. HOA decisions shape family wealth (property values), childhood experience (rules affecting play, gathering, decoration), and intergenerational relationships (rules on multi-generational households, accessory dwellings).

Threshold: if HOA practices systematically advantage long-term residents over new residents, or systematically disadvantage families with children, this triggers review.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** If HOA enforcement is documented as retaliation against residents who:
- Speak publicly about HOA practices
- File complaints
- Run for Board against incumbents
- Advocate for policy changes

...this is a finding for each case.

If signage rules suppress political speech beyond lawful aesthetic limits, this is a finding.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Resident information shared outside HOA business — finding
- Records retained beyond stated retention — finding
- Surveillance of residents (cameras, recording) beyond common areas — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Financial harm threshold:* If the HOA initiates foreclosure proceedings against any resident with disability, fixed-income elderly status, or family with minor children without exhaustion of alternatives (payment plans, hardship reduction), each case is a finding. If aggregate foreclosure rate exceeds 0.5% of homes per year, this triggers review.

*Disparate enforcement threshold:* If enforcement actions correlate with race, ethnicity, family status, religion, or other protected characteristics at p<0.05, this triggers review.

*Selective enforcement threshold:* If similar violations receive systematically different responses, this triggers review.

*Capricious enforcement threshold:* If new residents face enforcement for matters tolerated for long-term residents, this triggers review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Member vote / Board recall**
- Activator: Members may petition for special meeting; quorum vote may direct Board action; petition signatures may force Board recall election.
- Trigger: Member determination of mismanagement.
- What "stopped" means: Board members may be recalled; rule changes may be reversed; specific actions may be halted.
- Independence: Members are independent of Board (Board exists at members' pleasure).
- Timeline: Per [STATE] HOA law and bylaws procedures.

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil litigation under [STATE] HOA law**
- Activator: Any member with standing.
- Trigger: Court determination of violation of CC&Rs, bylaws, or [STATE] HOA law.
- What "stopped" means: Court orders may halt practices, void actions, impose damages.
- Independence: Courts independent.
- Timeline: Per court procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — [STATE REGULATORY OVERSIGHT BODY]**
- Activator: The state agency with limited oversight authority over HOA practices under state law.
- Trigger: Regulatory investigation.
- What "stopped" means: Limited; primarily complaint handling and information.
- Independence: State agency.
- Timeline: Per agency procedures.

HOA stop paths are notably weaker than government authority stop paths. Member-vote stop paths require organizing collective action among neighbors, which is structurally difficult. Civil litigation requires individual financial commitment. State oversight is limited. This is acknowledged as a structural feature of HOA authority.
