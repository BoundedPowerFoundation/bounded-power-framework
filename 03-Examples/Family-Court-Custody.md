## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** FAMILY-COURT-CUSTODY-EXAMPLE — the state family court system's authority over child custody, visitation, and parental rights.

**1.2 Functional description:** The family court division of a state's general jurisdiction trial courts (sometimes a separate family court, sometimes a division of superior court — varies by state) exercises authority over the most intimate decisions in family life. This registration covers the custody, visitation, and parental-rights jurisdiction; the same court's domestic violence, divorce, child support, paternity, and adoption jurisdictions are related but separately scoped. Functional authority:

- Decides which parent (or third party) a child lives with after parental separation, divorce, or breakdown — the legal label is "physical custody" or "primary residence"
- Decides which parent makes decisions about a child's medical care, schooling, religious upbringing, and major life choices — the legal label is "legal custody" or "decision-making authority"
- Decides how often, on what schedule, and under what conditions a child sees the parent the child does not primarily live with — the legal label is "visitation," "parenting time," or "access"
- Decides whether visits must be supervised, by whom, in what setting, and at what cost
- Decides whether a parent's contact with a child may be suspended, restricted, or terminated for cause — including suspension during pendency of allegations of abuse, neglect, alienation, or domestic violence
- Issues, modifies, and enforces protective orders and stay-away orders affecting parent-child contact
- Appoints guardians ad litem (lawyers for children), custody evaluators (mental health professionals who assess families and recommend outcomes), and parenting coordinators (third parties who resolve day-to-day disputes between parents)
- Holds parents in contempt for violation of court orders (including non-compliance with visitation, decision-making, or financial obligations); contempt findings may include fines, supervision, and jail
- Modifies prior orders on showing of changed circumstances — orders are durable but not final
- Terminates parental rights — permanently and irrevocably — where statutory grounds are met (typically in dependency or adoption proceedings, but in some states also in private family court)
- Issues orders affecting where a parent may live with a child (relocation), what activities a child may engage in, what kinds of relationships a child may have with new partners of either parent, and other features of daily life

The court's authority is exercised by individual judges hearing individual cases, with substantial judicial discretion within a "best interest of the child" legal standard. The standard is not numerical and varies by judge, by jurisdiction, and over time.

The court's authority is consequential because it allocates time with children, decides which parent's voice carries in a child's upbringing, and can sever the parent-child relationship for life. Outcomes affect not only the parents and children at issue but the children's own future families, the parents' housing, employment, and mental health, and the children's lifetime development. Many of these outcomes are irreversible: childhood years lost cannot be regained; relationships severed during formative years cannot be fully restored by later reunification; the developmental and emotional consequences of high-conflict custody proceedings on children are documented in long-term clinical and developmental literature.

The court operates on the legal premise that its determinations serve the child's best interest. The framework treats this premise honestly: best-interest standards in custody proceedings are professionally and academically acknowledged to be substantially indeterminate, judicially variable, and influenced by factors the court does not formally measure (income, education, presentation, gender, cultural fit with the judge's expectations, ability to afford legal representation and expert witnesses, race, immigration status, and disability). The framework treats both the formal standard and the operational reality as the authority being registered.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting parental rights, child residence, family relationships, financial obligations, liberty (through contempt and protective order mechanisms), and through downstream effects on housing, employment, immigration, and mental health for parents and children.

**1.4 Domain:** Government (primary).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates under [STATE] family code (or analogous statute), [STATE] court rules, the state constitution, and the U.S. Constitution as applied to state family proceedings. Subject to: substantive due process protection of parental rights (Troxel v. Granville and related doctrine — recognizing that the right to direct the upbringing of one's children is fundamental, but that courts may intervene where the state interest in child welfare is engaged); procedural due process; the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) where applicable; the Hague Convention on International Child Abduction in international cases; the Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA) governing interstate jurisdiction; state-specific protections for domestic violence survivors; relevant case law. Authority to operate is permanent under state constitutional structure of judicial branch; this registration commits to a 3-year framework renewal cycle as a structural commitment beyond statutory requirement.

The framework's R28 (Expiration Default) applies awkwardly to state judicial authority. The court does not expire absent renewal in the statutory sense; the framework's renewal-cycle commitment is a structural disclosure commitment, not a substantive limit on judicial authority.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (anyone within state jurisdiction whose family relationships are subject to court determination)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties — children whose lives are determined by the court without their consent; parents who did not initiate proceedings but are subject to them; grandparents, stepparents, partners, and other family members affected by court orders
- [x] Vulnerable populations: children (always); parents experiencing domestic violence; parents with mental illness; parents with substance use disorder; parents with cognitive or developmental disability; parents with limited English proficiency; immigrant parents (some of whom face immigration consequences from family court contact); low-income parents (whose access to representation, expert witnesses, and evaluators is materially constrained by ability to pay); parents with HIV or other stigmatized health conditions; LGBTQ+ parents in some jurisdictions; incarcerated parents
- [x] Children (under age of majority) — all children subject to custody, visitation, and parental-rights determinations; children whose parents are involved in proceedings whether or not the children are formally parties; children testifying as witnesses; children interviewed by guardians ad litem or custody evaluators
- [x] Future generations — children's own future relationships, parenting capacity, and family formation are shaped by their experience of family court proceedings

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** For a typical state: approximately 80,000–120,000 active family court cases involving custody and visitation issues at any time; annual filings approximately 60,000–100,000. This registration uses approximate figures of ~240,000 people in active or recent proceedings (parents, children, third parties) for a mid-size state. Cumulative population with prior family court contact is substantially larger (multiples of the active number).

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Essentially all 240,000.

Children never consent to family court proceedings about them. Parents who initiate proceedings consent only to the proceeding's existence, not to its outcome — which they cannot decline. Parents who did not initiate are wholly non-consenting. Third parties (grandparents, stepparents, current partners) are non-consenting subjects of orders that may bind their conduct.

R14 (Non-Participant Exposure Mapping) and R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) apply with full force across the entire affected population. R26 applies with particular force to children, who are the primary subject of "best interest" determinations and who have the least capacity to participate in shaping the proceedings that determine their lives.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**

- [x] Children — *yes; this is the central vulnerable population*. Children encounter the court as: subjects of custody and visitation determinations; interviewees by guardians ad litem and custody evaluators; witnesses (in some proceedings, in some states); subjects of protective orders. The court's stated commitment is to the child's best interest. The framework requires honest disclosure of the gap between commitment and operational practice. The court commits to: (a) judicial training on child development and trauma; (b) appointment of qualified guardians ad litem; (c) limits on direct child testimony where less harmful methods are available; (d) attention to child voice in age-appropriate ways. R26 substantial gaps acknowledged: training quality varies; guardian ad litem appointments vary by available resources; child-voice elicitation practices vary by jurisdiction and judge; long-term outcome data on the children whose lives the court determines is not systematically collected by the court.
- [x] Patients during medical care — *partial*. Parents and children with mental illness, substance use disorder, or other health conditions may be evaluated by court-appointed clinicians whose role is forensic rather than therapeutic; the dual-role tension is documented in clinical literature. The court commits to qualifying evaluators and to disclosure of evaluator role to parties; substantive gap acknowledged regarding the structural tension between forensic evaluation and therapeutic relationship.
- [x] Detained or confined persons — *yes*. Incarcerated parents face structural disadvantages in custody proceedings (inability to attend hearings, limited access to counsel, restricted communication with children, inability to meet "active parenting" expectations). The court's accommodations vary by jurisdiction and case; substantial R26 gap acknowledged.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes*. Parents whose ability to retain counsel, hire expert witnesses, afford evaluator fees, attend hearings during work hours, and travel for visitation depends on economic resources are in coercive economic relationships with respect to the proceeding's outcome. Indigent parties in custody proceedings have no statutory right to appointed counsel in many states (in contrast to dependency or termination proceedings, where appointed counsel is constitutionally or statutorily required in most states). The financial cost of contested custody proceedings frequently exceeds the resources of one or both parents. R26 substantial structural gap.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Parties with limited English proficiency, cognitive or developmental disabilities, mental illness, or other capacity-affecting conditions face substantial advocacy disadvantage in custody proceedings. Court interpreters are available but quality varies; representation is conditional on ability to pay; self-represented litigants are common and at substantial disadvantage. R26 substantial gaps acknowledged.

The court acknowledges that R26 (stricter harm tolerance for non-participants) applies with particular weight because nearly all affected parties are non-consenting and a substantial portion are vulnerable across multiple categories simultaneously.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** [STATE] — statewide jurisdiction over residents, with interstate coordination per UCCJEA and international coordination per Hague Convention.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- *Allocate physical custody:* Determine which parent or third party a child resides with, including sole, joint, or split custody arrangements
- *Allocate legal custody / decision-making:* Determine which parent makes major decisions about the child
- *Set visitation / parenting time:* Determine schedule, conditions, supervision requirements, and locations for parent-child contact
- *Suspend or restrict contact:* Order supervised visitation, suspended visitation, or no-contact pending hearing or as final order
- *Order protective measures:* Issue restraining orders, stay-away orders, and protective orders affecting parents
- *Appoint third parties:* Guardians ad litem, custody evaluators, parenting coordinators, supervised visitation providers; orders may require parents to pay these costs
- *Hold parents in contempt:* For violation of court orders; may include fines, supervised probation, and incarceration up to statutory maximum
- *Order treatment, evaluation, classes:* Anger management, parenting classes, substance use treatment, mental health evaluation; conditions for restoration of contact
- *Modify prior orders:* On showing of changed circumstances; this means orders are durable but never final, exposing parents to repeated litigation throughout the child's minority
- *Terminate parental rights:* Permanently and irrevocably, where statutory grounds are met; in some states this occurs in private family court, in others only in dependency proceedings
- *Order relocation or restrict relocation:* Determine whether a parent may move with the child to a different jurisdiction
- *Order release of records:* Medical, educational, mental health records of parents and children
- *Subpoena witnesses, compel testimony, sanction parties for litigation conduct*

The court may NOT (per constitutional and statutory law):
- Override the constitutional right to parent absent state interest in child welfare (Troxel doctrine; the right is fundamental but not absolute)
- Terminate parental rights without statutory grounds and procedural due process
- Discriminate on race, religion, sex, sexual orientation, or disability in custody determinations (though documented patterns exist; aggregate disparate-outcome data limited)
- Compel a child to testify in ways that exceed protective limits
- Ignore ICWA where applicable
- Issue orders exceeding subject-matter jurisdiction
- Penalize a party for exercising appellate rights

**3.3 Resource ceiling:** For a typical state family court system:
- Operating budget: ~$120–250M annually (varies substantially by state size and structure)
- Judges hearing family cases: ~80–250 (some dedicated, many also handling other civil/criminal matters)
- Court staff: ~500–1,500
- Court-appointed evaluators, guardians ad litem, parenting coordinators (rostered): ~hundreds of qualified professionals
- Court facilities, records systems, case management technology

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** Bounded by [STATE] jurisdiction subject to UCCJEA and Hague.
- **Action:** May not exceed statutory remedies; may not impose criminal penalties beyond contempt; may not order outcomes inconsistent with constitutional protections.
- **Data:** Court records subject to confidentiality provisions for juvenile and family matters; mental health, substance use, and medical records subject to federal privacy law; unauthorized disclosure prohibited.
- **Procedural:** Hearings must comply with due process; ex parte orders limited per court rules; required notices and waiting periods per statute; counsel-of-choice rights protected.
- **Other:** Compliance with ICWA in cases involving Indian children; compliance with the Service Members Civil Relief Act in cases involving service members; compliance with disability accommodation requirements.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Substantial.

The court's outputs propagate widely:
- *Custody and visitation orders* shape children's daily lives, education, healthcare access, religious observance, and family relationships throughout the child's minority
- *Custody determinations* affect parents' housing decisions (including school district), employment (including travel availability), new relationships, and life trajectory
- *Custody evaluator reports* become part of the record; subsequent judges, guardians ad litem, and evaluators may rely on prior reports without independent verification
- *Findings about parents* (alleged abuse, alleged alienation, mental health diagnoses, substance use determinations) propagate into other proceedings, including criminal cases, immigration proceedings, employment screening (where records become public), and child welfare records
- *Termination of parental rights orders* are permanent records affecting parents' lifetime relationships with children and potentially with future children
- *Protective orders* propagate to law enforcement databases and into other contexts
- *Court-ordered conditions* (treatment, evaluation, supervised visitation requirements) generate ongoing financial and life obligations
- *Parental alienation findings or rejections* shape subsequent litigation in the same case and in similar cases through informal precedent within the court

R58 applies despite the court's classification as a direct authority: the propagation of findings, orders, and evaluator reports materially shapes downstream decisions made by other parties (subsequent judges, child welfare agencies, immigration adjudicators, employers in some contexts, schools, healthcare providers) for periods far longer than the originating proceeding.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The court system commits to not using the following proxies:

- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin, language:* Custody, visitation, and parental-rights determinations may not be based on race, ethnicity, national origin, language, or accent. Acknowledged that operational practice across the system has produced documented racial and ethnic disparities in child welfare and custody outcomes; aggregate disparate-impact data is limited because many family cases are confidential and outcome tracking is fragmented. R66/R74 finding acknowledged at registration; system-level disparate-impact data collection committed.
- *Proxy for socioeconomic status:* Determinations may not effectively use ability to maintain stable housing, employment, transportation, or to afford counsel and evaluators as proxy for parental fitness. Acknowledged that operational practice substantially correlates outcomes with economic resources; this is a structural R66 finding because the same conditions that limit parental resources (poverty, housing instability, employment precarity) are treated by the system as evidence of parental inadequacy rather than as conditions the system might address.
- *Proxy for cultural difference:* Child-rearing norms vary by culture; "best interest" determinations may not effectively privilege one culture's child-rearing norms over another's. Acknowledged that judicial training and evaluator training varies in cultural-competence depth; structural R66 concern.
- *Proxy for mental illness or substance use as parental unfitness:* Diagnostic labels may not function as proxy for unfitness. Treatment-engaged parents may not be disadvantaged solely on the basis of diagnosis. Acknowledged that operational practice varies; structural R66 concern requiring ongoing attention.
- *Proxy for parenting style or willingness to litigate:* Parents who decline conflict, accept unfavorable terms, or are less aggressive in litigation may not be advantaged or disadvantaged on that basis alone. Acknowledged that adversarial process structurally rewards aggressive litigation; this is a structural feature that the form of the proceeding itself produces.
- *Proxy for gender:* Mothers and fathers may not be treated differently on the basis of gender in custody determinations. Historical practice has shown documented gender-based patterns in different directions across decades; current operational practice in this state should be assessed against measurable outcome data.

The court acknowledges that all six proxy commitments require ongoing structural attention, that historical practice in family courts has produced repeated R66 findings on multiple of these dimensions, and that the system-level data needed to evaluate current operational practice is not currently collected with sufficient granularity in most states.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect but real.

The court does not directly use force on parties (with limited exceptions: contempt incarceration; sheriff's enforcement of orders). However:
- *Domestic violence outcomes:* When the court's determinations leave a victim of domestic violence in proximity to or under shared custody with the abuser, subsequent harm to victim or children is documented in many cases. Failure to credit credible domestic violence allegations is a recurring structural finding. Threshold: any post-order injury or death of a party or child where the order knowingly maintained access in the face of credible domestic violence evidence triggers individual and systemic review.
- *Child abuse outcomes:* When the court's determinations place children with a parent later substantiated as abusive, this triggers review of the determination process.
- *Suicide and self-harm outcomes:* Family court proceedings are associated in clinical literature with elevated suicide risk for parents in protracted high-conflict cases, including for parents who lose custody under contested circumstances. Threshold: any suicide of a party during pendency or within 12 months of order, or any in-court self-harm, triggers review.
- *Contempt incarceration medical and safety conditions:* Per general jail standards.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:**

- *Self-representation prevalence:* If aggregate self-representation rates exceed peer benchmarks for similarly-resourced courts, this triggers review of representation access. (In family court, self-representation rates of 60–80% in low- and middle-income cases are typical; this is itself a structural finding requiring attention.)
- *Procedural errors:* Aggregate appellate reversal rates on procedural grounds, aggregate findings of due process violation — review.
- *Language access failures:* Hearings without competent interpretation, document failures — finding for each instance.
- *Accommodation failures:* Disability, mental health, cognitive accommodations — findings for failures.
- *Ex parte order overuse:* Aggregate ex parte order issuance and post-hearing affirmance rates monitored; high reversal rates indicate process drift.
- *Evaluator and guardian ad litem capacity:* If court rosters cannot meet appointment demand, leading to delayed proceedings or under-qualified appointments, this triggers review.
- *Continuance and delay patterns:* Family court delays affect children's stability; aggregate time-to-disposition tracked; outliers reviewed.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not directly applicable.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial. Load-bearing.

The court's actions produce intergenerational effects that no subsequent order can reverse:

- *Childhood years lost:* Custody arrangements during a child's developmental years cannot be undone. A child who grows up primarily with one parent under a court order cannot have those years restored if the order is later modified
- *Parent-child relationships severed during formative years:* Visitation restrictions, no-contact orders, and termination of parental rights during a child's early years produce attachment, developmental, and relational effects that subsequent reunification cannot fully restore
- *Termination of parental rights is permanent and irrevocable:* The order severs the legal parent-child relationship for life. Adoption may follow; the parental rights cannot be reinstated regardless of subsequent change in parental circumstances
- *Children's own future family formation* is shaped by their experience of family court proceedings, parental separation, and the patterns they observe; this is documented in long-term outcome literature
- *Parental records of family court findings* (alleged abuse, alleged alienation, mental health and substance use determinations) propagate into subsequent custody cases (involving same children or subsequent children of the same parent) and into other contexts; affected parents may not know which records propagated where
- *Sibling separations:* Where the court allocates siblings differently, sibling relationships are disrupted in ways that subsequent reunification may not fully restore

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) apply with full force. The court cannot reverse these effects. The thresholds here are honest acknowledgment, not numeric:

- Termination of parental rights triggers immediate individual review; aggregate termination rates in private family court (vs. dependency proceedings) tracked
- Sibling separations require explicit on-the-record findings supporting separation
- Long-term contact restrictions (>12 months without review) require periodic re-examination
- Findings entered into the record without due process protections require correction processes

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**

- *Restrictions on parents discussing their cases:* The court may impose confidentiality orders; aggregate use and scope reviewed.
- *Retaliation findings:* Parties who file complaints, file appeals, or speak publicly about the court may not face retaliation in their proceedings — finding for documented retaliation.
- *Restrictions on children speaking with parents about case:* Where ordered, scope and duration reviewed.
- *Suppression of credible domestic violence testimony:* If the court systematically discounts or fails to credit credible domestic violence evidence, this is a systemic finding requiring review.
- *Gag orders affecting parents' ability to disclose case content:* Aggregate use and scope reviewed.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**

- Records released beyond court rule confidentiality protections — finding
- Mental health, substance use, or medical records used without proper authorization — finding
- Children's records released to non-party access — finding
- Findings in the record that contain material errors and that affected parties cannot correct — finding
- Sealing failures: records that should be sealed but are not — finding
- Custody evaluator reports stored or transferred beyond authorized purpose — finding

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Chronic asymmetry threshold:* If outcome rates correlate with race, ethnicity, immigration status, or socioeconomic status at levels exceeding what underlying case characteristics account for, this triggers systemic review. Acknowledged that current data collection limits ability to detect; the court system commits to disparate-outcome data collection.

*Domestic violence response threshold:* If credible domestic violence allegations are systematically discounted in custody determinations, leading to documented post-order harm, this is a recurring finding requiring structural response. The court system commits to: judicial training; specialized domestic violence dockets where caseload supports; evaluator qualification standards including domestic violence competence.

*Parental alienation finding threshold:* Findings of "parental alienation" or analogous concepts have been the subject of academic and clinical critique because the diagnostic basis is contested and findings have produced documented patterns of placing children with abusive parents. Aggregate use and outcomes reviewed; reliance on contested diagnostic frameworks reviewed.

*Child voice threshold:* Children's voice in proceedings — through guardian ad litem, in-camera interview, age-appropriate testimony — is variable across cases and judges. The court system commits to written practice on child voice elicitation and to review of cases where child voice was not elicited in age-appropriate ways.

*Self-represented litigant outcome threshold:* If outcomes for self-represented litigants differ significantly from outcomes for represented parties on similar facts, this triggers review of representation access and self-help resources.

*Termination-of-parental-rights threshold:* If termination of parental rights is sought or granted in private family court (rather than dependency proceedings) on bases that would not support termination in dependency proceedings, this triggers review.

*Contempt incarceration threshold:* If contempt incarceration is used in family court at rates exceeding peer benchmarks, particularly for non-payment of support or visitation interference, this triggers review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

This section requires honest engagement with the structure of judicial authority.

**STOP PATH 1 — Appellate review and post-conviction relief**
- *Activator:* Any party to a final order with standing.
- *Trigger:* Court of Appeals (and state supreme court on discretionary review) determination of legal or constitutional error.
- *What "stopped" means:* Appellate court may reverse, remand, or modify trial court orders; in extreme cases, may order new hearing before different judge; may issue precedential rulings affecting future cases.
- *Independence:* Appellate courts are structurally independent of trial courts. *Acknowledged limitation:* Appellate review applies an abuse-of-discretion standard to most family court decisions, which means the trial court's discretion is preserved unless clearly erroneous; many adverse outcomes are not legal errors and are not reviewable in practice. Appellate practice requires resources (counsel, transcript, briefing) that many parties cannot afford. Family court appeals frequently take 12–24 months, during which the trial court order remains in effect.
- *Timeline:* 12–24 months typical; expedited review available in some categories (e.g., termination of parental rights).

**STOP PATH 2 — Federal court action under §1983, due process, and ICWA review**
- *Activator:* Any person with standing under federal civil rights statutes; tribal authority under ICWA.
- *Trigger:* Federal court determination of constitutional violation; ICWA violation findings.
- *What "stopped" means:* Federal courts may issue orders affecting state court conduct, declare specific practices unconstitutional, impose monitors. *Acknowledged limitation:* Younger abstention doctrine limits federal court intervention in pending state family proceedings; Rooker-Feldman doctrine limits federal review of completed state court judgments. Federal §1983 actions for due process violations in family court are typically narrow.
- *Independence:* Federal judiciary is structurally independent.
- *Timeline:* Per federal procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Judicial conduct review, legislative reform, and judicial education systems**
- *Activator:* [STATE] judicial conduct commission (for individual judge misconduct); state legislature (for statutory and procedural reform); state judicial education programs; state supreme court rule-making authority.
- *Trigger:* Misconduct determination; legislative finding of structural failure; rule-making determination.
- *What "stopped" means:* Individual judges may be disciplined or removed; rules may change; statutes may be amended.
- *Independence:* Judicial conduct commissions are constitutionally independent in most states; legislatures are independent of judicial branch. *Acknowledged limitation:* Judicial conduct commissions have historically focused on individual misconduct rather than structural patterns; legislative reform of family court has been incremental rather than systemic; rule-making processes are often dominated by family law practitioners with stakes in current practice.
- *Timeline:* Per body procedures; structural reform typically spans years to decades.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed in policy — no penalty applies. *Acknowledged structural concern:* Parties who file appeals, complaints against judges, or motions for reconsideration may face informal disadvantage in subsequent proceedings before the same judge or in the same court. The court system commits to: random reassignment after successful appeal where practical; formal review of any retaliation complaint; aggregate tracking of post-appeal outcomes for parties who prevailed on appeal.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** Federal courts and judicial conduct commissions are structurally independent. Appellate review is independent but bounded by deferential standards. Legislative reform is independent but historically slow.

*Acknowledged R03 (Institutional Capture) finding:* The family court ecosystem includes private bar (family lawyers), custody evaluators, guardians ad litem, parenting coordinators, and supervised visitation providers whose livelihoods depend on the court's continued use of their services. The framework for evaluating "best interest" is shaped substantially by professional consensus among practitioners with stakes in current practice. R03 finding acknowledged.

*Acknowledged R55/R59 finding:* Coordination among trial court, appellate court, court-appointed evaluators, guardians ad litem, parenting coordinators, supervised visitation providers, and the family-law bar functions operationally as a system. Containment review of any single component is incomplete without addressing the system.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 Type 1 dependencies:**
- Court-appointed custody evaluators (rostered mental health professionals; per-case fees typically borne by parties)
- Court-appointed guardians ad litem (lawyers for children; per-case fees typically borne by parties or court)
- Parenting coordinators (rostered third parties; per-case fees borne by parties)
- Supervised visitation providers (rostered or contracted; per-visit fees borne by parties or court)
- Court interpreters (contracted; court-funded)
- Court reporters and transcript services
- Case management technology vendors
- Family court mediation programs and mediators
- Court-ordered evaluation services (substance use, mental health, parenting capacity)

**6.2 Type 2 dependencies:**
- [STATE] appellate courts
- [STATE] supreme court
- Federal courts (limited)
- [STATE] child welfare agency (when cases cross between private family court and dependency)
- [STATE] domestic violence services and shelters
- Tribal courts (in ICWA cases)
- [STATE] judicial conduct commission
- [STATE] legislature
- [STATE] bar association (admission, discipline, education)

**6.3 Type 3 dependencies:**
- *Court records and case management systems:* If systems fail or records lost, the court cannot demonstrate compliance with retention or appellate-record requirements
- *Roster of qualified evaluators, guardians ad litem, parenting coordinators:* If insufficient qualified rostered professionals, appointments delayed and quality compromised
- *Court interpreter availability:* If absent, hearings cannot proceed for parties with limited English proficiency
- *Self-help center and pro se resources:* The 60–80% self-represented party rate makes self-help infrastructure operationally critical to the court's ability to process cases at all
- *Domestic violence shelter and victim service infrastructure:* Court orders for protection and supervised visitation depend on community infrastructure to be operative

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes. Substantial.

- *Family bar capture:* Private family lawyers shape rule-making, judicial education, and informal practice in ways that may favor practitioners' interests over parties' interests. R03 finding acknowledged.
- *Evaluator capture:* The custody evaluator profession is small and self-regulating; rostered evaluators have stakes in continued use of evaluator-driven decision-making. R03 finding acknowledged.
- *Funding concentration:* 100% [STATE] judicial branch and local court funding; structural to a state court system.
- *Administrative dependence:* The court depends on legislative appropriations; cycles of underfunding produce caseload backlogs that themselves shape practice (delays favoring status quo orders, judges spending less time per case).
- *Vendor lock-in:* Case management and records systems create switching costs.
- *Practitioner-judge revolving door:* Family lawyers becoming judges and judges returning to family practice create relational dependencies.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**
- Records of family court proceedings persist in court records, with confidentiality protections that vary; some records propagate to law enforcement and other databases
- Termination of parental rights orders are permanent and irrevocable
- Custody outcomes during a child's developmental years cannot be undone
- Personnel obligations to judges, court staff, and rostered professionals
- Pending cases at any cessation point would require continuation under successor authority

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:**

The court system coordinates with:

**Acknowledged R55/R59 finding:** The state's family court division, dependency court, and child welfare agency function operationally as a coordinated system for many families. Containment review across these is incomplete if undertaken separately.

**6.7 Disclosure completeness assertion:** [x] Asserted with the standard acknowledgment that complete dependency mapping at this scale is structurally unattainable; informal practitioner relationships and informal precedent within the court are not fully mapped.

**6.8 External authority operationalization:**

- *Custody evaluator recommendations:* Evaluators are not the decision-makers; the judge is. But evaluator recommendations are typically followed in 80–90% of cases by the trial judge. Functionally, evaluator recommendations are operationalized as court authority. R72 applies. Affected parties typically experience the recommendation and the order as functionally identical, though only the order is appealable.
- *Guardian ad litem recommendations:* Similar dynamic. R72 applies.
- *Parenting coordinator decisions:* Where parenting coordinators are empowered to resolve disputes between parents, their decisions are operationalized as court authority subject to limited review. R72 applies.
- *Mental health and substance use evaluations:* External clinicians' diagnostic and risk determinations become court findings about parents. R72 applies; the dual-role tension between forensic and therapeutic relationships is acknowledged.
- *Algorithmic risk assessment tools:* Where used (in some jurisdictions, for domestic violence risk assessment, child welfare risk, or related), tool outputs operationalize as court authority. R72 applies; methodology and validation subject to R67 floor.
- *Child welfare agency investigations and reports:* Where joint cases exist, agency findings operationalize within family court determinations. R72 applies.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** This question is structurally complicated. Family court authority is constitutionally embedded in state judicial branch structure; "dismantling" in the framework's sense would mean substantial restructuring rather than literal cessation, since some authority over family relationships is constitutionally required where state interest in child welfare is engaged.

For framework purposes: 60 months from formal restructuring decision to substantive change, given constitutional, statutory, contractual, judicial-personnel, and case-pendency complexities. Restructuring possibilities include: separation of high-conflict adversarial cases from cooperative cases into different procedural tracks; non-adversarial alternatives; specialized dockets; reduction of evaluator dependence; expansion of representation access; legislative reform of "best interest" standard to be more determinate; alternative configurations involving non-judicial dispute resolution.

**9.2 Dismantling process (interpreted as restructuring):**
- Months 1–12: Legislative or rule-making action; transition planning; identification of restructured authority for continuing functions; pending case continuation planning
- Months 13–36: Phased transition to new structure; rule changes; judicial reassignment; evaluator and guardian ad litem roster transition; pending cases continued under transitional procedures
- Months 37–60: Full operation of restructured system; legacy case completion; outcome monitoring of restructured practice

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Substantial.
- Constitutional embedding of state judicial authority
- Pending cases (~80,000–120,000 in any state at any time) require continuation
- Multi-year statutory and rule-making processes
- Practitioner economy depends on current case-handling structure
- Judicial career structure embedded in current configuration
- Records propagation to law enforcement, child welfare, immigration, school systems cannot be recalled
- Termination of parental rights orders are permanent
- Adoptions completed cannot be reversed

R61 (Anti-Entrenchment) finding acknowledged: state family court authority is structurally entrenched in ways that exceed any single restructuring decision. The framework treats this as a structural finding rather than as the system's individual failure.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Court records archived per [STATE] retention; confidentiality protections continue per court rule; some records propagate to law enforcement and other systems and cannot be recalled
- *Institutional records:* Per [STATE] records law
- *Derived outputs:* Findings and orders propagated to other systems cannot be recalled. R63 finding fully applicable.
- *Audit logs:* Archived per applicable law

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:**

This section requires direct engagement.

The court's actions have caused effects that no restructuring or dismantling can reverse:

- *Children whose childhoods have been determined by court orders* cannot have those years restored. A child who grew up primarily with one parent, in one school district, in one community, under one set of conditions, cannot relive those formative years under different terms. The order may be modified prospectively; the years are gone.
- *Severed parent-child relationships during formative years* cannot be undone. Subsequent reunification, where it occurs, does not restore the missed development, the missed milestones, or the attachment patterns formed in absence
- *Terminations of parental rights are permanent and irrevocable.* The legal parent-child relationship is severed for life. Children adopted following termination form new legal families; the original relationship is not restorable regardless of subsequent change in circumstances
- *Sibling separations* enacted during childhood cannot be fully undone in adulthood
- *Records of family court findings* (alleged abuse, alleged alienation, mental health diagnoses, substance use determinations) propagate into custody cases involving the same parent's other children, into immigration proceedings, into employment screening in some contexts, into the parent's lifelong record. Affected parents may not know which records propagated where. Errors in these records may be uncorrectable
- *Parents who lost custody on the basis of erroneous or biased determinations* may have built lives — new families, new locations, new careers — around the court's determination; subsequent recognition of error does not restore the years
- *Domestic violence victims placed in continued contact with abusers by court order* who suffered subsequent harm cannot be made unharmed by subsequent recognition of error
- *Children placed with abusive parents on the basis of contested findings (parental alienation, etc.)* cannot have the years of harm undone
- *Suicides, breakdowns, and other harms to parties during high-conflict litigation* cannot be undone
- *Professional and economic costs* — careers ended, savings depleted, housing lost during protracted litigation — are not generally recoverable

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) apply with particular force. The court cannot reverse these effects. R65 (Propagation Duty) requires that on restructuring or cessation: (a) the system notify downstream systems that prior findings may not be authoritative; (b) the system support remediation processes for affected parties, including expungement and correction processes for findings entered without due process; (c) the system preserve records to enable historical investigation and remediation.

The framework's reversibility requirement is, for family court, a commitment to mitigate, to acknowledge, and to support remediation — not a commitment that effects can be undone. The gap between the structural authority to cause irreversible harm and the structural capacity to prevent or reverse harm is, in family court authority over children's lives, among the largest of any authority type the framework registers. The fact that this gap is rarely articulated by the court or its practitioners does not reduce its structural reality.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:**

R51 (Institutional Replacement Feasibility) requires that this authority be replaceable without destabilizing services beyond authorized scope.

Family court authority *could* be partially replaced through alternative configurations: non-adversarial mediation for cooperative cases; specialized non-judicial dispute resolution; legislative codification of more determinate standards reducing judicial discretion; reduced reliance on evaluators and guardians ad litem; expanded representation access; expanded pro se infrastructure. Restructuring is possible.

The constitutional core — that state authority over child welfare must be exercised through some adjudicative process — cannot be eliminated; it can only be reconfigured. R51 partial gap acknowledged: constitutional and structural constraints limit dismantling beyond restructuring.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:**

No formal restructuring rehearsal has been conducted by the court system. The court has no operational practice of simulating substantial structural change. R43 (Reversibility Verification) gap acknowledged at full scale.

The §9.1 timeline is estimated based on legislative process, judicial transition complexity, contract obligations, and case-pendency considerations. It is not verified.

The court system acknowledges that R43 applied honestly to family court authority is a substantial commitment, and that the question "what would substantial restructuring actually look like" has historically been treated as politically and professionally inadmissible rather than operationally engaged.
