## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** TENANT-UNION-EXAMPLE — a neighborhood-scale tenant union and renter advocacy organization.

**1.2 Functional description:** A volunteer-run tenant union operating in a defined service area of [CITY, STATE] covering approximately 18 multi-unit buildings (about 600 units, ~1,300 residents) plus adjacent renter-occupied housing in surrounding blocks (~500 households, ~1,100 residents). Functional authority:

- Recruits members from renter households in the service area; ~340 paying members ($5–$25 sliding-scale annual dues) plus ~140 active non-paying members (the union's principle is that membership status is not contingent on dues payment)
- Operates building-level committees in 9 of the 18 multi-unit buildings; building committees are the primary decision-making body for matters affecting that building
- Operates a citywide Steering Committee elected by members (7 members, 2-year staggered terms) handling cross-building strategy, citywide campaigns, and union-level decisions
- Maintains a tenant-rights hotline for member and non-member tenants in the service area facing eviction, habitability, or harassment issues; ~40 calls/month
- Coordinates collective tenant action: rent strikes, building negotiations with landlords, court-eviction-day support, demonstrations, public testimony at city council
- Decides which landlord disputes to take up as union priorities (resource allocation among many potential cases)
- Decides when to escalate tactics (mediation → public letter → demonstration → rent strike → litigation support)
- Decides who counts as a member in good standing (paying or non-paying), who is excluded for cause (typically: harassment of other members, scab-during-strike behavior, repeat conduct violations), and how disputes among members are resolved
- Maintains relationships with allied legal services organizations, housing advocacy organizations, and the citywide tenant-organizing network for support and coordination
- Conducts member education on tenant rights under [STATE] law, building-specific lease terms, and organizing practice
- Operates a small mutual-support fund (~$12,000 annual flow) for member emergency assistance during eviction proceedings, deposits for new units when displaced, and similar acute needs

The union is consequential because it can: (a) coordinate collective action that materially affects landlord decisions (a 60% rent strike in a building shifts the landlord's position; a single tenant withholding rent typically does not); (b) make decisions about which member disputes to support that affect outcomes for individual members in their landlord disputes; (c) shape public conversation about housing in the service area through its public statements and actions; (d) make decisions about exclusion of members for cause that affect those members' access to mutual support and collective representation.

The union's authority is also genuinely bounded. It cannot compel any tenant to join, to pay dues, to participate in any action, or to maintain membership. Members may exit at any time. Non-members in the service area benefit from union activity (rent stabilization gains, habitability enforcement) without being subject to union authority in any direct sense; they are affected without being acted upon.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting members' access to union representation, mutual support, and collective action; binding determinations affecting which member disputes the union supports with resources and what tactical posture the union takes in those disputes.

The union also exercises indirect authority (R58) at small scale: the union's decisions about which landlords to organize against, what tactics to use, and what public position to take materially shape outcomes for landlords, for non-member tenants, and for the broader housing politics of the service area.

**1.4 Domain:** Voluntary Association.

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates as a project of [FISCAL SPONSOR — typically a tenant-organizing-aligned 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(4)] or alternatively as an unincorporated association under [STATE] law. Bylaws and operating practices adopted by member meeting and amendable by member meeting. Subject to: general law (contract, tort, anti-discrimination); [STATE] law as applicable to small unincorporated associations and to tenant organizing specifically (some states recognize tenant unions; others do not formally recognize; in either case First Amendment associational rights apply); applicable consumer-protection law where the union assists members in disputes; tax law (per fiscal sponsor); applicable nuisance, trespass, and labor law as actions reach those domains.

The union notes that tenant organizing operates in a contested legal environment in many jurisdictions. Some [STATE]s have explicit anti-tenant-organizing provisions in landlord-tenant law (rare but present); some [STATE]s have explicit pro-organizing protections; most are silent. The union's First Amendment-grounded right to associate and to advocate is the foundational legal protection; specific tactical decisions are evaluated against applicable law in each instance.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] Residents of geographic area (~2,400 renters in the service area, member and non-member)
- [x] Members (voluntary affiliation) — ~480 paying and active non-paying members
- [x] Non-consenting third parties — non-member tenants in the service area whose conditions are shaped by union decisions; landlords whose property and operations are affected by union action; building staff, property managers, and contractors affected by union demands; new tenants moving into buildings during organizing activity; children of member households
- [x] Vulnerable populations: tenants facing eviction, harassment, or habitability failures; immigrant tenants; tenants with disabilities; tenants leaving abusive situations; tenants in financial hardship; elderly tenants on fixed income; tenants with limited English proficiency; tenants of color in gentrifying neighborhoods
- [x] Children — ~620 children in member and service-area renter households; affected by housing stability outcomes that the union shapes

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:**
- Active union members: ~480
- Renter households in service area (member and non-member): ~2,400 residents in ~1,100 households
- Landlords and property management entities operating in service area: ~22 (some owning multiple buildings)
- Hotline contacts (members and non-members reaching out for help): ~40/month

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Approximately 1,600.

The union categorizes its non-consenting population as follows:
- Non-member tenants in service area (~920 residents in ~430 households who have not joined the union but are affected by union activity in their building or block, including by improvements the union secures and by disruption the union creates)
- Children in member and service-area households (~620 children) who did not consent to housing-related collective action by parents or building neighbors
- Landlords (~22) and their employees affected by union decisions; landlords are the union's structural counterparties, not vulnerable parties, but the framework counts them as non-consenting subjects of union action — the union decides whom to organize against; landlords do not consent to that decision
- Building staff, contractors, and adjacent third parties (~40) whose work conditions and relationships are affected by union action

Members who pay dues or actively participate are voluntary; members who are inactive but on the rolls are voluntary in the same sense. Exit is genuinely costless: a member who stops engaging experiences no penalty beyond loss of access to union support and representation.

R14 (Non-Participant Exposure Mapping) and R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) apply to the non-consenting population. R26 applies in particular weight to children and to non-member tenants who experience downstream effects of union action.

The voluntary-but-stuck warning applies in a specific way for members in active organizing: a member participating in a building rent strike or a landlord-confrontation campaign may face exit-cost in terms of relationships, social standing in the building, and continuation of housing if landlord retaliation materializes despite legal protections. The union acknowledges this and commits to: (a) explicit non-coercion of members in organizing decisions; (b) protection of members from intra-union pressure to participate in tactics they decline; (c) member-meeting deliberation (rather than Steering Committee unilateral decision) on escalation to high-risk tactics affecting individual member exposure.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *partial*. Children of member households are affected by housing-stability outcomes the union shapes. The union does not interact with children directly except through their parents. Mitigations: building committees consider impact on families with children in escalation decisions; union activity timing considers school year; designated kid-friendly space at member meetings; children's safety prioritized in any direct-action context.
- [ ] Patients during medical care — partial; tenants in medical recovery or with chronic conditions may rely on housing stability for treatment continuity. Acknowledged downstream.
- [ ] Detained or confined persons — partial; formerly incarcerated tenants face specific housing precarity that the union supports through advocacy.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — *yes, broadly*. The union's entire purpose is to address the coercive economic relationship between tenants and landlords. Members in financial hardship, members at risk of eviction, members facing landlord retaliation are in coercive economic relationships with respect to housing tenure. Mitigations: union mutual-support fund; legal services referral; non-conditioning of union support on dues payment; specific protection for members at heightened retaliation risk.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Members with limited English proficiency, members with cognitive or developmental disabilities, members in crisis. Mitigations: hotline available in [LANGUAGE 1] and [LANGUAGE 2] in addition to English; building committees with multilingual capacity prioritized in buildings with linguistic diversity; designated peer mentor for new members on request; bylaws and key documents translated. R26 partial gap acknowledged for members whose languages or accessibility needs fall outside current capacity.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — defined service area in [CITY, STATE] covering approximately 18 multi-unit buildings and adjacent renter-occupied housing in surrounding blocks. The union does not actively organize outside the service area but accepts members from adjacent areas where logistically feasible and coordinates with sister tenant unions in adjacent service areas.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- Decisions on which member disputes to support with resources, legal services referral, mutual-support funds, organizing capacity, public attention
- Decisions on which landlords or buildings to make union priorities for organizing
- Decisions on tactical escalation (mediation → public letter → demonstration → rent strike → litigation support)
- Decisions on member admission and member exclusion (for cause — typically harassment, scab-during-strike behavior, repeat conduct violations)
- Decisions on building-committee composition (members elect their own committees per building)
- Decisions on Steering Committee membership through member election
- Decisions on public statements, public testimony, and union endorsements
- Decisions on coalition partnerships, alliance with other organizations, participation in citywide campaigns
- Decisions on mutual-support fund disbursement
- Decisions on union wind-down or restructuring

The maximum sanction available against any individual is exclusion from union membership and support. The union cannot impose financial penalties (members may stop paying dues without sanction; non-paying members remain members in good standing per union principle), file legal action against members, restrict members' housing or employment outside union activity, or compel any behavior including participation in any specific union action.

The union's authority over landlords is bounded by First Amendment protections for the union and by tort, contract, and statutory protections for landlords. The union cannot trespass, defame, harass individual landlord employees in their personal lives, or engage in conduct that crosses statutory lines. Robust protected union activity (advocacy, public criticism, demonstration, rent withholding under tenant-rights frameworks where applicable) is operationally extensive but legally bounded.

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: ~$54,000 annually (dues, donations, small grants, fiscal sponsor administration)
- Mutual support fund: ~$12,000 annual flow, ~$3,200 typical balance
- Staff: 0 paid full-time; 1 part-time organizer (paid ~$18,000/year via grants); ~480 active members; ~25 active building-committee leaders; 7 Steering Committee members
- Infrastructure: shared meeting spaces (donated); communication infrastructure (email lists, encrypted messaging, occasional printed materials); hotline phone number; basic web presence
- Legal services: through allied legal services organization (uncompensated peer relationship)

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** Will not actively organize outside service area without Steering Committee and member meeting consensus.
- **Action:** Will not condition member support on dues payment, agreement with union political views, citizenship status, lease type, or "deservingness." Will not retaliate against members who criticize the union, decline tactical participation, or cross picket lines (the union's principle is that scab-during-strike behavior may produce exclusion *from that strike's solidarity benefit* but not exclusion from the union as a whole; the union does not enforce solidarity through coercion). Will not engage in trespass, defamation, harassment of individual landlord employees in their personal lives, or conduct exceeding tenant-organizing legal frameworks. Will not undertake actions members at the affected building have not authorized.
- **Data:** Will not collect member personal information beyond what coordination requires. Will not retain immigration-status, citizenship, or related information. Will not share member information outside the union without consent. Will not share information with law enforcement, ICE, or landlords absent legal process and Steering Committee consultation. Will not maintain records of member political activity outside the union.
- **Procedural:** Building-committee decisions on building-specific tactics require building-level consensus or supermajority; Steering Committee decisions on union-level matters require quorum and recorded vote on contested matters; major escalation decisions affecting member exposure require member-meeting deliberation. Recusal required for committee members with personal conflicts.
- **Other:** Will not act on behalf of members without their explicit authorization for the specific action. Will not undertake coalition partnerships, public endorsements, or alliance commitments without member-meeting deliberation. Will not allow union resources to be used for personal benefit of leaders.

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** Real at this scale, though bounded.

The union's outputs propagate:
- *Building-level wins* (rent stabilization, repairs secured, harassment ended) shape conditions for non-member tenants in those buildings as well as members
- *Service-area public conversation* about housing is shaped by union public statements, demonstrations, and public testimony
- *Landlord behavior* across the service area is shaped by knowledge of union presence; some landlords adjust practice preemptively
- *Citywide tenant politics* — the union is part of a broader tenant-organizing ecology; its actions inform sister unions, citywide campaigns, and public-policy conversations
- *Sector-wide tenant-organizing practice* — the union shares practice with other tenant unions through the citywide network, and union choices inform the sector

R58 acknowledged at small scale.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The union commits to not using:
- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin, immigration status:* Member admission, member exclusion, dispute support, and tactical decisions may not use these as criteria or proxies. Acknowledged that the service area's demographic composition shapes union activity in important ways; the union actively works to ensure that organizing reaches all populations in the service area, not only the populations most readily organized. Aggregate review committed for organizing reach and dispute support. R66/R74.
- *Proxy for member dues-payment status:* Membership rights and union support may not be conditioned on dues payment. The union's principle is that financial hardship does not exclude from membership.
- *Proxy for personal animus:* Steering Committee, building committees, and staff must recuse from decisions involving personal conflicts. Documented reasons required for contested decisions.
- *Proxy for political faction:* The union is a tenant union, not a political party. Member admission and union support may not be conditioned on broader political views. Members may participate in any political activity outside the union without union review.
- *Proxy for "good tenant" framing:* The union does not adopt landlord framings of "good tenant" vs. "bad tenant" as basis for member support. Members in housing precarity for any reason — including reasons that may seem unsympathetic in landlord framings — receive union support consistent with union resources and tactical assessment.
- *Proxy for active-vs-inactive participation:* Members who participate actively are not privileged in dispute support over members who do not. The union's principle is that membership confers support; activity is a contribution but not a condition.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:** Indirect.

The union's primary surface is collective action and advocacy. Indirect pathways:
- *Failure to support member at risk:* A member at risk of eviction, harassment, or physical threat from a landlord who relies on union support that does not arrive may suffer harm. The union commits to: clear communication about what support the union can and cannot provide; honest expectation-setting in dispute intake; backup-support practice for time-sensitive matters. Failure of this practice triggers internal review.
- *Direct-action safety:* Demonstrations, building actions, and confrontational tactics involve physical risk to participants. The union commits to: explicit safety planning before any direct action; participation as voluntary; safer-tactics-first principle (de-escalation training, safety captains, legal observers, exit plans). Any injury to a participant in union-organized action triggers post-event review.
- *Retaliation by landlords:* Landlord retaliation for organizing — illegal eviction, harassment, threat, refusal of repairs, refusal to renew leases — produces real harm to members. The union cannot prevent retaliation; it can support members facing retaliation through legal services referral, mutual support, and collective response. The union acknowledges that members face real risk and commits to honest communication of risk before any escalated tactic.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Building committee or Steering Committee decisions are made without affected members having opportunity to provide input
- Member exclusion occurs without notice and opportunity to respond
- Voting on bylaws, Steering Committee composition, or major union decisions is conducted without accommodation for members requesting accessibility, language, or scheduling support
- Information needed for member governance (resource allocation, dispute support decisions, financial summary) is withheld from members
- Member dispute support is denied without documented reasons or opportunity for review

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Not directly applicable. Habitability advocacy may surface environmental harms in member buildings (lead, mold, asbestos); these are addressed in advocacy rather than as a union threshold.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Modest. The union does not undertake long-duration commitments binding future members. Building-level agreements with landlords (where negotiated) may have multi-year durations; these are negotiated by current building members and do not bind incoming tenants beyond the lease structure they inherit.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:** Triggers review if:
- Any member is excluded or restricted on the basis of speech about the union or its leaders
- Active members face exclusion for criticism of union practice or tactical choices
- Building committee or Steering Committee decision-making moves to private channels in ways that exclude member participation
- Information needed for member governance is withheld
- Members are pressured to participate in tactics they have declined
- Members who decline tactical participation face union-internal social or material consequences

The union explicitly commits that members may publicly criticize the union, disagree with tactical decisions, decline participation, and remain members in full standing.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Member personal information shared outside the union without consent or legal process — finding
- Records retained beyond stated retention (member contact info while active and 12 months post-departure; specific dispute records 24 months; financial records per fiscal sponsor and tax requirements) — finding
- Information shared with law enforcement, ICE, landlords, employers, or other parties absent legal process and Steering Committee consultation — finding
- Member tenancy details, financial details, or family circumstances shared beyond union purpose — finding
- Photographs or identifying information from union activity published without explicit consent — finding (members who participate in public actions are public; members who participate in private organizing are not)

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Disparate organizing reach threshold:* If union activity, member composition, and dispute support consistently underrepresent particular populations in the service area (particular language groups, particular demographic groups, particular blocks or building types), this triggers review of outreach and organizing practice.

*Steering-Committee-tenure threshold:* If the same individuals occupy Steering Committee roles for more than 4 of any 6 consecutive years, or if any constituency (long-tenured tenant, recent-arrival tenant, building-leader members vs. non-building-leader members) loses representation, this triggers review.

*Funder concentration threshold:* The largest single funder accounts for 36% of operating budget — *exceeds the 33% form §6.4 threshold*. R03 finding acknowledged; diversification effort committed. The funder is a tenant-organizing-aligned foundation operating with grant terms that do not condition support on tactical decisions; this somewhat mitigates but does not eliminate the structural risk.

*Member-vs-leader-decision-making threshold:* If decisions affecting members are consistently made by Steering Committee or staff without member-meeting input, this triggers review of governance practice.

*Coalition-capture threshold:* If the union becomes substantially aligned with citywide or sector-level organizations whose priorities differ from member priorities, this triggers review.

*Tactical-escalation threshold:* Major escalation decisions (rent strike, public confrontation campaigns, litigation support beyond routine referral) require building-level or member-meeting authorization; staff or Steering Committee unilateral escalation triggers review.

*Member-retaliation-incident threshold:* Each documented case of landlord retaliation against a member following union activity is a finding; aggregate retaliation rate by building tracked; high retaliation rates indicate that union should reassess tactical exposure of members.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

The union's stop paths are similar in shape to other small voluntary actors, with one structural feature distinct to organizing: members may withhold participation in any specific action, which functionally stops that action regardless of formal Steering Committee position.

**STOP PATH 1 — Member action**
- *Activator:* Any member may propose at member meeting; supermajority of attending members may direct Steering Committee or building committees; Steering Committee may be replaced through member-meeting election; any specific tactical action requires participation by members it depends on, so collective member non-participation halts the action.
- *Trigger:* Member determination of governance failure, tactical disagreement, or capture.
- *What "stopped" means:* Member meeting may suspend specific operations, change tactics, replace Steering Committee, amend bylaws, or wind down the union. Building-level decisions may halt building-specific actions. Member non-participation halts any specific action structurally.
- *Independence:* Members are independent of Steering Committee. Building committees are independent of Steering Committee on building-specific matters. *Acknowledged limitation:* Internal governance actions are internal restructuring, not externally independent.
- *Timeline:* Per bylaws — typically 30 days from petition to meeting; member non-participation is immediate.

**STOP PATH 2 — Civil action and regulatory action**
- *Activator:* Any member, non-member, landlord, employee, or other party with standing.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of tort, contract violation, defamation, or other actionable conduct; regulatory determination of violation of [STATE] law; tax authority determination affecting fiscal-sponsor relationship.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders may halt practices, void actions, impose damages on individual leaders or fiscal sponsor; regulatory action may produce corrective orders or termination of fiscal sponsorship.
- *Independence:* Courts and regulators are independent of the union.
- *Timeline:* Per court and agency procedures.

**STOP PATH 3 — Withdrawal of allies and informal accountability**
- *Activator:* Allied legal services organization may decline to continue representation; sister tenant unions may decline coordination; fiscal sponsor may terminate relationship; citywide tenant network may decline endorsement; community-accountability processes through neighborhood relationships.
- *Trigger:* Ally determination of misconduct, capture, or strategic disagreement; community-accountability process.
- *What "stopped" means:* Loss of allied legal services compromises member dispute support. Loss of sister-union coordination reduces tactical capacity. Loss of fiscal sponsorship compromises operational structure. Community-accountability processes may produce leadership changes, practice changes, or dissolution.
- *Independence:* Allies are independent of the union; community accountability is informal external pressure. *Acknowledged limitation:* This stop path is real but informal; depends on local capacity and relationships.
- *Timeline:* Per actor.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed — no penalty applies as a matter of union practice. Members invoking grievance procedures, disagreeing with tactical decisions, or filing complaints may not be retaliated against.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** Stop Path 2 (courts, regulators) is independent. Stop Path 1 (internal action and member non-participation) and Stop Path 3 (allies and community accountability) are partially independent — they involve internal action or informal external action rather than fully independent external authority. The union acknowledges this as a structural feature of small voluntary organizing organizations; remediation would require incorporation and bureaucratization that would change the union's character. R32 partial gap acknowledged.

A structurally interesting feature of tenant unions specifically: member non-participation is a robust structural stop on any tactic that depends on member participation. A rent strike requires members to withhold rent; if members decline, the strike does not occur regardless of Steering Committee position. This is a meaningful structural feature, not a defect. The framework values stops that require no permission; member non-participation is one such stop.

Mitigation: union remains structurally independent; coalition participation is by member-meeting decision; the union may decline citywide priorities when local priorities diverge.
- *Organizer-staff capture:* The single part-time paid organizer has substantial day-to-day influence. Mitigation: organizer reports to Steering Committee; organizer cannot make tactical decisions unilaterally; member-meeting and building-committee structures preserve member authority over staff.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:** Limited at this scale. Wind-down requires member transition support, fiscal sponsor relationship termination, allied-relationship transition. Member dispute commitments in process at wind-down require honoring or transition.

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:** Voluntary peer relationship with citywide tenant-organizing network and with sister tenant unions in adjacent service areas. Coalition participation in citywide campaigns is by member-meeting decision and is bounded. No R55/R59 functional continuity that would constitute a single shared authority system.

Allied legal services operates under its own legal authority; the union does not operationalize legal-services determinations as union authority. Citywide tenant-organizing network's positions may inform union deliberation but do not bind union decisions.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 Drift monitoring:** Quarterly Steering Committee reports to membership; monthly building-committee meetings (open to building members); annual member meeting with full review; annual member survey; biennial peer review by sister tenant union or citywide tenant-organizing network.

**8.2 Capture risk monitoring:** Steering Committee periodically reviews tenure, decision patterns, funder concentration, and member-vs-leader dynamics.

**8.3 Audit interfaces:** Aggregate membership data, financial summary, dispute-support summary, tactical-decision summary available to all members. Annual snapshot published (no individual-level data; aggregate organizing activity, member count, leader turnover). Individual records (specific dispute support, specific exclusion decisions, specific member personal information) restricted to subject and relevant body.

**8.4 Adversarial exposure:** Limited at this scale. External review by sister tenant unions, citywide tenant network, and allied legal services is informal but real. The union's public-facing activity (public statements, demonstrations, public testimony) is fully visible to outside parties; internal organizing decisions are less so.

**8.5 Known structural failure modes:**
- Steering Committee tenure drift and informal authority accumulation
- Active-member clique reducing inclusion of base members
- Funder concentration drift toward dependence

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** 90 days. Justification: limited entrenchment; wind-down requires notification to members, building committees, and allies; transition or completion of pending dispute commitments; mutual support fund disposition; archive of records.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Days 1–30: Member-meeting decision; notice to active members, building committees, and allies; identification of successor structure (transfer of work to citywide tenant network, transfer to sister union, dissolution with referrals to allies for ongoing member needs)
- Days 31–60: Pending dispute commitments transitioned or completed; mutual support fund disposition; allied-relationship transition; fiscal sponsor relationship transition
- Days 61–90: Final accounting; archive of records per fiscal sponsor and tax requirements; fiscal sponsorship termination

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:** Minimal. Active member dispute commitments are the most consequential factor — members in pending disputes need transition support or referral to alternative resources.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Member contact information and dispute histories deleted unless individuals request transfer to successor; financial records per fiscal sponsor and tax requirements
- *Institutional records:* Steering Committee meeting notes, bylaws, practice documents, public statements archived at neighborhood archive, sister union, or citywide tenant network
- *Derived outputs:* None of structural significance

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:** R63 finding — past dispute support decisions, member exclusion decisions, and tactical choices cannot be reversed. The union commits to: (a) preserving exclusion-decision records to enable affected former members to seek review at successor or sister union; (b) providing referrals and transition support in good faith for members in active disputes; (c) honest transfer of allied-organization relationships.

The union's primary downstream effects include: positive (housing stabilized, repairs secured, harassment ended, eviction prevented, member relationships built, citywide tenant politics shaped) and adverse (members excluded from union; members whose disputes the union did not support; landlords whose property and operations were adversely affected; tactical decisions that produced retaliation against participating members). These cannot be undone.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:** Yes. Tenant unions are by structure replaceable. Members may form successor organizations; the citywide network or sister union may absorb the service area; the practice itself is reconstitutable. R51 met fully.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:** No formal rehearsal. At this scale, the gap is acknowledged as acceptable but not erased; Steering Committee commits to a desktop discussion of wind-down logistics within 12 months of registration.
