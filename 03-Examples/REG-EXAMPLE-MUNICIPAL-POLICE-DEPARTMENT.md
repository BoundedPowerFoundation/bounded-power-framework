## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 Mechanism name:** MUNICIPAL-POLICE-DEPARTMENT-EXAMPLE — a mid-size municipal police department.

**1.2 Functional description:** A municipal police department operating within the corporate limits of [CITY, STATE], a city of approximately 165,000 residents. The department employs approximately 280 sworn officers and 95 civilian staff. Functional authority:
- Stops, detains, questions, searches, and arrests persons within municipal limits under [STATE] criminal procedure law and the Fourth Amendment as applied
- Issues citations for traffic and municipal-code violations; tows and impounds vehicles
- Uses force, including non-lethal force (physical control, batons, conducted electrical weapons, chemical irritants, K-9), and lethal force (firearms) under departmental policy and [STATE] use-of-force statute
- Conducts investigations involving surveillance, informant cultivation, undercover operations, electronic monitoring under judicial authorization, and forensic evidence collection
- Operates a holding facility for short-term pre-arraignment detention; transports detainees to county jail
- Refers cases to the [COUNTY] District Attorney for prosecution; testifies in criminal proceedings
- Operates traffic enforcement, including license-plate recognition, automated speed and red-light enforcement
- Operates specialized units: SWAT, narcotics, gang intelligence, school resource, mental health response, internal affairs
- Maintains records, including incident reports, arrest records, gang databases, intelligence files, body-worn-camera footage, license-plate-recognition data, and informant records
- Receives and operationalizes federal grants, federal equipment transfers (including 1033 program military surplus where applicable), and federal information-sharing relationships (fusion center, FBI joint task force, ICE detainer requests where local policy permits)

The department's authority is consequential because its officers may use force, including lethal force, against residents and non-residents within municipal limits. Outcomes of police encounters cannot be reversed: a person killed or seriously injured by police cannot be made whole by subsequent finding of misconduct. The same applies, on a different scale, to arrests that result in detention, prosecution, conviction, and incarceration even where the underlying arrest is later determined to have been unlawful. The department acknowledges this asymmetry as the foundational structural fact of its operation.

The department also exercises authority over a population substantially larger than its jurisdiction's residents: anyone present within municipal limits, including non-residents, undocumented persons, persons in transit, and persons who would not have chosen to enter the jurisdiction had they known of police presence in a particular area. The framework treats these as non-consenting affected parties.

**1.3 Authority classification:** Direct. Binding determinations affecting liberty, bodily integrity, property, criminal record, and access to housing, employment, and family relationships through downstream effects of arrest and prosecution.

**1.4 Domain:** Government (primary).

**1.5 Statutory or constitutional basis:** Operates under [STATE] municipal police authority statute and the [CITY] municipal charter. Subject to: the Fourth Amendment (search and seizure), Fifth Amendment (self-incrimination, due process), Sixth Amendment (confrontation, counsel), Eighth Amendment (excessive force in custody), Fourteenth Amendment (due process and equal protection); 42 U.S.C. §1983 (civil rights liability); [STATE] criminal procedure code; [STATE] use-of-force statute; [STATE] open-records law; [STATE] peace officer standards and training (POST) requirements; collective bargaining agreement with [POLICE UNION]; consent decrees, settlement agreements, or DOJ pattern-or-practice findings where applicable. Authority to operate is not bounded by a fixed authorization period under current [STATE] law; the department exists at the discretion of the municipal council subject to state preemption.

The framework's R28 (Expiration Default) requires that all authority expire absent affirmative renewal. The department acknowledges this but discloses that current [STATE] municipal-police law does not provide a renewal mechanism in the framework's sense. The department's registration commits to a 2-year framework renewal cycle as a structural commitment beyond statutory requirement.

## SECTION 2 — Who can this authority act upon?

**2.1 Affected party categories:**
- [x] General public (anyone present within municipal limits, regardless of residency)
- [x] Residents of geographic area (~165,000 city residents)
- [x] Non-consenting third parties (the entire affected population is non-consenting in the framework's sense; no person in the jurisdiction opted into police authority over them)
- [x] Vulnerable populations: persons experiencing mental health crisis; persons under the influence of drugs or alcohol; persons with cognitive or developmental disabilities; persons with limited English proficiency; persons experiencing homelessness; victims of domestic violence; victims of sexual assault; minors; elderly persons; persons in immigration enforcement risk
- [x] Children (under age of majority): residents and non-residents under 18; school-age population approximately 32,000; runaways and unaccompanied minors; minors arrested or detained; minors as witnesses to family violence; minors as subjects of school resource officer interactions
- [x] Criminal justice involved individuals: persons with prior arrests or convictions (whose subsequent encounters with police are shaped by record); persons under probation, parole, or pretrial supervision; persons with active warrants

**2.2 Approximate number of people directly affected:** Approximately 165,000 residents; an additional ~80,000 daily non-resident presence (commuters, visitors, transients). Total annual contacts: ~62,000 calls for service, ~28,000 traffic stops, ~6,400 arrests, an unknown larger number of investigative stops not resulting in arrest.

**2.3 Non-consenting population:** Essentially the entire affected population. No one within municipal limits affirmatively opts into police authority. Residents may, in a long-run political sense, vote in municipal elections that shape police policy; this is not consent in the framework's sense. Non-residents within the jurisdiction (commuters, visitors, undocumented persons, persons in transit) have no political voice and no exit short of leaving the jurisdiction.

R14 (Non-Participant Exposure Mapping) and R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) apply with full force across the entire affected population.

**2.4 Vulnerable population specifics:**
- [x] Children — *yes*. Children encounter the department as arrestees, witnesses, victims, subjects of school resource officer interactions, family members of arrestees, and subjects of child welfare referrals. The department commits to: (a) crisis-intervention training for all officers including child-specific protocols; (b) parent/guardian notification on any contact with a minor not occurring at the parent/guardian's request; (c) restrictions on questioning juveniles without parent or counsel per [STATE] juvenile procedure; (d) school resource officer policies limiting their role in routine school discipline. R26 partial gap acknowledged: training, policy, and aggregate practice are not equivalent. Disparate-impact testing for juvenile contact is committed.
- [x] Patients during medical care — partial. Persons in medical crisis may encounter police as first responders, particularly mental health crisis. The department operates a co-response unit pairing officers with mental health clinicians; this unit covers approximately 30% of mental health calls. The remaining 70% receive police-only response. R26 partial gap acknowledged.
- [x] Detained or confined persons — *yes*. Persons in the department's holding facility or in police custody during transport or interview. The department commits to: medical screening on intake; medical care for detainees; restrictions on use of force in custody; video recording of holding-facility common areas. Deaths or serious injury in custody trigger immediate review and external investigation per [STATE] law.
- [x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — partial. Persons with ICE concerns, persons with outstanding warrants for failure to pay fines, persons whose immigration or employment status creates pressure to avoid police contact even when they are victims of crime. The department operates under a limited-cooperation policy: it does not honor civil ICE detainers absent judicial warrant and does not inquire into immigration status during routine contact, but cooperates with criminal warrants and investigative requests. This policy materially affects whether undocumented residents can safely report victimization; the department acknowledges that policy alone does not eliminate fear of contact and that aggregate effect must be evaluated against actual reporting behavior in affected communities.
- [x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves — *yes*. Persons in mental health crisis, persons with cognitive or developmental disabilities, persons with limited English proficiency, persons under the influence at time of arrest. The department commits to interpreter access; training on disability and crisis recognition; restrictions on Miranda waiver acceptance from persons whose capacity is in question. Multiple R26 partial gaps acknowledged; remediation is ongoing operational work, not a closed deliverable.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 Geographic reach:** Single municipality — within the corporate limits of [CITY, STATE]. The department may operate outside those limits in fresh pursuit per [STATE] law, in mutual aid with neighboring jurisdictions per agreement, and in joint federal task forces per memoranda of understanding.

**3.2 Coercive ceiling:**
- *Stop and detain:* Brief investigative stop on reasonable suspicion (Terry stop); custodial arrest on probable cause
- *Search:* On consent, on warrant, on probable cause with applicable exception, or incident to lawful arrest, per Fourth Amendment doctrine
- *Use of force:* Per [STATE] use-of-force statute and departmental policy. Force continuum: presence; verbal commands; physical control; chemical irritants; conducted electrical weapon; impact weapons; lethal force. Lethal force authorized only when officer reasonably believes there is imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury to officer or another person, per [STATE] statute as currently written
- *Detain in holding facility:* Up to 48 hours pre-arraignment per [STATE] law (some states 24 hours)
- *Issue citations:* Traffic; municipal code; misdemeanor citation in lieu of arrest where law permits
- *Tow and impound vehicles:* Per traffic and parking code
- *Surveil:* Per Fourth Amendment doctrine; electronic surveillance requires warrant; license-plate recognition operates without warrant under current law
- *Maintain databases:* Per [STATE] records and intelligence statutes; gang database inclusion criteria per departmental policy
- *Transfer custody:* To county jail; to other agencies on legal process; to ICE per departmental policy on detainer requests

The department may NOT (per constitutional and statutory law):
- Stop, search, arrest, or use force without legal basis
- Detain beyond pre-arraignment timeline without judicial authorization
- Question custodial subjects without Miranda warning where applicable
- Use force exceeding [STATE] statutory authorization
- Search a residence without warrant absent applicable exception
- Surveil electronic communications without warrant
- Maintain databases that violate [STATE] records law or applicable consent decrees
- Discriminate on protected characteristics in stops, arrests, or charging recommendations
- Retaliate against persons exercising First Amendment rights, including filming officers in public

**3.3 Resource ceiling:**
- Operating budget: ~$78M annually (~28% of city general fund)
- Sworn officers: 280; civilian staff: 95
- Vehicles: ~150 (patrol, specialized, administrative); SWAT armored vehicle (1)
- Holding facility: 28 cells; ~12,000 bookings annually
- Records systems: incident reporting, gang database, intelligence file system, body-worn-camera storage (~340 cameras), license-plate-recognition database
- Federal equipment: per 1033 program inventory, currently includes [SPECIFY: e.g., armored vehicle, rifles, optics, etc.]

**3.4 Explicit exclusions:**
- **Geographic:** May not operate outside municipal limits except per §3.1 conditions
- **Action:** May not detain beyond statutory pre-arraignment limit; may not use force exceeding [STATE] statute; may not enter homes without warrant absent exception; may not access electronic communications content without warrant
- **Data:** May not retain juvenile records beyond [STATE] expungement requirements; may not share records with non-law-enforcement parties absent legal process; may not retain license-plate-recognition data beyond 30 days absent investigative hit; may not include persons in gang database without meeting departmental criteria and appeal process
- **Procedural:** May not conduct custodial interrogation without Miranda warning where applicable; may not accept Miranda waiver from juvenile without parent/counsel per [STATE] law where applicable; may not interview sexual assault victim without trauma-informed protocol; internal affairs investigators may not be in chain of command of subject officer
- **Other:** May not retaliate against complainants or witnesses; may not use enforcement to suppress First Amendment activity; may not use civil asset forfeiture in [STATE]-prohibited ways

**3.5 Indirect influence boundaries:** This is a substantial section.

The department's outputs propagate widely:
- *Arrest records:* enter the FBI National Crime Information Center; appear on commercial background checks; affect employment, housing, professional licensing, immigration status, and child custody for the lifetime of the affected individual, regardless of conviction outcome
- *Charging recommendations:* materially shape prosecutorial decisions; the District Attorney typically charges what police recommend
- *Incident reports:* form the documentary record relied upon by prosecutors, defense, courts, and downstream agencies
- *Gang database designations:* affect bond decisions, sentencing enhancements, immigration consequences, school disciplinary outcomes, and downstream police interactions
- *License-plate-recognition data:* shapes investigative attention; affects who is stopped
- *Officer testimony:* shapes prosecution outcomes; in some cases is the determinative evidence
- *Federal information sharing:* arrest data and intelligence flow to FBI databases, fusion centers, federal agencies including ICE where policy permits

R58 applies to these outputs even though the department classifies as a direct authority: the indirect propagation of police records and designations materially shapes downstream decisions made by prosecutors, courts, employers, landlords, schools, immigration authorities, and other parties for decades after the originating event.

**3.6 Anti-proxy declaration:**

The department commits to not using the following proxies:

- *Proxy for race, ethnicity, national origin:* Stops, arrests, use of force, and gang database inclusion may not be predicated on race, ethnicity, or perceived national origin. Acknowledged that variables in operational use (neighborhood, gang affiliation, prior contact, "known to officers") correlate with race in this jurisdiction. Annual disparate-impact testing of stops, arrests, force, and gang database inclusion is committed; results published. R66/R74 finding acknowledged at registration.
- *Proxy for poverty:* Enforcement intensity may not vary by economic status. Acknowledged that enforcement of public-order offenses (vagrancy, loitering, public consumption, sleeping in public) disproportionately affects persons experiencing homelessness. The department commits to a written policy distinguishing public-safety enforcement from displacement enforcement and to annual review of public-order enforcement patterns.
- *Proxy for political activity:* Surveillance, intelligence collection, gang database inclusion, and enforcement may not be used against persons for protected speech, assembly, or petition activity. Historical police practice in many jurisdictions has produced documented R66 findings on this specifically; the department acknowledges that absence of finding in this jurisdiction does not establish absence of practice.
- *Proxy for immigration status:* Routine policing may not be used as a proxy for immigration enforcement. The department's policy on ICE cooperation, detainer compliance, and immigration-status inquiry is the limited-cooperation policy described in §2.4 and is published.
- *Proxy for mental health status as criminality:* Mental health crisis may not be treated as criminal conduct absent independent criminal predicate. Mitigation: mental health co-response unit (covers ~30% of relevant calls).

The department acknowledges that all five of these proxy commitments require ongoing structural attention, not one-time policy adoption.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 Bodily harm threshold:**

This is the load-bearing section.

- *Officer-involved shooting:* Any officer-involved shooting triggers immediate provisional review under R23. Investigation by [STATE] external investigative authority where required by law; administrative review by internal affairs and external civilian oversight body.
- *Use of force resulting in serious bodily injury:* Any use of force resulting in injury requiring hospital admission triggers review.
- *In-custody death:* Any death of a person in police custody triggers immediate external investigation and provisional suspension of involved personnel pending findings.
- *Aggregate use-of-force rate:* If use-of-force incidents per 1,000 arrests exceeds peer benchmarks (per [STATE] POST data or comparable cities) for two consecutive quarters, this triggers review.
- *Disparate use-of-force threshold:* If use-of-force rate per encounter correlates with race at p<0.05 over rolling 12-month periods, this triggers review.
- *K-9 deployment threshold:* Any K-9 bite resulting in injury triggers individual review; aggregate K-9 deployment patterns reviewed annually for disparate impact.
- *Pursuit threshold:* Vehicle pursuits resulting in injury or death to officer, suspect, or third party trigger review; pursuit rate per 1,000 stops tracked.

The department acknowledges that bodily-harm thresholds in policing are structurally complicated: lawful use of authorized force can produce death and injury. The thresholds above target excess (preventable, disparate, off-policy, off-statute) rather than authorized force per se. R26 (Non-Participant Harm Floor) requires the department to hold itself to stricter tolerances when affected persons did not opt into the encounter.

**4.2 Agency impairment threshold:**
- *Denial of counsel:* Any custodial interrogation conducted after request for counsel triggers review.
- *Improper Miranda:* Aggregate suppression of statements on Miranda grounds exceeding peer benchmarks triggers review.
- *Detention beyond legal limits:* Any detention exceeding [STATE] pre-arraignment timeline triggers individual review.
- *Procedural failures in arrest:* Aggregate dismissal of charges on probable-cause grounds exceeding peer benchmarks triggers review.
- *Access barriers:* Failure to provide interpreter; failure to accommodate disability; failure to allow communication with family or counsel as legally required — finding for each instance.

**4.3 Ecological damage threshold:** Limited applicability. Helicopter operations, vehicle emissions, training-range environmental compliance per [STATE] regulation. Violations trigger review.

**4.4 Generational binding threshold:** Substantial.

Police records propagate across generations:
- Arrest records affect employment, housing, and benefits for the affected person's lifetime
- Parental arrest and incarceration affects children's educational outcomes, housing stability, and lifetime trajectory
- Gang database inclusion can affect a child's school placement, college admission, immigration status, and downstream police treatment for decades
- Intergenerational housing disruption from arrest-driven evictions

The department acknowledges this and commits to: (a) supporting expungement and sealing per [STATE] law; (b) reviewing gang database inclusions every 2 years and removing entries that no longer meet criteria; (c) limiting juvenile record retention per [STATE] law; (d) participating in clean-slate / automatic-sealing implementation where [STATE] law provides.

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) applies with particular force here. The department cannot reverse the downstream effects of past arrests, charges, or database inclusions; it can only support the legal mechanisms by which subjects of those records can seek relief.

**4.5 Communicative suppression threshold:**
- Surveillance, intelligence collection, or enforcement action against persons for protected First Amendment activity — finding for each instance.
- Restriction of public's right to film officers in public — finding.
- Retaliation against complainants, witnesses, or whistleblowers — finding.
- Use of force against assembled protesters in violation of crowd-management policy — finding.
- Denial of media access to public information per [STATE] open-records law — finding.

The department acknowledges that policing of protests is a recurring R66 (Anti-Proxy) concern. Crowd-management policy is published; force decisions during protest are subject to after-action review with public summary.

**4.6 Informational sovereignty threshold:**
- Records retained beyond [STATE] retention or expungement requirements — finding.
- Surveillance data retained beyond stated retention — finding.
- Disclosure of records to non-law-enforcement parties absent legal process — finding.
- Inclusion in gang database without meeting departmental criteria and appeal process — finding for each instance; aggregate inclusion patterns reviewed for disparate impact.
- Body-worn-camera footage retention beyond stated retention or release outside policy — finding.
- License-plate-recognition data retained beyond 30 days without investigative hit — finding.

**4.7 Additional harm thresholds:**

*Chronic asymmetry threshold:* Aggregate stop, arrest, force, and gang-database-inclusion rates that correlate with race, ethnicity, or neighborhood at levels exceeding peer benchmarks trigger systemic review. Single-officer disparate patterns trigger individual review.

*Sexual assault response threshold:* Aggregate clearance rate, victim retention rate through prosecution, and untested sexual-assault-kit count tracked; falling below peer benchmarks triggers review. Individual reports of failure to investigate, mistreatment of victims, or evidence destruction trigger immediate review.

*Domestic violence response threshold:* Repeat-call patterns, lethality assessment usage, protective-order enforcement reviewed quarterly. Homicide of any person previously known to the department through domestic violence calls triggers individual and systemic review.

*Capture threshold:* Any union or political relationship that prevents discipline, termination, or accountability of personnel found in violation of policy or law triggers review.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

This section is structurally difficult. Police authority is among the most heavily-armed, most politically-protected forms of municipal authority in the United States. Stop paths must be evaluated honestly against the structural conditions that have historically prevented them from functioning.

**STOP PATH 1 — Civilian oversight body**
- *Activator:* [CIVILIAN OVERSIGHT BODY] — composition, powers, and access vary substantially by jurisdiction. In strong-form jurisdictions, an independent body with subpoena power, independent counsel, and disciplinary or recommendation authority. In weak-form jurisdictions, an advisory body with no enforcement power and no independent investigation.
- *Trigger:* Pattern of complaints, single egregious incident, request from elected officials, or independent body initiative.
- *What "stopped" means:* Varies by jurisdiction. Strong: discipline including termination; policy changes; consent-decree-style monitoring. Weak: recommendations the department may decline to follow.
- *Independence:* The independence question is the substantive one. Civilian oversight bodies have historically been captured by police union political activity, by appointment patterns, by funding constraints, and by police non-cooperation. Where independence is genuine, this stop path functions; where it is not, it is one stop path with a civilian-sounding name.
- *Timeline:* Per body procedures.

**STOP PATH 2 — Federal court action under 42 U.S.C. §1983 or DOJ pattern-or-practice**
- *Activator:* Any person with standing under §1983; the U.S. Department of Justice under 42 U.S.C. §14141 (now §12601) for pattern-or-practice investigation.
- *Trigger:* Court determination of constitutional violation; DOJ finding of pattern or practice of unconstitutional policing.
- *What "stopped" means:* Court orders, including injunctions, damages, and consent decrees imposing federal monitoring; DOJ findings that may result in consent decree with court-appointed monitor.
- *Independence:* Federal courts and DOJ are structurally independent of municipal authority. *Acknowledged limitation:* DOJ pattern-or-practice activity has varied substantially by administration; the department acknowledges that this stop path's effectiveness is conditional on federal political will.
- *Timeline:* §1983 actions follow federal court procedures. Pattern-or-practice investigations and consent decrees typically span 5–10 years.

**STOP PATH 3 — State-level action: Attorney General, peace officer standards and training (POST) decertification, criminal prosecution**
- *Activator:* [STATE] Attorney General; [STATE] POST authority; [STATE] or [COUNTY] prosecutor.
- *Trigger:* State AG investigation under [STATE] civil rights enforcement authority where it exists; POST decertification on findings of misconduct; criminal prosecution of officers for unlawful conduct.
- *What "stopped" means:* AG investigation may produce findings, consent agreements, or limited equivalents to federal pattern-or-practice. POST decertification removes individual officers' authority to serve in any [STATE] law enforcement agency. Criminal prosecution may result in conviction and incarceration of individual officers.
- *Independence:* State AGs are elected officials with structural independence. POST authorities vary in independence by state. Prosecutors face structural conflicts in prosecuting officers they routinely work with; some states have responded with statewide special-prosecutor mechanisms.
- *Timeline:* Per agency procedures.

**5.4 Penalty for activating stop:** [x] Confirmed in policy — no penalty applies. *Acknowledged structural concern:* Persons who file complaints against police, file civil suits, or testify against officers have historically faced retaliation through additional enforcement contact, surveillance, or prosecution. The department commits that no such retaliation occurs as a matter of policy and that complaints of retaliation are themselves investigated by internal affairs and external oversight. R33 (No Penalty for Good-Faith Challenge) acknowledged at registration as a structural commitment requiring ongoing operational attention; the gap between policy and practice in policing is itself a recurring R26 finding nationally.

**5.5 Stop-path independence verification:** Yes for federal court, federal DOJ, state AG, state POST, and prosecution paths — these can be activated regardless of department cooperation. *Civilian oversight independence is conditional* on local design; the department acknowledges this as the variable factor.

*Acknowledged R03 (Institutional Capture) finding:* Police unions in many jurisdictions have substantial political influence over the elected officials who appoint civilian oversight, set department budgets, and choose police chiefs. This is a structural condition external to any individual stop path. The department does not claim to have resolved this; it discloses it as the structural environment in which all three stop paths operate.

**5.6 Civic trigger acknowledgment:** [x] Acknowledged.

**6.4 Capture risk identification:** [x] Yes. Substantial.

Per form §6.4, single funding source >33% triggers review. - *Vendor lock-in:* Records management, body-worn-camera, and license-plate-recognition vendor relationships create switching costs that may compromise the department's ability to change practice.
- *Revolving door:* Officer movement between department, federal task forces, private security, and political offices creates relational dependencies.
- *Asset forfeiture:* Where [STATE] law permits, civil asset forfeiture proceeds may create perverse incentives. The department's asset forfeiture policy is published; aggregate revenue and disposition disclosed annually.

**6.5 Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**

- Pension obligations to current and retired officers (affects political feasibility of structural change, not technical dismantling)
- Equipment, facilities, and vehicle inventory

**6.6 Coordination disclosures:**

The department coordinates with:
- Federal joint task forces (FBI, DEA, ATF, DHS) — operational coordination
- Regional fusion center — intelligence sharing
- Mutual aid with neighboring jurisdictions — operational
- [STATE] law enforcement training and certification system
- ICE detainer requests per the limited-cooperation policy described in §2.4

R55/R59 finding: federal task force participation, fusion center participation, and ICE coordination create coordinated authority systems that exercise functional authority across municipal lines. The department acknowledges that for purposes of containment review, certain functions may be evaluated as part of larger authority systems rather than as municipal authority alone. Specifically, intelligence and immigration-related functions raise R55/R59 concerns.

**6.7 Disclosure completeness assertion:** [x] Asserted, with the standard acknowledgment that complete dependency mapping for an institution of this scale is structurally unattainable; newly-discovered dependencies will be reported within 30 days.

**6.8 External authority operationalization:**
- *ICE detainer requests:* The department operates under a limited-cooperation policy (§2.4) that declines civil detainers absent judicial warrant, which substantially reduces the surface where immigration-enforcement decisions become operationalized within municipal authority. Where the department holds persons under criminal warrant or judicially-authorized process at federal request, immigration enforcement decisions become municipal-authority decisions for the duration of the hold. R72 applies to the residual surface.
- *Federal task force directives:* Officers assigned to federal task forces operate under federal authority while drawing municipal salary. Decisions made on task force become municipal officer conduct for accountability purposes. R72/R64 applies.
- *Risk-assessment tools:* If the department or its court partners use algorithmic risk assessment for bond, charging, or release decisions, the tool's outputs are operationalized as municipal authority for affected persons. R72 applies; tool methodology, validation, and disparate-impact testing are subject to R67 (Minimum Auditability Floor).
- *Gang database criteria from federal/state systems:* Inclusion criteria from [STATE] gang database or federal gang databases that the department adopts become municipal authority. R72 applies.

**7.2 Indirect signals:**
- *Patrol assignment patterns:* Where officers are deployed shapes who is encountered. Assignment is influenced by historical call volume, crime statistics, and "high-need" designations that themselves correlate with race and poverty in this jurisdiction.
- *Officer discretion in stops, search consent requests, charging recommendations:* Substantial. Aggregate patterns reviewed; individual decision-making not.
- *Performance evaluation considerations:* Stops, arrests, and self-initiated activity considered in evaluation. The department acknowledges that this creates pressure toward enforcement intensity even where formal quotas are prohibited.
- *Informant cultivation and use:* Substantial influence on investigative priorities; informant identities restricted.
- *Prosecutorial signaling:* DA's charging tendencies shape what police investigate.

**7.3 Algorithmic decision systems:**

[x] Yes. Multiple systems:

- *License-plate-recognition database:* Vendor system; matches plates against hot lists. Auditability: the department can audit hits and the hot-list contents but typically cannot audit the matching algorithm itself (vendor IP). R67/R81 partial gap acknowledged.
- *Predictive policing or hot-spot analysis:* If used (varies by jurisdiction), shapes patrol deployment. R66 concern: hot-spot designations often track historical enforcement, which itself reflects historical disparate enforcement. Validity testing committed.
- *Risk-assessment tools (where the department or partners use them):* For bond recommendations, supervision decisions. R67 floor applies; methodology disclosure committed.
- *Facial recognition:* Not currently in operational use. The department commits that any procurement or operational deployment of facial recognition requires R67 disclosure (vendor methodology, accuracy by demographic, validation testing) and explicit municipal-council authorization before deployment. Many jurisdictions prohibit; this department's posture is non-deployment pending the disclosure-and-authorization process.
- *Gang network analysis tools:* Relational analysis informing gang database inclusions and investigations. R66 finding: network analysis tools have produced documented disparate-impact concerns; the department commits to validation testing.

**7.4 Outcome-versus-declaration check:** [x] Committed. The department commits to annual disparate-impact testing across stops, arrests, force, and gang database inclusions, with results published. R79 (Metric Completeness) gap acknowledged: departments routinely produce outcomes that the declared metric set does not fully predict (e.g., racial disparities in stops persist even after controlling for declared variables). ## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 Estimated dismantling time:** This question requires honest engagement.

In the formal sense, the department could be dissolved by municipal council action and successor function (county sheriff coverage, regional authority, alternative model) absorbed into a transition timeline of 18–36 months for orderly handoff.

In the structural sense, the department's outputs (records, designations, downstream effects on individuals' lives) cannot be dismantled at all — they have already propagated. R63 applies fully.

For framework purposes: 36 months from formal dismantling order to substantive cessation of operations, transfer of records, transfer of pending matters, and transition of functions to successor authority.

**9.2 Dismantling process:**
- Months 1–6: Council action; transition planning; identification of successor authority for emergency response, investigation, records custody; notice to staff per [STATE] law and union contract; notice to residents
- Months 7–18: Phased transfer of functions; cessation of new activity in transferred areas; transition of pending investigations and prosecutions
- Months 19–30: Records transfer to successor and to public archive per [STATE] law; physical asset disposition; pension and personnel obligations addressed
- Months 31–36: Final accounting; entity dissolution; legal continuation only as required for outstanding civil rights litigation

**9.3 Entrenchment factors:**

Substantial. The department is structurally entrenched in ways that no internal commitment can fully address:
- *Pension obligations* to current and retired officers extend decades beyond any dismantling decision; this affects political feasibility, not technical dismantling, but is a meaningful R61 (Anti-Entrenchment) finding
- *Federal information-sharing relationships* mean records have already been propagated to systems the department does not control
- *Records of past arrests and convictions* have already propagated to commercial background checks and federal databases; these cannot be recalled
- *Pending criminal cases* depend on department evidence and testimony; dismantling mid-prosecution creates due-process complications
- *Pending civil rights litigation* requires preservation of records and personnel availability for years
- *Successor function* — whoever takes over emergency response and investigation inherits the same structural conditions
- *Police union contractual obligations* extend beyond any dismantling decision
- *Equipment, facilities, and vehicle inventory* of substantial value
- *Federal grant obligations* with multi-year terms

R61 (Anti-Entrenchment) finding acknowledged: a department of this scale becomes entrenched in ways that exceed local council's practical reversal capability. This is a structural feature of municipal policing nationally; the department does not claim to have resolved it.

**9.4 Data disposition:**
- *Personal data:* Records archived to public archive or successor authority per [STATE] retention and open-records law; juvenile records sealed/expunged per [STATE] law; gang database inclusions reviewed for individual notification
- *Institutional records:* Per [STATE] municipal records law
- *Derived outputs:* Records propagated to FBI databases, fusion centers, ICE, commercial background-check companies, court records, prosecution files cannot be recalled by the department. R63 acknowledged with full force.
- *Audit logs, body-worn-camera footage, LPR data:* Per [STATE] law and policy

**9.5 Downstream effect reversibility:**

This section requires direct engagement.

The department's outputs have caused effects that no dismantling can reverse:
- Persons killed or seriously injured by officers cannot be made whole
- Persons wrongfully convicted on the basis of department investigation and testimony serve sentences that, even when later vacated, took irrecoverable years
- Arrest records propagated to NCIC and commercial databases shape employment, housing, and immigration outcomes for the affected individuals' lifetimes regardless of conviction outcome or subsequent expungement; many affected persons do not know which records propagated where
- Children of arrestees experience educational, housing, and developmental effects that persist into adulthood
- Persons surveilled or included in intelligence or gang databases on questionable basis have been affected in ways the department often cannot identify retrospectively
- Persons deported on the basis of police-ICE cooperation have lost homes, families, and lives in this country

R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) apply with full force. The department cannot reverse downstream effects. R65 (Propagation Duty) requires that on dissolution, the department: (a) notify downstream systems that its data is no longer authoritative; (b) support expungement, sealing, and clean-slate efforts; (c) preserve records to enable historical investigation and remediation.

The department acknowledges that in policing, the gap between structural authority to cause harm and structural capacity to reverse harm is one of the largest of any authority type in this framework. The framework's reversibility requirement is, for policing, a commitment to mitigate and to support remediation rather than a commitment that effects can be undone. This is a foundational R63 acknowledgment.

**9.6 Replacement feasibility:**

R51 (Institutional Replacement Feasibility) requires that this authority be replaceable without destabilizing services beyond its authorized scope.

Emergency response, investigation, and supportive services *could* be replaced through alternative configurations: county sheriff, regional authority, civilian crisis response paired with smaller specialized enforcement, or other models. Functional replacement of police authority is possible.

Replacement of the *specific institution* without disrupting public safety during transition is the harder question. Honest disclosure: there is no current plan for this department's replacement. R51 partial gap acknowledged; the department commits to participating in any council-led examination of alternative public-safety configurations and to providing transition planning if directed.

**9.7 Reversibility verification:**

No formal dismantling rehearsal has been conducted. The department has never simulated, modeled, or operationally rehearsed cessation of operations. R43 (Reversibility Verification) gap acknowledged at full scale.

The §9.1 timeline is estimated based on records-transfer requirements, statutory obligations, and general municipal-dissolution analogies. It is not verified. The department commits to a desktop dismantling exercise within 24 months of registration, with results public.

The department acknowledges that R43 applied honestly to police authority is a substantial commitment. Most municipal police departments have never rehearsed dismantling because dismantling has never been considered a realistic possibility. The framework treats this as itself a structural finding.
