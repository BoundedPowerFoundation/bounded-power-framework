# Bounded Power

## Structural Constitution for Limiting Institutional Authority

**The Bounded Power Framework**

A structural architecture designed to ensure that institutional authority remains limited, interruptible, reversible, and subject to renewal.

*Bounded Power Foundation*

\---

## Dedication

This is for the people living inside systems they didn't build. For anyone who has watched things get bigger, more complicated, and harder to deal with. For anyone who has had the feeling that something is off, even when everything seems to be working. And for anyone who thinks the future shouldn't be locked in place.

Systems don't turn into a problem overnight. They turn into a problem when they keep going past the point where they can still be changed. This is about not letting it get that far.

\---

## Section 1 — The Problem

This is about something most people already notice, even if they don't have a clean way to say it.

Systems don't usually break all at once. They just keep going. Things get added. Nothing really gets taken away. Temporary becomes normal. Small changes stack. Over time, what started as something specific turns into something bigger, harder to follow, and harder to change.

No single step looks like the problem. The pattern is the problem.

This doesn't come from bad people or hidden plans. It comes from how systems are designed. Once something is in place, it tends to stay in place. Continuation is the default. Stopping requires effort. And after a while, stopping becomes harder than letting things keep going.

There is no identifiable moment when most people enter the systems that govern their lives. No terms are presented. No agreement is signed. The system is already in place. Participation is implicit. By the time it is noticed, it has already been operating for a long time.

Most of it works. That is part of the problem. Enough works that nothing forces a full re-evaluation. At the same time, there are conditions that don't resolve. Effort doesn't always convert to stability. Problems that could be addressed earlier continue long enough to become harder to fix.

This does not show up as a single failure. It shows up as a pattern, spread across different parts of life.

\---

## Section 2 — The Inversion

Most systems are built on an assumption so common it goes unexamined: authority continues unless someone stops it.

That assumption puts all the pressure on people. Catch problems. Push back. Somehow reverse things that have already built up. That gets harder as systems grow and more things depend on them continuing. So most of the time, things just continue.

This framework inverts that assumption.

Authority does not continue by default. It must justify continuation. If it cannot, it ends.

That shift is small. Its effects are not. Instead of everything stacking up, things have to hold their place. Instead of growing by default, authority stays within its defined lane. Instead of becoming permanent, it stays temporary unless someone makes the case to keep it.

This is not a political position. Different people want different outcomes, and that doesn't change. What this addresses is something underneath all of that — how authority behaves once it exists, regardless of who holds it or what it claims to be doing.

\---

## Section 3 — What Safety Actually Means

Safety is not a feeling. It is a structural condition.

A system is safe only if it remains controllable. Control means the ability to stop, examine, correct, and replace. If any of those conditions are missing, the system is not safe — regardless of how well-intentioned it is, how competently it is managed, or how long it has been operating without incident.

This distinction matters because most people think of safety in terms of outcomes — is it causing harm right now? This framework thinks of safety in terms of structure — can it be stopped if it does?

A system that cannot be stopped is unsafe by definition, even if it has never caused harm. A system that cannot be examined cannot be corrected. A system that cannot be replaced cannot be improved.

There is also a distinction worth making between authority and structure. Authority is the power to decide. Structure is what carries out those decisions. A system can have many moving parts — people, processes, vendors, technologies — but those parts are not the source of authority. They execute what authority directs.

Responsibility does not move just because work is distributed. If a system causes harm, the authority that directs it remains responsible, regardless of how many layers sit between the decision and its effects.

Work can be distributed. Responsibility cannot.

\---

## Section 4 — What This Framework Does

This framework does not prescribe outcomes. It does not allocate rights. It does not determine who should govern or what decisions should be made. It does not assume good actors, constant vigilance, or correct interpretation.

It assumes drift, expansion, and evasion — and designs against them structurally.

Three conditions apply to all authority, without exception:

**Power must remain small enough to stop.**  
An authority that cannot be halted is not authority — it is a permanent condition. Stoppability is not a feature. It is the baseline requirement.

**Harm must remain interruptible before it becomes permanent.**  
Some harms can be undone. Others cannot. The framework distinguishes between them and requires that authorities be stopped before harm crosses from recoverable to irreversible.

**Mistakes must remain correctable before they become irreversible.**  
Systems make mistakes. The question is whether mistakes can be caught and corrected, or whether they accumulate until correction is no longer practical.

These are not aspirations. They are structural requirements. When they are met, authority remains within reach. When they are not, authority moves beyond the point where it can still be changed.

## Section 5 — What This Means for Ordinary People

The average person has almost no practical leverage over the systems that govern their daily life. They can vote. They can file complaints. They can, in some circumstances, join class action lawsuits. Beyond that, the tools are thin and the friction is high.

This framework adds something that currently does not exist: a publicly visible, permanently documented record of what every registered authority is allowed to do, when that authorization expires, what conditions would trigger a stop, and how to initiate one.

This matters in two ways.

First, visibility. Right now, if you want to know what a government agency, a private company, or a regulatory body is authorized to do, how long that authorization lasts, and what would trigger a review — that information either doesn't exist in one place, is buried in language that takes specialists to navigate, or simply isn't public. Under this framework, every registered authority has a plain-language public record. That record cannot be silently altered. It is permanent and auditable.

Second, the stop trigger. Any affected party can submit a stop trigger — a structured, documented report that something is wrong. It does not immediately stop anything. What it does is create a permanent, public, timestamped record that a specific concern was raised about a specific authority on a specific date, and that a response is now required within a defined window.

Right now, when an ordinary person raises a concern about an institution, the concern goes into a system that institution controls, is handled at the institution's discretion, and leaves no public trace. Under this framework, the submission is public the moment it is made. The response — or the failure to respond — is also public.

That is not a guarantee of outcomes. But it is something that currently does not exist for most people: a documented voice that cannot be quietly ignored.

## Section 6 — How to Read What Follows

This document is the entry point. Everything after it builds on what has been established here.

**The Foundational Theory** explains in depth why institutions expand, how drift happens, and why structural constraint is the correct response. Read it if you want to understand the reasoning behind the framework before engaging with the rules.

**The Operations Manual** is for anyone who needs to authorize, operate, review, or stop an authority under this framework. It is practical and step-by-step. You do not need a legal background to use it.

**The Structural Constitution** is the only binding document in the framework. It contains the rules. Only its clause text has authority — everything else explains and applies. If anything in this document or the others conflicts with the Structural Constitution, the Structural Constitution governs.

**The Testing Protocol** defines how compliance is evaluated. It is a diagnostic tool, not an enforcement mechanism.

**The Enforcement System** defines what happens when the Testing Protocol identifies a violation. Detection triggers action. There is no discretionary gap.

**The Integration and Adversarial Testing document** defines how real-world authority systems attach to the framework and how the framework is stress-tested against hostile compliance.

One principle runs through all of it: if something conflicts, the narrower authority wins. This framework does not expand authority. It constrains it.
