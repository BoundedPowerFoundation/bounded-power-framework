# Keeping Power Small

## Definitions and First Principles

Power tends to expand when it is left unattended. This document assumes that tendency as an ordinary condition, not a moral failure. The structures described here are shaped to remain small by default, not by ongoing effort, vigilance, or virtue. When power remains small, fewer corrective mechanisms are needed, fewer people are exposed to harm, and fewer irreversible decisions are made.

Power is defined here as the ability to compel outcomes, allocate resources, restrict behavior, or bind future action. Any structure that can do these things is treated as power-bearing, regardless of intention, legitimacy, popularity, or claimed necessity. Naming power does not accuse it. It simply places it within the scope of containment.

Power is kept small by limiting its surface area. Surface area includes scope, duration, reach, speed, and opacity. When any of these increase, power grows even if formal authority does not. The system therefore treats expansion along any dimension as meaningful growth, not as a technical detail.

The smallest unit of power is preferred. When an action can be performed by a smaller unit, it is not assigned to a larger one. This is not an ethical preference. It is a containment measure. Smaller units fail in smaller ways and are easier to stop.

No unit of power is assumed to be permanent. Continuation is treated as an active condition that must remain compatible with stoppability. Expiration is ordinary. Renewal is separate. Nothing continues by momentum alone.

## Surface Area: Duration, Scope, Reach, Speed, Opacity

Duration is capped by default. Power that exists only briefly is less likely to reshape its environment in irreversible ways. Short duration does not imply urgency or exception. It implies limits.

Scope is narrow by default. Power applies only to the specific function it is created to perform. Adjacent problems do not expand scope. Convenience does not expand scope. Efficiency does not expand scope. If a new function appears necessary, it is treated as a separate structure and evaluated independently.

Reach is bounded geographically, materially, and socially. Power does not automatically scale across populations, territories, or systems. Scaling is treated as multiplication, not repetition. Each increase in reach is a new instance of power, not an extension of the old one.

Speed is constrained. Rapid action reduces opportunities to interrupt, review, or reverse. Slower processes are not treated as weaknesses. They are treated as brakes. When speed increases, additional stopping points are required, not fewer.

Opacity is minimized. Hidden power is harder to interrupt. This does not require spectacle or disclosure of sensitive details. It requires that the existence, location, and boundaries of power be knowable to those affected by it. Invisibility refers to absence of dominance, not absence of accountability.

No power is allowed to define its own size. Self-definition leads to drift. Boundaries are structural facts, not interpretations. When ambiguity exists, the smaller interpretation prevails.

## Aggregation, Delegation, Automation, Expertise

Aggregation is treated cautiously. Combining small powers does not necessarily produce a small outcome. Aggregated power is evaluated as a new structure, not as the sum of its parts. If aggregation reduces stoppability, it is rejected regardless of efficiency gains.

Delegation does not dissolve responsibility. When power is delegated, the original structure remains within the chain of stoppability. Delegation is treated as a redistribution of action, not a removal of risk.

Automation does not reduce power. It often increases it by increasing speed and reducing friction. Automated systems are treated as power-bearing entities with the same containment requirements as human-operated ones.

Expertise does not enlarge authority. Knowledge informs action but does not widen scope. Experts operate within constraints they do not control. This is not a statement about trustworthiness. It is a structural boundary.

Power does not accumulate precedent. Past use does not justify future use. Repeated activation does not normalize expansion. Each instance stands alone and is evaluated against the same limits as the first.

Emergency framing does not increase allowable size. Urgency compresses time but does not expand scope. If a situation cannot be addressed within existing limits, it is treated as a signal that the structure is unsuitable, not that the limits should be removed.

## Stopping Mechanisms

Stopping mechanisms are built into the shape of power, not attached afterward. Power that cannot be stopped externally, cleanly, and without negotiation is considered oversized. The absence of a clear stop is treated as failure at the design level.

Stopping does not require explanation. It does not require consensus. It does not require proof of harm. It requires only that the stopping condition be met as defined at creation. These conditions are factual triggers, not judgments.

When power is stopped, it leaves no residual authority. Restart is not automatic. Restart requires re-entry through the same constraints as initial creation. Temporary suspension does not preserve latent control.

If a power-bearing structure begins to grow beyond its defined limits, growth itself is treated as malfunction. The response is contraction or termination, not reform. Repair is not assumed to be possible.

The system assumes that even well-designed limits will sometimes fail. For that reason, power is layered shallowly. No single layer carries enough authority that its failure produces systemic collapse.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** each unit of power is bounded in scope, duration, reach, speed, and opacity; each boundary includes an external stop that does not rely on the unit's cooperation; expiration occurs automatically; aggregation and expansion trigger termination rather than adaptation; and no restart occurs without re-entry through the same constraints.

## Tolerances and Scarcity

Power is not treated as a moral problem to be corrected through oversight or culture. It is treated as a physical quantity that must remain within tolerances. The tolerances are fixed at creation and do not adjust to circumstances, performance, or perceived need.

Creation of power-bearing structures is constrained by scarcity. Scarcity here is structural, not economic. There are limits on how many such structures may exist simultaneously, how many may operate within the same domain, and how many may interact directly. These limits are not optimized. They are intentionally conservative.

Redundancy is preferred to concentration. Multiple small structures performing similar functions are less hazardous than one large structure performing all of them. Redundancy does not imply coordination or hierarchy. It implies parallel capacity without shared control.

Coordination mechanisms are themselves treated as power-bearing. Any system that aligns, synchronizes, or prioritizes the actions of multiple units is evaluated as a separate source of power. If coordination reduces independent stoppability, it is restricted or removed.

Interfaces between power-bearing structures are minimized. Each interface is a potential path for expansion. When interfaces exist, they are narrow, explicit, and asymmetrical toward refusal. Default interaction is non-cooperation.

Power does not inherit authority across contexts. A structure that operates legitimately in one domain carries no implied authority in another. Context boundaries are treated as hard edges, not gradients.

## Drift and Compliance

Measurement does not justify expansion. Metrics are descriptive, not permissive. Improved outcomes do not authorize broader scope, longer duration, or faster operation. Performance is not a lever for growth.

Compliance mechanisms are kept simple. Complexity creates dependency and obscures boundaries. Simple rules fail visibly and are easier to interrupt. Complexity is treated as a growth vector, not as sophistication.

Appeals to alignment, shared goals, or common good do not alter limits. Agreement does not make power smaller. Consensus does not make it safer. These conditions are treated as external to structural containment.

Power does not manage dissent. Disagreement is not a failure mode. Structures that require agreement to function are considered fragile. Power remains bounded regardless of approval or resistance.

Resource accumulation is capped. Control over resources is a primary amplifier of power. Accumulation beyond predefined thresholds triggers automatic shedding or termination. These thresholds are numeric and non-negotiable.

Time-based drift is assumed. Even stable structures tend to expand their interpretations over time. To counter this, definitions are fixed and non-extensible. When reality no longer fits the definition, the structure ends rather than adapts.

Language is not allowed to expand power. Reinterpretation, reclassification, or renaming does not change underlying limits. If a function resembles a prohibited action, it is treated as such regardless of terminology.

Power does not self-correct. Feedback mechanisms inform observers but do not grant authority to adjust boundaries. Correction occurs only through predefined stops or termination paths.

Ownership does not legitimize expansion. Whether power is held by public bodies, private entities, or temporary collectives does not alter its containment requirements. Form does not override function.

Cultural acceptance is not a stabilizer. Normalization increases risk by reducing scrutiny. Familiarity is treated as a warning sign, not a safety indicator.

Power is not preserved for future use. Dormant power is still power. Structures that exist without active function are terminated rather than stored. Potential authority is treated as active risk.

If maintaining small size requires continuous effort, the design is considered faulty. Correct size should persist without intervention. Maintenance effort is not a substitute for containment.

When a power-bearing structure cannot remain small without special care, it is dismantled rather than supported. Support is not neutral. It is a form of expansion.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** scarcity limits prevent proliferation; redundancy avoids concentration; interfaces default to refusal; resource caps trigger automatic shedding; definitions do not stretch; dormant structures terminate; and any drift beyond fixed tolerances results in contraction or dismantling without adaptation.

## Authorization, Refusal, and Trust

Power is not improved by clarity of intention. Good intentions do not shrink impact. For this reason, intent is not recorded, evaluated, or preserved as a stabilizing factor. Only structure matters.

Authorization is limited in form. Power is not granted broadly with internal discretion to narrow it. It is granted narrowly with no discretion to widen it. Where discretion exists, it is bounded by explicit refusals rather than permissions.

Refusal is treated as a first-class outcome. Systems are designed so that saying no, stopping, or declining to act is ordinary and costless. Power that requires action to remain safe is oversized.

Costs of stopping are externalized away from the stopping party. No individual or group bears penalty, liability, or burden for initiating a stop when conditions are met. Stopping is mechanical, not adversarial.

Escalation paths are short. When power exceeds tolerance, it does not move through layers of review. It moves directly to reduction or termination. Delay is treated as silent expansion.

Power does not benefit from interpretation. Ambiguity does not invite judgment. It defaults to the smallest operative meaning. If ambiguity prevents operation, the structure pauses rather than infers.

Training does not substitute for limits. Reliance on skilled operators is treated as fragility. Structures must remain safe when operated by ordinary, inattentive, or fatigued people.

Trust relationships are not load-bearing. Personal reliability, reputation, or track record do not increase allowable size or duration. Trust may ease coordination but does not alter containment.

## Influence, Information, and Necessity

Power does not propagate through advice. Recommendations, guidance, or non-binding influence that predictably shapes behavior at scale are treated as power-bearing when their effects are equivalent to directive control.

Financial incentives are treated as coercive tools. When incentives materially constrain choice, they are regulated as power, not as voluntary exchange. Size limits apply regardless of consent.

Legal form is not protective. Contracts, mandates, or charters do not make power smaller. They only describe it. If description conflicts with function, function governs containment.

Information control is power. Control over data access, visibility, or framing is constrained as tightly as control over action. Information asymmetry is treated as reach.

Power does not survive context collapse. When conditions change such that original assumptions no longer hold, authority does not persist. Structures end rather than reinterpret their mandate.

The system avoids single points of moral decision. No individual is placed in a position where restraint depends on character. When such positions arise, the structure is redesigned or removed.

Power does not benefit from being necessary. Necessity increases risk. When a function becomes indispensable, it is decomposed into smaller, stoppable parts rather than protected.

Failure is not corrected by expansion. When power fails to achieve its purpose, the response is reduction, replacement, or abandonment, not reinforcement.

Records do not extend authority. Archives, logs, or institutional memory do not justify continued operation. Memory informs observers, not power.

When uncertainty increases, power shrinks. Unknowns are treated as reasons to reduce scope and duration, not as reasons to consolidate control.

Power is assumed to be temporary even when problems are persistent. Long-term issues are addressed through sequences of small, expiring structures rather than permanent authority.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** refusal is costless and ordinary; ambiguity collapses toward non-action; escalation bypasses review; trust and intent are non-operative; incentives and information control are bounded; necessity does not protect authority; and increased uncertainty triggers automatic reduction or termination.

## Entrenchment, Continuity, and Soft Power

Power is not allowed to anchor itself by solving problems it helps create. When a structure generates dependency, demand, or secondary effects that require its continued operation, this is treated as self-entrenchment. The response is removal, not accommodation.

Problem definition is constrained. Power-bearing structures do not define the problems they address beyond a narrow, fixed description. When reality exceeds that description, the structure does not expand its mandate. It ends.

Power does not claim guardianship. Protection narratives do not alter limits. Acting "on behalf of," "for the benefit of," or "in the interest of" others does not enlarge scope or duration. Representation is not amplification.

Continuity is not a value. Persistence is neutral at best and hazardous at scale. Structures are not rewarded for longevity. They are expected to end cleanly.

Transition planning does not preserve authority. Preparing for shutdown does not justify extension. Wind-down mechanisms operate within the same limits as active operation.

Power does not accumulate legitimacy through survival. The fact that harm has not yet occurred does not increase tolerance. Absence of failure is not evidence of safety.

Boundary enforcement is external. Structures do not monitor their own size, reach, or impact. External monitors do not direct action; they only trigger stops. Monitoring without stopping authority is informational, not supervisory.

Power does not bargain with its limits. There are no exception clauses, waivers, or discretionary overrides. Limits are not inputs to negotiation. They are conditions of existence.

Soft power is still power. Influence exercised through norms, defaults, or environment design is constrained when it predictably channels behavior. Subtlety does not exempt scale.

Power does not require persuasion to end. Termination is not a verdict. It is a routine outcome when conditions are met. No justification is owed beyond the fact of mismatch with limits.

Reinstatement is not presumed. When a structure ends, the environment is allowed to adapt without it. Absence is not treated as a gap to be filled automatically.

Power does not persist through identity. Renaming, restructuring, or rebranding does not preserve authority. Continuity of function governs, not continuity of name or personnel.

Complex interdependence is avoided. When many systems rely on a single power-bearing structure, stopping becomes costly. This is treated as overgrowth. Dependency itself triggers reduction.

Power does not optimize across generations. Binding future conditions reduces reversibility. Long-horizon commitments are decomposed into short, revisable segments.

Power is not justified by comparison. The existence of larger, harsher, or less constrained powers elsewhere does not relax limits here. Containment is local and non-relative.

Power does not fill silence. Inaction is acceptable. Gaps are tolerated. Not every condition requires intervention.

When harm is uncertain, restraint prevails. The system does not wait for evidence of damage at scale. Early contraction is preferred to late correction.

The section closes without continuity guarantees. Keeping power small is not a phase. It is a constant condition enforced through expiration, refusal, and dismantling.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** self-entrenchment triggers removal; mandates do not stretch; guardianship and legitimacy narratives have no effect; external monitors trigger non-negotiable stops; dependency and interdependence cause contraction; long commitments are segmented; and uncertainty results in restraint and termination.

# Stopping Harm

## Foundations

Harm is treated as an ordinary outcome of systems interacting with people, environments, and time. It is not treated as a failure of character, intent, or care. Because harm is predictable, systems are shaped to make it easier to stop than to justify, manage, or explain.

Harm is defined here as damage that reduces future choice, health, safety, stability, or recoverability. This includes physical, psychological, ecological, and structural damage. Harm does not require malice. It does not require visibility. It does not require consensus.

The system does not wait for certainty. High confidence is not a prerequisite for interruption. When credible pathways to harm exist, reduction begins without proof of outcome. Delay increases exposure.

Stopping harm takes precedence over completing tasks. Tasks are not sacred. Goals do not override interruption. When harm indicators appear, continuation is the exceptional action that must justify itself structurally. Absent such structure, action stops.

Harm prevention does not rely on prediction alone. Forecasting is limited and often wrong. Systems therefore include multiple points where harm can be observed directly and acted upon without interpretation.

Indicators of harm are simple and coarse. Fine-grained measurement is not required to stop. When thresholds are crossed, action is reduced or terminated without analysis. Precision is not a virtue in stopping.

The cost of stopping is intentionally low. Interruption does not trigger blame, penalty, or loss of standing. No actor is required to demonstrate correctness or authority to stop harm. The mechanism does not ask why. It responds to conditions.

Stopping harm does not require attribution. Identifying fault is separate from interruption. Investigation, if any, occurs after exposure is reduced. Stopping is not delayed for diagnosis.

## Compounding, Reversibility, and Locality

Harm pathways are assumed to compound. Small harms accumulate and interact. Systems are therefore biased toward early interruption rather than proportional response. The aim is to prevent accumulation, not to calibrate damage.

Reversibility is prioritized over remediation. Repair after harm is uncertain and often incomplete. Systems prefer designs where harmful effects can be undone or dissipated quickly rather than compensated later.

Harm prevention is local before global. Exposure is reduced at the smallest affected boundary first. Broad interventions are not assumed to be safer. Local stops reduce collateral effects.

Emergency framing is avoided. Declaring emergency compresses time and removes friction, increasing risk of secondary harm. Ordinary stopping mechanisms remain active regardless of urgency. No emergency disables them.

Harm to non-participants is weighted heavily. When effects extend beyond those who consented, opted in, or benefited, thresholds for stopping are lower. Consent does not authorize external damage.

Environmental harm is treated as long-duration harm. Delayed effects are not discounted. When recovery timelines exceed human planning horizons, prevention replaces mitigation.

Information about harm is not filtered through performance goals. Reporting mechanisms do not route through success metrics, leadership approval, or reputational concerns. Suppression of harm signals is itself treated as harm.

The system does not trade harms. Choosing one group to absorb damage to benefit another is not optimization. Such trade-offs are treated as escalation and trigger reduction.

Stopping harm does not resolve the underlying problem. It creates space. Solutions, if attempted, occur under reduced exposure, not ongoing damage.

When stopping harm conflicts with continuity, continuity yields. Preservation of systems is not a protected value.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** simple harm indicators trigger interruption without attribution; stopping is costless and non-adversarial; exposure is reduced locally and early; emergencies do not disable stops; non-participant and environmental harms lower thresholds; and continuity never overrides interruption.

## Activation and Independence

Harm prevention mechanisms are placed as close as possible to points of effect. Distance increases delay and distortion. Those nearest to impact are not asked to judge intent or proportionality. They are only asked to recognize conditions and activate stops.

Activation authority is broad. Any role positioned to observe harm indicators is able to trigger interruption. Authority to stop is not scarce. Scarcity increases hesitation and delay.

Stops are physical or procedural facts, not permissions. They do not require approval. They do not escalate upward. They occur automatically when conditions are met.

Multiple independent stopping paths exist. No single mechanism is relied upon. If one fails, others remain available. Redundancy favors interruption over continuity.

Harm indicators are decoupled from performance signals. Success metrics do not suppress or outweigh stop triggers. Systems that reward throughput, growth, or completion do not control stopping thresholds.

Stopping harm does not assume reversibility. Even when effects are permanent, interruption still reduces further damage. Irreversibility lowers tolerance for continuation rather than raising it.

The system avoids moral language when stopping harm. Words like blame, fault, or responsibility are not required to act. Moral evaluation is not a prerequisite for safety.

Affected parties are not required to advocate for themselves. Burden of articulation is not placed on those experiencing harm. Silence or incapacity does not prevent stopping.

Harm to systems that enable harm is not privileged. When interruption damages infrastructure, process, or investment, this is treated as acceptable loss. Preservation of enablers does not outweigh exposure reduction.

Gradual degradation is treated as harm. Slow erosion of capacity, trust, or environment triggers the same mechanisms as sudden damage. Time does not normalize injury.

Uncertainty about scope does not delay stopping. When the boundaries of harm are unclear, the response is to shrink action until clarity improves. Expansion never resolves uncertainty.

Compensation does not substitute for stopping. Paying for damage while allowing continuation is treated as escalation. Financial settlement does not reduce exposure.

Harm signals are not triaged for convenience. Volume of reports does not dilute response. Repetition is treated as confirmation, not noise.

Stopping harm does not require public justification. Transparency is structural, not performative. Stops occur quietly and without spectacle.

Learning occurs after stopping, not during exposure. Feedback loops inform future design but do not authorize continuation in the present.

If harm prevention mechanisms are bypassed, disabled, or ignored, this is treated as a critical failure. The response is termination of the structure rather than repair of the mechanism.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** proximity-based activation allows early interruption; authority to stop is broad and non-scarce; stops are automatic and redundant; performance metrics cannot suppress them; uncertainty shrinks action; compensation does not substitute for interruption; and bypassing harm mechanisms triggers termination.

# Fixing Mistakes

## Foundations

Mistakes are treated as expected outcomes of action under uncertainty. They are not treated as violations of trust, competence, or intent. Because mistakes are ordinary, systems are designed to absorb, expose, and correct them without escalation.

Fixing a mistake begins with reducing its effects. Explanation does not precede reduction. Understanding may follow, but it is not required to act.

Mistakes are defined as actions or omissions that produce outcomes outside intended or tolerated bounds. Intention is not a stabilizer. Alignment does not erase error.

Correction mechanisms are immediate and local. The first response occurs where the mistake manifests, not where authority resides. Delay increases coupling and cost.

The system does not require admission to correct. Denial does not block correction. Silence does not prevent adjustment. Correction is mechanical.

Fixing a mistake does not imply fault. There is no ritual of accountability required before change. Attribution, if it occurs, is separated in time and function from correction.

## Detection, Rollback, and Isolation

Reversibility is the primary repair strategy. When actions can be undone, undoing precedes analysis. When undoing is partial, reduction still occurs.

Mistake detection is biased toward sensitivity rather than precision. False positives are acceptable. False negatives compound harm.

Correction paths are simple and rehearsed. Complex recovery plans increase error under stress. Simple steps reduce cognitive load and reliance on expertise.

Fixes do not introduce new authority. Temporary corrective actions do not expand scope, duration, or reach. Correction does not become a justification for permanence.

Mistakes do not accumulate justification. Repeated error does not legitimize redesign through expansion. Persistence of error triggers contraction or replacement.

The system avoids corrective heroics. No individual is expected to save a failing structure. Reliance on exceptional effort is treated as design failure.

Rollback is preferred to patching. Layered fixes obscure original boundaries and reduce clarity. Returning to a prior state restores known limits.

When rollback is unavailable, isolation follows. Effects are contained to prevent spread. Isolation is not punishment. It is boundary restoration.

Learning mechanisms do not override stopping or rollback. Insight does not keep systems running. Reflection occurs under reduced exposure.

Mistakes at scale reduce allowable scale. Large errors trigger smaller future structures rather than more controls.

Documentation does not stabilize mistakes. Recording error does not permit continuation. Records inform redesign or removal, not endurance.

Fixing mistakes does not aim for optimization. The goal is return to tolerable bounds, not improvement beyond them.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** correction begins with effect reduction; admission and attribution are non-blocking; rollback and isolation are primary tools; false positives are acceptable; fixes do not expand authority; repeated errors cause contraction; and no system relies on heroic intervention.

## Identity, Time, and Distribution

Mistakes are not treated as opportunities for growth within the same structure. Growth increases exposure. When learning requires continued operation, the system chooses learning without continuation.

Correction does not preserve identity. When a structure fails, its name, leadership, or legacy does not carry forward by default. Replacement is distinct from repair.

Fixing mistakes is time-bounded. Correction windows are short and predefined. Open-ended repair periods create drift and normalize deviation. If correction does not occur within the window, termination follows.

Mistakes do not justify exception handling. Creating special rules to address failure increases complexity and weakens boundaries. The same correction paths apply regardless of context.

The system does not protect sunk costs. Past investment does not raise tolerance for error. Attachment to effort or expense is treated as bias.

Error reporting is frictionless. Reporting does not require formatting, approval, or proof. Barriers to reporting are treated as error amplifiers.

Retaliation for reporting is itself a critical mistake. When it occurs, the response is structural reduction rather than disciplinary escalation.

Fixes are observable in effect, not narrative. Change is verified by reduction in exposure or restoration of bounds, not by statements of intent.

When multiple mistakes interact, correction prioritizes decoupling. Separating systems reduces cascading failure. Integration is not attempted during repair.

Mistakes that cannot be corrected within existing limits are not forced to fit. The structure ends rather than stretching constraints to accommodate failure.

Fixing mistakes does not aim to restore trust. Trust is not required for operation. Safety is structural.

After correction or termination, absence is allowed. The system does not rush to replace what failed. Time without intervention is treated as valid.

Fixing mistakes is not centralized. Distributed correction reduces single points of failure. Central repair authorities are avoided.

The system does not promise immunity from future mistakes. It promises only that mistakes will be smaller, shorter, and easier to stop.

The document ends without resolution. Systems remain provisional. Power remains small. Harm remains stoppable. Mistakes remain correctable.

**How this is stopped if it goes wrong:** correction windows are short and bounded; identity and sunk costs do not preserve structures; reporting is frictionless and protected; fixes are validated by effect; uncorrectable errors trigger termination; and absence is tolerated without replacement.
