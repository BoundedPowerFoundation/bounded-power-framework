# SCBP-02 — Foundational Theory

*Explanatory Layer*
*Subordinate to: the Structural Constitution*

---

## Why Institutions Exist

Large groups of people require systems to function. Resources have to be coordinated, infrastructure has to be maintained, disputes have to be resolved, and safety has to be preserved. These things do not organize themselves at scale. Institutions exist because they make this coordination possible.

At the same time, institutions carry a consistent risk that does not depend on intent.

**Authority tends to expand.**

---

## How Expansion Happens

It rarely expands all at once. More often, it moves in small steps. Definitions shift slightly. Temporary powers remain in place. Administrative shortcuts become standard practice. New capabilities extend reach without changing formal boundaries. Oversight weakens at the edges. Each change appears reasonable when it happens. Over time, those changes accumulate.

What begins as something limited can become something broad, persistent, and difficult to change.

At a certain point, the issue is no longer whether authority is being used correctly. The issue is whether it can still be changed at all.

---

## Safety Means Controllability

A system is only as safe as it is controllable.

Control does not mean influence or participation. It means something more direct. A system is controllable if it can be stopped, examined, corrected, and replaced without creating wider instability. If any of those conditions are missing, the system begins to move outside practical control.

Once that happens, authority is no longer meaningfully accountable, even if it continues to operate within formal rules.

---

## Authority vs. Structure

It helps to separate two things that are often treated as the same.

**Authority** is the power to decide.

**Structure** is what carries out those decisions.

A system can have many moving parts — people, processes, contractors, technologies — but those parts are not the source of authority. They execute what authority directs.

Responsibility does not move just because work is distributed.

If a system causes harm, the authority that directs it remains responsible, regardless of how many layers sit between decision and execution.

### The Kitchen Example

A kitchen prepares food. Staff cook, clean, and manage daily operations. But the authority to stop unsafe conditions does not belong to the kitchen. It belongs to the inspector. The inspector does not perform the work. The inspector has the ability to halt it.

That distinction matters. Work can be distributed. Authority cannot.

---

## Drift

Over time, systems change without appearing to. This is drift.

Drift does not usually come from deliberate expansion. It comes from small adjustments that are not reversed. Scope extends slightly. Timeframes lengthen. Dependencies form. New functions are added without removing old ones.

Because each step is small, the overall change is hard to see while it is happening. Eventually, the system is no longer what it was originally authorized to be.

---

## Fragmentation

Some systems attempt to avoid limits by dividing themselves into smaller parts. Authority is split across agencies, departments, contractors, or networks. Each part appears limited on its own. Taken together, they operate as a single system.

If those parts coordinate, their combined effect is what matters. Structural limits apply to the system as a whole, not just its individual pieces.

---

## Information as Authority

In modern systems, authority is often exercised through information. Data systems store, sort, and act on information about people and organizations. When those systems retain information indefinitely, they begin to create a different kind of authority — one that persists through records rather than direct action.

If information cannot be removed, individuals can become permanently defined by it. That is a form of authority that does not expire on its own.

---

## The Approach

The approach taken here treats institutional safety as a structural problem. It does not try to predict every possible misuse of authority. That is not practical.

Instead, it focuses on maintaining the conditions that allow systems to be corrected when something goes wrong. Those conditions are simple to describe, but difficult to preserve:

- Authority stays within defined limits
- It can be interrupted
- It can be examined
- It can be reversed or replaced
- It does not continue automatically

When those conditions hold, systems remain within reach. When they do not, systems accumulate beyond the point where change is practical.

---

## How the Framework Works

This framework is built around three parts that work together.

**The Structural Constitution** defines the limits that authority cannot cross.

**The Testing Protocol** checks whether those limits are being maintained.

**The Enforcement System** defines what happens when they are not.

Together, they are designed to keep authority within conditions where it can still be changed.

---

## Working Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Authority | The power to compel outcomes |
| Structure | The system that carries out decisions |
| Control | The ability to stop, change, or remove a system |
| Drift | Gradual expansion beyond original limits |
| Reversibility | The ability to safely undo or replace |
| Scope | The defined boundary of authority |
| Dependency | When other systems rely on a system to function |
