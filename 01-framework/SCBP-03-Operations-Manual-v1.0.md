# BOUNDED POWER — OPERATIONS MANUAL

## A Practical Guide to Authorizing and Maintaining Authority Within SCBP Rules

### SCBP-03 — Operational Guidance Layer

**Status:** Operational Guidance — Non-Binding
**Subordinate to:** the Structural Constitution
**Purpose:** Help real people do this correctly

---

> This manual introduces no new authority and creates no new rules. Everything here is a practical translation of constraints already established in the Structural Constitution. When in doubt, the Structural Constitution governs.

---

## Before You Start: What This Manual Is For

This manual is for anyone who needs to:

* **Create** a new authority (an agency, program, office, regulatory body, or any system that exercises power over others)
* **Operate** an existing authority while staying within constitutional limits
* **Review** an authority to determine whether it should continue
* **Stop** an authority that has breached its limits or completed its purpose
* **Audit** a system to determine whether it is structurally sound

You do not need a legal background to use this manual. You need to be willing to answer hard questions honestly.

The manual is organized around the lifecycle of an authority: **Authorize → Operate → Monitor → Review → Renew or Stop → Retire**. Start at the stage that applies to your situation.

---

## A Note on Language

Throughout this manual:

* **Authority** means any system that can compel outcomes, restrict behavior, allocate resources, or bind future action — whether formal or informal, governmental or private.
* **Mechanism** means the specific institution, program, agency, office, or system exercising that authority.
* **Operator** means you — whoever is responsible for the mechanism at this moment.
* **Stop authority** means any person or body with the power to halt the mechanism, independent of the mechanism itself.
* **Renewal** means the affirmative decision to allow a mechanism to continue beyond its authorized term.

## STAGE 1 — AUTHORIZATION

### How to Create a New Authority Correctly

Authorization is the moment you design the container. Get this right and everything downstream becomes manageable. Get it wrong and you are building a system that will be difficult to stop and easy to abuse.

---

### Step 1.1 — Define What This Authority Actually Does

Write one paragraph describing the mechanism by **functional effect**, not by its intended name or category.

Ask yourself: what can this mechanism *actually do* to people or institutions? What can it compel, restrict, take, require, or prevent?

**Good example:**

> This mechanism monitors air quality at industrial sites, collects data from sensor networks, issues violation notices to operators who exceed defined thresholds, and may order temporary suspension of operations pending remediation review.

**Bad example:**

> This is an environmental protection agency.

The bad example describes a category. The good example describes a function. You need the function, because authority is defined by what it can do — not what it is called.

*Why this matters:* If you cannot describe what the mechanism does in plain functional terms, you cannot define its limits. Undefined limits are no limits.

---

### Step 1.2 — Define the Boundaries

For each functional power you identified in Step 1.1, define the boundary explicitly. A boundary answers: where does this power stop?

Work through each dimension:

**Scope** — What domains, subjects, or populations does this authority cover? List them. Then explicitly list what is excluded. If a domain is not listed as included, it is excluded by default.

**Geographic reach** — What territory or jurisdiction does this authority apply to? If it is not defined, it is subject to expansion by interpretation later.

**Duration** — How long is this authorization valid? Pick a specific date or interval. If you are not sure, pick a shorter period — you can always renew. You cannot easily un-expand authority that has been accumulating for years.

**Coercive intensity** — What is the strongest action this mechanism can take? Define the ceiling. Can it fine? Suspend? Revoke? Detain? Each level of coercive intensity must be explicitly authorized. Anything not listed is not permitted.

**Data collection** — What information may the mechanism collect, from whom, and for how long? Data systems accumulate their own authority. Define limits now.

**Resource ceiling** — What is the maximum budget, staffing level, or resource allocation? Uncapped resource access enables scope expansion without explicit decisions.

---

### Step 1.3 — Map the Harm Categories

Before this mechanism operates, identify every type of harm it could cause — both through action and through failure to act.

Use these categories as a checklist:

* **Bodily harm** — Could this mechanism cause physical harm to individuals? Under what conditions?
* **Agency impairment** — Could this mechanism restrict someone's ability to communicate, participate in governance, or access correction processes?
* **Ecological harm** — Could this mechanism authorize or enable environmental damage?
* **Generational binding** — Could this mechanism commit future generations to obligations they cannot revise?
* **Informational harm** — Could this mechanism suppress, distort, or monopolize information people need to protect themselves?
* **Non-participant harm** — Who could be harmed by this mechanism who never agreed to interact with it?

For each harm category that applies, define a **measurable threshold** — the observable condition that signals the harm is occurring or about to occur. If you cannot define a measurable threshold, you cannot define a stop trigger. If you cannot define a stop trigger, the mechanism cannot be safely authorized.

*Write the threshold as a condition, not an intention:*

**Good threshold:** Recorded air particulate matter exceeds 150 µg/m³ averaged over a 24-hour period at any monitored site.

**Bad threshold:** When air quality becomes harmful to public health.

The bad threshold requires interpretation. The good threshold does not.

---

### Step 1.4 — Design the Stop Mechanism

Every mechanism must have at least three independent pathways to stop it. "Independent" means: if one pathway is blocked, the others still work.

For each stop pathway, define:

1. **Who can activate it** — This must include parties outside the mechanism itself. A mechanism that can only be stopped by its own operators is not stoppable.
2. **What triggers it** — Either a defined threshold breach (automatic) or a discretionary judgment by the stop authority.
3. **What "stopped" means** — Specifically: does operation pause? Does authorization lapse? Does data collection halt? Define it.
4. **What happens after stopping** — Who conducts the post-stop review? How long does it take? What must happen before operation can resume?

**Critical rule:** Stopping must impose no penalty on whoever activates the stop. If someone is penalized for stopping a mechanism — professionally, legally, financially, or socially — the stop mechanism is compromised. Document explicitly that no such penalty applies.

---

### Step 1.5 — Plan for Reversal

Before authorizing a mechanism, define how it would be dismantled if it needed to stop permanently.

Answer these questions:

* What would it take to shut this mechanism down completely within 90 days? Within one year?
* What dependencies would need to be unwound? Who else relies on this mechanism continuing?
* What data would need to be archived, transferred, or destroyed?
* What services would be disrupted and what backup exists?
* Who has the authority to order termination and execute it?

If you cannot answer these questions now, you are authorizing a mechanism that will be difficult to stop later. That is a structural problem, not an operational one. Redesign before authorizing.

---

### Step 1.6 — Set the Renewal Term and Schedule

Authorization expires on a specific date. There are no exceptions.

Guidelines for choosing a renewal interval:

* **High coercive intensity, broad scope, or novel mechanism:** 1–2 years
* **Moderate scope, established function, low harm potential:** 3–5 years
* **Narrow scope, well-understood function, strong track record:** 5–7 years

Never set a renewal interval longer than 10 years regardless of the mechanism.

Set the first renewal review to begin at least 6 months before expiration — not when expiration arrives. A review that begins at expiration cannot be completed without extending a potentially invalid authorization.

---

### Step 1.7 — Complete the Authorization Packet

Collect everything from Steps 1.1–1.6 into a single Authorization Packet. This packet is the mechanism's foundational document. It must be:

* Written in plain language that a non-specialist can read
* Publicly accessible to affected parties (with narrow exceptions for security-sensitive stop path details)
* Stored in a location that is not controlled by the mechanism itself

The Authorization Packet must contain:

* \[ \] Functional description (Step 1.1)
* \[ \] Boundary definitions for all dimensions (Step 1.2)
* \[ \] Harm category mapping with measurable thresholds (Step 1.3)
* \[ \] Stop pathway design with at least three independent paths (Step 1.4)
* \[ \] Reversal and dismantling plan (Step 1.5)
* \[ \] Expiration date and renewal schedule (Step 1.6)
* \[ \] Signature of authorizing body and date

If any element is missing, authorization is incomplete. An incompletely authorized mechanism may not begin operation.

## STAGE 2 — OPERATION

### How to Run an Authority Within Its Limits

Once authorized and registered, the mechanism may operate. Operating correctly means staying inside the boundaries defined in your Authorization Packet and watching for signs that you are drifting.

---

### Step 2.1 — Know Your Boundaries at All Times

The operator responsible for a mechanism must be able to answer these questions without looking anything up:

1. What is the scope of this mechanism's authority?
2. When does the current authorization expire?
3. Who can stop this mechanism and how?
4. What are the harm thresholds and how close are we to them?
5. What would trigger an automatic pause?

If you cannot answer these questions, you need to review your Authorization Packet before proceeding further.

---

### Step 2.2 — Classify Expansion Before It Happens

The most common way mechanisms exceed their authority is through small decisions that each seem reasonable individually. Before taking any action that is new, larger, or different from previous operation, ask:

* Does this action fall within the scope defined in my Authorization Packet?
* Does this increase my geographic reach, coercive intensity, resource use, or data collection beyond authorized limits?
* Does this create a new dependency that could compromise my stop paths?
* If I do this repeatedly for five years, does it expand the effective authority of this mechanism?

If the answer to any of these is "yes" or "I'm not sure," stop. Get a scope determination from an independent reviewer before proceeding. Do not interpret your own authority.

*A mechanism may not resolve ambiguity about its own scope. Ambiguity defaults to the narrower interpretation and, if needed, provisional pause.*

---

### Step 2.3 — Log Interpretation Changes

Every time you make a decision about what your authority does or does not cover — even a small one — log it. The log must contain:

* Date
* The question or ambiguity that arose
* The interpretation applied
* The reasoning
* Who made the determination
* Whether the interpretation was reviewed by anyone independent of the mechanism

This log becomes part of your renewal evidence. A mechanism with no interpretation log has either never encountered ambiguity (unlikely) or has been resolving ambiguity informally without documentation (a drift risk).

---

### Step 2.4 — Dependency Hygiene

As the mechanism operates, it will develop dependencies — on vendors, data systems, funding sources, partner agencies, and infrastructure. Each dependency is a potential capture risk and a potential stop path vulnerability.

Every six months, review your dependency list and ask:

* Has any single dependency grown to the point where losing it would compromise our stop paths?
* Has any funder or vendor gained informal influence over our operational decisions?
* Are our data systems owned or operated by parties outside our control?
* If our largest vendor withdrew tomorrow, could we continue to operate within 30 days?

If the answer to any of these raises concern, document it and begin mitigation before the next renewal review.

---

## STAGE 3 — MONITORING

### How to Watch for Drift Before It Becomes a Problem

Drift is not dramatic. It accumulates through small decisions, informal expansions, gradual dependency growth, and the slow erosion of stop path independence. Monitoring is the discipline of catching drift early.

---

### Step 3.1 — Monthly: Threshold Proximity Check

Each month, check current operational data against every harm threshold defined in your Authorization Packet.

For each threshold, record:

* Current level
* Trend direction (stable, rising, falling)
* Distance from threshold (how much headroom remains)
* Any unusual pattern in the past 30 days

If any threshold shows a rising trend for three consecutive months, escalate to a formal review regardless of whether the threshold has been breached. Thresholds are floors, not targets. Consistently approaching a threshold is a signal that the mechanism is operating near its safety limit.

---

### Step 3.2 — Quarterly: Scope Differential Review

Every quarter, compare current operations to the functional description in your Authorization Packet.

Ask: does what we actually do today match what is described in the packet?

Write a brief scope differential note — even if the answer is "no change." Over time, these notes create a record of how the mechanism has evolved. If the differential grows, you will see it building before it becomes a compliance problem.

Signs of scope drift to watch for:

* Staff are regularly performing tasks not described in the original functional description
* The mechanism is being used to address problems it was not designed for
* Other agencies or parties are referring cases to this mechanism that fall outside its defined domain
* The mechanism's public communications describe its role differently than the Authorization Packet does

---

### Step 3.3 — Annually: Stop Path Integrity Test

Once a year, test your stop paths. Do not just verify that they exist on paper. Test whether they work.

For each stop pathway:

1. Confirm the stop authority is still structurally independent of this mechanism
2. Confirm the activation procedure is still operable (contact information current, access granted, no new procedural barriers)
3. Confirm no penalty — formal or informal — has accumulated around stop activation
4. Ask the stop authority directly: is there anything that would make it harder to stop this mechanism than it was when we started?

Document the results. If any stop path has degraded, restore it before the next operational cycle.

---

### Step 3.4 — Ongoing: Capture Risk Awareness

Capture — where external interests gain structural influence over a mechanism — usually happens gradually and is rarely obvious from inside. Watch for:

* A vendor, funder, or partner whose continued participation has become essential to operation
* Personnel movement between the mechanism and regulated parties
* Informal advisory relationships where outside parties influence operational decisions without accountability
* Funding arrangements where continuation is contingent on reaching certain outcomes

If you notice these patterns, document them and raise them in your next renewal review. Capture is not always preventable, but it must be visible.

---

## STAGE 4 — REVIEW

### How to Conduct an Honest Evaluation

A review is not a ritual. It is an honest evaluation of whether this mechanism should continue. The question is not "has anything gone catastrophically wrong?" The question is "does this mechanism still deserve to exist as currently structured?"

---

### Step 4.1 — Scope Integrity

Conduct a side-by-side comparison:

| Dimension | Originally Authorized | Currently Operating |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | | |
| Geographic reach | | |
| Coercive intensity | | |
| Data collection | | |
| Resource level | | |
| Duration | | |

For any dimension where "Currently Operating" exceeds "Originally Authorized," this is a finding. Document it, explain how it occurred, and determine whether it was authorized through a formal renewal or accumulated through drift.

**If drift is found:** The mechanism must contract to its authorized scope before renewal is considered. Renewal of an expanded mechanism is renewal of an unauthorized expansion.

---

### Step 4.2 — Harm Compliance

For each harm threshold defined in the Authorization Packet:

* Was the threshold breached during the review period? When and how often?
* When breaches occurred, what corrective action was taken?
* Was corrective action taken promptly or was there delay?
* Were any breaches concealed, minimized, or re-classified to avoid triggering stop procedures?
* Are non-participants (people who did not consent to interact with this mechanism) being harmed at rates that exceed the defined tolerance?

**Honest question to ask yourself:** If an independent journalist reviewed our threshold data and breach response records, would they conclude we took harm seriously?

---

### Step 4.3 — Stop Path Review

For each stop pathway:

* Is it still operationally functional?
* Is the stop authority still genuinely independent?
* Has the cost or friction of stopping increased since authorization?
* Has anyone been penalized — formally or informally — for activating a stop or requesting review?
* If we needed to stop tomorrow, could we do it within 24 hours?

If any stop path has become functionally compromised, document it as a structural finding. A mechanism with compromised stop paths is operating outside its constitutional limits regardless of whether thresholds have been breached.

---

### Step 4.4 — Reversibility Check

Could this mechanism be permanently shut down within its defined termination timeline?

Walk through the dismantling plan from your Authorization Packet. Is it still accurate? Has the mechanism become more entrenched since it was written? Are there now dependencies, technical systems, or contractual obligations that would make dismantling harder or slower?

If the answer is yes — if the mechanism is harder to dismantle today than it was when authorized — this is a structural finding. Entrenchment must be addressed before renewal is granted.

---

### Step 4.5 — Necessity Review

The most important question, and the one most commonly skipped:

**Is this mechanism still necessary?**

Not "is it doing useful things?" Not "would people miss it?" The question is: does the specific harm it was created to address still exist at the scale and character that justified this authority?

If the original problem has diminished, the scope of authority should diminish proportionally. Expiration of necessity is the most legitimate reason for a mechanism to contract or retire.

---

### Step 4.6 — Compile the Review Finding

The review produces one of four outcomes:

**RENEW** — No significant findings. Mechanism is operating within authorized limits, harm thresholds are compliant, stop paths are intact, reversibility is confirmed, necessity persists. Proceed to Stage 5.

**RENEW WITH CONDITIONS** — Findings exist but are remediable. Renewal is granted for a shorter term with specific conditions that must be met and verified before the next renewal. Document conditions explicitly.

**NARROW** — Findings indicate the mechanism has expanded beyond its authorization. Renewal is granted only for the reduced, compliant scope. Expanded activities must cease.

**DO NOT RENEW** — Findings indicate the mechanism cannot be brought into compliance, or necessity has expired, or structural harm exists that cannot be remediated within a reasonable term. Proceed to Stage 6.

---

## STAGE 5 — RENEWAL

### How to Renew Authority Correctly

Renewal is not a formality. It is a new authorization decision, made with current evidence.

---

### Step 5.1 — Assemble Renewal Evidence

Renewal requires documented evidence across five domains. Assemble one section for each:

**Scope Integrity Evidence**
The side-by-side comparison from Step 4.1, with explanation for any differentials found and documentation of how they were corrected.

**Harm Compliance Evidence**
Threshold monitoring data for the full review period, breach records and responses, and non-participant exposure analysis.

**Stop Path Evidence**
Results of the annual stop path integrity tests (Step 3.3), confirmation of current stop authority independence, and documentation that no penalty has attached to stop activation.

**Dependency and Capture Evidence**
Current dependency inventory, comparison to authorization-period dependency profile, and any capture risk findings with mitigation plans.

**Reversibility Evidence**
Updated dismantling plan, results of any termination rehearsals, and confirmation that the mechanism can be exited within the defined timeline.

---

### Step 5.2 — Independent Review

The renewal decision must be made by a body that is structurally independent of the mechanism being renewed. This means:

* The reviewing body does not receive funding from the mechanism
* The reviewing body does not depend on the mechanism for its own continued operation
* Personnel of the mechanism may not constitute a majority of the reviewing body
* The reviewing body has genuine authority to deny renewal, not just to recommend

If no such body exists, one must be created before renewal can proceed. A mechanism that reviews its own renewal is not subject to renewal — it is permanent.

---

### Step 5.3 — Renewal Decision

The independent reviewing body evaluates the evidence and makes a binary determination for each domain: PASS or FAIL.

Any FAIL in any domain results in non-renewal unless an extraordinary necessity finding is made under the constitutional standard. Procedural completion alone does not constitute a PASS. Evidence must be sufficient for an independent observer to reach the same conclusion.

If renewal is granted, set the new expiration date and document any conditions attached to the renewal.

---

## STAGE 6 — STOPPING AND RETIREMENT

### How to Stop an Authority Correctly

Stopping is not failure. It is the system working as designed.

---

### Step 6.1 — Types of Stop

**Provisional Stop** — triggered by threshold breach or constitutional concern. Operation pauses pending review. This is not termination. It is a pause while the situation is evaluated. The mechanism may resume if review clears it.

**Expiration Stop** — authority expires without renewal. Operation ceases automatically on the expiration date. There is no grace period. An expired mechanism that continues operating does so without authorization.

**Termination** — permanent cessation following a "Do Not Renew" determination or a finding that the mechanism cannot be brought into compliance.

---

### Step 6.2 — Executing a Provisional Stop

When a harm threshold is breached or a stop path is activated:

1. Cease the operations implicated by the breach immediately
2. Notify all dependent parties within 24 hours
3. Preserve all records — do not alter, archive, or delete any data pending review
4. Identify the independent reviewer who will conduct the post-stop assessment
5. Set a timeline for the post-stop review (maximum 30 days for initial determination)
6. Document the stop event in the public ledger

The post-stop review determines one of three outcomes: resume (threshold breach was addressed, operation may continue), resume with conditions (structural changes required before resuming), or refer to termination.

---

### Step 6.3 — Executing Retirement

When a mechanism expires or receives a "Do Not Renew" determination:

**Week 1–2:**

* Cease all operations that require authorization
* Notify all parties who interact with or depend on the mechanism
* Begin data archival process
* Transfer any ongoing obligations to successor mechanisms (if authorized)

**Week 3–8:**

* Unwind vendor and contractor relationships
* Transfer or securely delete personal data per retention policy
* Settle outstanding obligations
* Conduct final public ledger entry summarizing the mechanism's operational history

**Final documentation:**

* Authorization packet (archived)
* Full monitoring history
* Stop event log
* Renewal review records
* Retirement summary including: what the mechanism accomplished, where it encountered difficulty, and what structural lessons future mechanism designers should know

Retirement documentation is not optional. It is how the system learns.

## QUICK REFERENCE

### The Five Questions Every Operator Must Be Able to Answer

1. What is my mechanism authorized to do?
2. When does authorization expire?
3. How would this mechanism be stopped if it needed to be?
4. What are the current harm threshold levels and how close are we?
5. What would make it harder to stop this mechanism than it was when we started?

### Red Flags — Stop and Get Independent Review

* You are doing something that is not described in your Authorization Packet
* Ambiguity about scope is being resolved internally without documentation
* A vendor, funder, or partner has become essential to operation
* A stop path has become harder to activate than it was at authorization
* Someone faced informal consequences for raising concerns about this mechanism
* The renewal process feels like a formality rather than an honest evaluation
* This mechanism is now harder to dismantle than it was when authorized

### The Default Rules

When in doubt about scope: **narrower interpretation applies.**

When a threshold is approaching: **escalate before it is breached, not after.**

When a stop path is compromised: **restore it immediately, not at the next renewal.**

When renewal evidence is incomplete: **incomplete evidence defaults to non-renewal.**

When a mechanism is harder to stop than when authorized: **this is a structural finding, not an operational detail.**
